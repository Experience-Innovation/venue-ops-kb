# Fly System and Stage Rigging Operations

Permanent fly systems in performing arts centres, including counterweighted and motorized rigging arrays used to move scenery, lighting, and acoustic shells, governed by ESTA/ANSI standards (E1.4, E1.47, E1.64, E1.43).

## Overview

Performing arts centres operate permanent fly systems that are largely absent from stadiums and convention halls. These systems use counterweighted or motorized rigging arrays to move scenery, lighting battens, masking, and acoustic shells vertically above the stage. ESTA/ANSI rigging standards codify inspection schedules, working-load limits, and control-system requirements unique to entertainment rigging.

## Key Standards

- **ANSI E1.4** — Manual counterweight rigging systems
- **ANSI E1.47** — Inspection of entertainment rigging systems
- **ANSI E1.64** — Portable and semi-permanent stage structures
- **ANSI E1.43** — Stage-machinery control systems
- **ETCP Rigger-Theatre** — Certification for theatrical rigging competence

## System Types

- **Single-purchase counterweight** — Arbor weight equals load weight; fly rail at stage level
- **Double-purchase counterweight** — Mechanical advantage reduces arbor travel; fly rail at grid level
- **Motorized (automated)** — Computer-controlled hoists with programmable speed, position, and acceleration profiles
- **Manual hemp** — Rope-based systems in older venues; higher operational risk

## Distinction from Arena Rigging

While arena rigging (covered under Entertainment Rigging Systems) focuses on touring loads, chain motors, and temporary rigging points, fly-system operations in PACs involve permanent counterweight infrastructure, daily scenery moves during performances, and stage-management cue integration. The certification pathway (ETCP Rigger-Theatre vs. ETCP Rigger-Arena) reflects this operational distinction.
