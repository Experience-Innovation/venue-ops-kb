# Event Contracting and License Agreements

Event contracting in public assembly venues follows a license structure, not a lease structure. This distinction carries significant legal implications: the licensee is granted a non-exclusive, limited right to use designated spaces for a specified period, explicitly not creating a tenancy or leasehold interest. The venue retains the right to enter the space at any time [[Source-0317-LawDepot-Event-Venue-Contract]].

## Deal Structures

Three primary deal structures exist across entertainment venues, each reflecting a different risk-reward allocation [[Source-0313-IAVM-Arenas-Performance-Reporting-Handbook]]:

**Flat Rental (License Fee)** carries the lowest venue risk. The promoter assumes all financial risk while the venue charges a flat rental fee plus a "house nut" covering event-specific expenses (labour, equipment, production support). This structure is used for major touring acts, established promoters, and high-demand events [[Source-0313-IAVM-Arenas-Performance-Reporting-Handbook]].

**Co-Promotion (Co-Pro)** involves shared risk documented per deal points, with revenue split after expenses. Terms vary per deal. This structure suits new markets or situations where the venue seeks content it could not otherwise attract [[Source-0313-IAVM-Arenas-Performance-Reporting-Handbook]].

**In-House/Self-Promote** carries the highest venue risk but allows the venue to retain all revenue streams (tickets, F&B, merchandise, parking). This is suited to venues with strong programming capability and risk-tolerant operators [[Source-0313-IAVM-Arenas-Performance-Reporting-Handbook]].

For rental deals, arenas often charge a flat rental rate alongside a one-time house nut. Rebates from the venue to the promoter based on "drop count" (actual turnstile attendance) are increasingly common in major promoter relationships [[Source-0313-IAVM-Arenas-Performance-Reporting-Handbook]].

## Rider Requirements

Artist and production riders are legally binding addenda to the primary performance contract that detail operational requirements. They fall into two categories [[Source-0318-Artist-Rider-Guide]]:

**Technical Riders** specify stage dimensions, power requirements, PA and lighting specifications, backline equipment, load-in logistics, and production schedules [[Source-0318-Artist-Rider-Guide]].

**Hospitality Riders** detail catering specifications, dressing room requirements, hotel accommodations, ground transportation, and security protocols [[Source-0318-Artist-Rider-Guide]].

Standard lead time for rider delivery is 4-6 weeks before a performance, with longer timelines for large festivals and international tours. Venue operators must cross out inapplicable terms and annotate venue-specific constraints [[Source-0318-Artist-Rider-Guide]].

## Insurance and Indemnification

Standard event contract insurance requirements include general liability insurance (minimum $1M-$2M per occurrence, $2M aggregate, with $5M aggregate recommended for higher-risk events), Certificate of Insurance (COI) required 30-45 days prior with the venue named as additional insured, workers' compensation for production and exhibitor employees, and event cancellation insurance which has become increasingly common post-COVID [[Source-0319-WilmerHale-Force-Majeure-COVID]].

Indemnification clauses typically require each party to indemnify, defend, and hold harmless the other from claims arising out of their own negligence or breach. Mutual indemnification is now common in well-negotiated agreements [[Source-0317-LawDepot-Event-Venue-Contract]].

## Force Majeure: Post-COVID Evolution

Pre-pandemic force majeure clauses typically listed specific triggering events without explicitly naming pandemics or government-ordered shutdowns. COVID-19 litigation exposed the ambiguity in these provisions [[Source-0320-Fordham-Force-Majeure-COVID-Analysis]].

Post-2021 industry practice has evolved in several directions [[Source-0319-WilmerHale-Force-Majeure-COVID]] [[Source-0320-Fordham-Force-Majeure-COVID-Analysis]]:

Contracts now commonly include explicit pandemic language listing "epidemic," "pandemic," or "public health emergency" as triggering events. Government mandate clauses specifically address government-ordered prohibitions on public gatherings. Partial performance provisions address reduced-capacity scenarios with revenue adjustments and rights to reschedule versus cancel. Tiered liquidated damages schedules (e.g., 25% if cancelled 90+ days out, escalating to 100% under 30 days) have become standard. Duration limits set maximum periods (typically 90-180 days) during which force majeure excuses performance. Deposit and advance treatment is now addressed explicitly, as this was the most litigated issue in 2020-2022 [[Source-0319-WilmerHale-Force-Majeure-COVID]] [[Source-0320-Fordham-Force-Majeure-COVID-Analysis]].

## Standard License Agreement Clauses

Virtually all public assembly venue license agreements include clauses covering: venue and access description, event details, rental period, license (not lease) declaration, rental fee and payment schedule, catering/F&B exclusivity, capacity limits, permitted activities and restrictions, insurance requirements, indemnification, force majeure, cancellation and liquidated damages, ADA/accessibility compliance, and subordination [[Source-0317-LawDepot-Event-Venue-Contract]].
