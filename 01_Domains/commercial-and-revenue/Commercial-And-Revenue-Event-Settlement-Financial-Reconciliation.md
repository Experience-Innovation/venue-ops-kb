# Event Settlement and Financial Reconciliation

Financial settlement is one of the least visible but most operationally important venue disciplines because it determines whether estimated event economics become realized cash. The process spans pre-event deal structuring, event-day revenue and cost capture, post-event reconciliation, and archiving that feeds future pricing decisions ([[Source-0380-Henslow-Irving-Guarantee-vs-Splits|Henslow & Irving Guarantee vs. Splits]]; [[Source-0376-ABA-Managing-Performance-Venue-Rentals-2024|ABA Rental Study 2024]]).

## Deal Structures

### Guarantee vs. Percentage

In performing arts rental and presenting models, contracts commonly use "guarantee versus percentage" structures, where the artist or visiting company receives either a minimum guarantee or an agreed share of gross box office, whichever is greater. Box-office splits often fall in broad ranges such as 65% to 80% to the visiting company, with the venue retaining the remainder. Actual terms vary by demand, market strength, and which party carries marketing risk ([[Source-0380-Henslow-Irving-Guarantee-vs-Splits|Henslow & Irving Guarantee vs. Splits]]).

This logic extends beyond performing arts: concerts, one-off events, and co-promoted events all require settlement worksheets that define gross revenue, deductible expenses, contra items, taxes, fees, and payment timing ([[Source-0380-Henslow-Irving-Guarantee-vs-Splits|Henslow & Irving Guarantee vs. Splits]]).

### Stadium and Arena Tenant Splits

In stadium and arena settings, settlement complexity increases because of deeply negotiated revenue-sharing agreements with anchor tenants. Fixed annual rent plus ticket surcharges, tiered thresholds for sharing net revenue from rents, concessions, and parking, and contractual treatment of non-tenant events all shape the settlement outcome. A venue can host blockbuster events and still deliver far less net venue revenue than attendance alone would suggest ([[Source-0372-Tampa-Sports-Authority-FY2024-Audit|Tampa Sports Authority FY2024 Audit]]).

## Settlement Workflow

A practical venue settlement workflow includes five stages:

1. **Pre-event estimate and deal memo** covering rent or guarantee, expense assumptions, revenue shares, deposit requirements, insurance obligations, and settlement deadline ([[Source-0376-ABA-Managing-Performance-Venue-Rentals-2024|ABA Rental Study 2024]]).
2. **Event-day capture** of box office receipts, ticket scans, F&B revenue, parking, merchandise, labor charges, security costs, and contractor expenses ([[Source-0372-Tampa-Sports-Authority-FY2024-Audit|Tampa Sports Authority FY2024 Audit]]; [[Source-0376-ABA-Managing-Performance-Venue-Rentals-2024|ABA Rental Study 2024]]).
3. **Post-event reconciliation** of actuals against estimates, with backup documentation for each line item and a defined process for disputed costs ([[Source-0372-Tampa-Sports-Authority-FY2024-Audit|Tampa Sports Authority FY2024 Audit]]).
4. **Net settlement payment or refund** after deposits and advances are applied ([[Source-0376-ABA-Managing-Performance-Venue-Rentals-2024|ABA Rental Study 2024]]).
5. **Event P&L archiving** for use in pricing, forecasting, and client-credit evaluation for future deals ([[Source-0371-Ottawa-Convention-Centre-Business-Plan-2024-25|Ottawa Convention Centre Business Plan]]).

## Pre-Event Timelines

In performing arts rental benchmarks, contracts often require confirmation approximately two to six months in advance, with around four weeks needed to finalize date, estimate, and contract, and about eight weeks of lead time preferred to set up box office and marketing. Convention centre closeout follows different timelines but applies the same principles, since deposits, actual-versus-estimate reconciliation, and multi-year booking commitments all feed the venue's cash-flow discipline ([[Source-0376-ABA-Managing-Performance-Venue-Rentals-2024|ABA Rental Study 2024]]; [[Source-0371-Ottawa-Convention-Centre-Business-Plan-2024-25|Ottawa Convention Centre Business Plan]]).

## Deposits and Credit Management

Deposits and deferred revenue are core financial controls, especially in convention and rental-heavy venues. Advance cash collection is a live operating feature rather than a minor accounting line. Many venues require upfront deposits, certificates of insurance, and substantial prepayment before the event, particularly when the renter is new or financially uncertain. This is effectively venue-level credit underwriting ([[Source-0376-ABA-Managing-Performance-Venue-Rentals-2024|ABA Rental Study 2024]]; [[Source-0371-Ottawa-Convention-Centre-Business-Plan-2024-25|Ottawa Convention Centre Business Plan]]).
