# Venue Revenue Taxonomy

Public assembly venues do not share a single revenue model. The revenue mix varies fundamentally by venue type, ownership structure, and the contractual relationship between venue operator, anchor tenants, and event promoters. Understanding this taxonomy is essential because the distinction between venue-operator revenue and tenant/promoter revenue determines what a venue actually controls financially ([[Source-0372-Tampa-Sports-Authority-FY2024-Audit|Tampa Sports Authority FY2024 Audit]]).

## Revenue Streams by Venue Type

### Convention Centres

Convention centres typically have the clearest venue-controlled revenue stack because the operator usually controls the building, catering relationship, and event service ecosystem. The Ottawa Convention Centre's 2024-25 business plan documents a revenue composition of F&B at approximately 57.2% of gross revenue, facility rental at 26.4%, commissions at 11.0%, advertising at 4.0%, and other income at 1.5% ([[Source-0371-Ottawa-Convention-Centre-Business-Plan-2024-25|Ottawa Convention Centre Business Plan]]). This pattern -- rental plus F&B dominant, with thin operating margins -- is characteristic of the convention centre model.

### Arenas

Arena revenue is more fragmented because the venue owner, venue manager, anchor team, promoter, ticketing company, and food-service provider may each control different pieces of the commercial stack. Primary revenue drivers include ticket-related rent and fees, premium seating, sponsorship, and F&B. Secondary streams include parking, merchandise commissions, and private events. The critical structural constraint is that tenant or team control over premium assets and sponsorship economics can significantly limit what the venue operator retains ([[Source-0372-Tampa-Sports-Authority-FY2024-Audit|Tampa Sports Authority FY2024 Audit]]; [[Source-0374-Tampa-Sports-Authority-Budget-Book-2023-24|Tampa Budget Book]]).

### Stadiums

Stadium revenue shares the fragmented pattern of arenas but at greater scale and with deeper negotiation of revenue splits. Tenant rent, facility fees, ticket surcharges, concerts, parking, and concession splits are primary. Naming rights participation, club spaces, and non-sports events are secondary. The Tampa Sports Authority's FY2024 results show $28.23 million in operating revenue from charges for services, but contractual revenue splits with the anchor tenant mean actual venue-operator net revenue is substantially lower than gross event spending would suggest ([[Source-0372-Tampa-Sports-Authority-FY2024-Audit|Tampa Sports Authority FY2024 Audit]]).

### Performing Arts Centres

Performing arts centres derive revenue from ticketing, facility rentals, contributed income (donations, grants), subscriptions, and single-ticket sales ([[Source-0375-StatsCan-Performing-Arts-Industry-2024|Statistics Canada Performing Arts 2024]]). Secondary streams include F&B, ticketing service fees, marketing services, and filming rentals ([[Source-0376-ABA-Managing-Performance-Venue-Rentals-2024|ABA Rental Study 2024]]). The structural constraint is high mission pressure that often produces weaker earned-income margins. Statistics Canada data for 2024 shows the Canadian performing arts industry operating at a 12.6% margin overall but -2.4% for not-for-profit organizations ([[Source-0375-StatsCan-Performing-Arts-Industry-2024|Statistics Canada Performing Arts 2024]]).

### Fairgrounds and Mixed-Use Campuses

Fairgrounds operate a hybrid model blending annual signature events, year-round rentals, convention business, parking, sponsorship, and public support. This model reduces dependence on a single event category. Year-round programming is increasingly positioned as a higher net-revenue contributor relative to seasonal anchor events, making fairgrounds financially closer to a portfolio of businesses than to a single-use venue.

## Venue-Operator vs. Tenant Revenue

The most important analytical distinction in venue finance is between venue-operator revenue and tenant/promoter revenue. In stadium and arena settings, suites, naming rights, advertising, ticketing economics, and concessions may be partly or largely retained by the tenant team or allocated through detailed venue agreements. Gross event spending does not automatically translate into venue income ([[Source-0374-Tampa-Sports-Authority-Budget-Book-2023-24|Tampa Budget Book]]). This distinction is essential for benchmarking because publicly reported attendance and spending figures often overstate what the venue operator actually captures.
