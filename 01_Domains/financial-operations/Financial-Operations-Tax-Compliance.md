# Venue Tax Compliance

Venue tax compliance spans admissions taxes, non-resident entertainer withholding, GST/HST application, and property tax status — with significant variation across US state, Canadian provincial, and municipal jurisdictions that creates a complex multi-jurisdictional obligation for venue operators and touring productions [[Source-0412-IAVM-Arena-Performance-Handbook]].

## US Admissions and Amusement Taxes

State and local admissions taxes vary significantly. New York applies 4% state sales tax plus local tax but exempts live dramatic and musical arts performances. Maryland imposes local admissions tax up to 2% of gross receipts. Seattle charges 5% admission tax with exemptions for nonprofit arts organizations and venues under 1,000 seats. Venue operators must register in each jurisdiction where they collect admission charges [[Source-0412-IAVM-Arena-Performance-Handbook]].

## Non-Resident Entertainer Withholding

US federal law requires 30% withholding on payments to non-resident alien performers unless a tax treaty applies. Multiple US states impose additional non-resident entertainer taxes (Minnesota and Missouri at 2%, California at 7% above $1,500). In Canada, 15% withholding applies under Regulation 105 of the Income Tax Act, with waiver applications available when Canadian income falls below deductible expenses [[Source-0412-IAVM-Arena-Performance-Handbook]].

## Canadian GST/HST

GST (5%) applies federally; HST applies in participating provinces (Ontario 13%, Atlantic provinces 15%). Most event admissions are taxable with key exemptions for amateur sporting events, amateur artist performances, and qualifying charity fundraising events. Convention sponsors selling to non-resident attendees benefit from reduced GST/HST base calculations. Registration is mandatory when taxable supplies exceed $30,000 over four consecutive quarters ($50,000 for public service bodies) [[Source-0418-Halifax-CC-Annual-Report]].

## Property Tax Status

Publicly owned US venues are generally exempt from property taxes. Privately operated venues on public land face complex treatment depending on state law; PILOT (Payment in Lieu of Taxes) agreements provide voluntary payments to host municipalities. In Alberta, the COPTER exemption under the Municipal Government Act covers community organizations including fairs, exhibitions, arts centres, and sports/recreation centres [[Source-0418-Halifax-CC-Annual-Report]].

## Ownership Structure Tax Implications

The choice of ownership/operating structure has cascading tax effects across property tax, bond access, income tax, and GST/HST treatment. Public authorities access tax-exempt bonds but face FOIA and board approval requirements. Non-profit operators may qualify for 501(c)(3) status and partial GST/HST rebates. Private operators bear full tax obligations but have maximum commercial flexibility. P3 structures combine elements with terms specified in operating agreements [[Source-0412-IAVM-Arena-Performance-Handbook]].
