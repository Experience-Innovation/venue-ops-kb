# Venue Budgeting and Forecasting

Venue budgeting is a rolling operational discipline, not a once-a-year spreadsheet exercise. Live-event demand is volatile enough that a static annual budget quickly becomes stale, requiring structured in-year reforecasting tied to attendance assumptions, booking pace, and market conditions ([[Source-0371-Ottawa-Convention-Centre-Business-Plan-2024-25|Ottawa Convention Centre Business Plan]]).

## Budget Construction

### Annual Cycle

Venue operating budgets are typically constructed in fall for the following fiscal year, with board approval before the fiscal year begins. The budget establishes revenue targets by category, expense allocations by department, capital investment priorities, and operating surplus or deficit projections. For public venues, the budget also addresses debt service obligations, reserve fund contributions, and reporting requirements to government oversight bodies ([[Source-0372-Tampa-Sports-Authority-FY2024-Audit|Tampa Sports Authority FY2024 Audit]]).

### Review Cadence

Effective venue financial management operates at multiple review frequencies. The Ottawa Convention Centre's business plan documents executive leadership reviewing financial and performance goals weekly, with monthly internal financial review and quarterly reporting to the board and finance and audit committee ([[Source-0371-Ottawa-Convention-Centre-Business-Plan-2024-25|Ottawa Convention Centre Business Plan]]). This layered cadence ensures that operational, management, and governance levels each have appropriate visibility.

## Revenue Forecasting Methodology

The most durable forecasting framework combines several analytical lenses:

- **Historical trending** by event type, season, client segment, and promoter performance ([[Source-0372-Tampa-Sports-Authority-FY2024-Audit|Tampa Sports Authority FY2024 Audit]]; [[Source-0371-Ottawa-Convention-Centre-Business-Plan-2024-25|Ottawa Convention Centre Business Plan]]).
- **Event pipeline and booking pace**, especially critical for convention centres and rental-dependent venues. Booking pace is itself a KPI; the Ottawa Convention Centre set a 2024-25 booking pace target of $21 million, reflecting that forward-sold business is often more actionable than trailing realized revenue ([[Source-0371-Ottawa-Convention-Centre-Business-Plan-2024-25|Ottawa Convention Centre Business Plan]]).
- **Market factors** including inflation, labor cost pressure, tourism access, visa policies, and local market conditions ([[Source-0371-Ottawa-Convention-Centre-Business-Plan-2024-25|Ottawa Convention Centre Business Plan]]).
- **Contractual revenue mechanics** such as ticket surcharges, rent escalators, commission agreements, and revenue-share thresholds ([[Source-0374-Tampa-Sports-Authority-Budget-Book-2023-24|Tampa Budget Book]]; [[Source-0372-Tampa-Sports-Authority-FY2024-Audit|Tampa Sports Authority FY2024 Audit]]).
- **Capital and lifecycle timing**, because major repairs and renovations can reshape net operating results even in years with strong top-line demand.

## In-Year Reforecasting

Structured in-year forecast reviews are critical for venues because live-event demand is inherently volatile. Best practice involves scheduling at least two formal reforecast cycles during the fiscal year, explicitly linking updated projections to attendance performance, year-round business activity, debt service obligations, and any new capital requirements. This ensures management and governance bodies are working from current projections rather than stale annual targets.

## Expense Structure

Venue expense categories recur across all venue types, though proportions vary. The Ottawa Convention Centre's 2024-25 budget documents the following expense composition as a percentage of gross revenue: facilities at approximately 26.6%, utilities at 6.3%, sales and marketing at 10.9%, and general and administration at 10.4% ([[Source-0371-Ottawa-Convention-Centre-Business-Plan-2024-25|Ottawa Convention Centre Business Plan]]).

Capital planning and operating budgets cannot be separated in practice. Even venues with planned operating surpluses may show negative results after interest and amortization. For publicly owned venues, this distinction between operating viability and public-balance-sheet treatment is normal rather than exceptional ([[Source-0371-Ottawa-Convention-Centre-Business-Plan-2024-25|Ottawa Convention Centre Business Plan]]; [[Source-0372-Tampa-Sports-Authority-FY2024-Audit|Tampa Sports Authority FY2024 Audit]]).
