# AODA Integrated Accessibility Standards

Ontario's Accessibility for Ontarians with Disabilities Act (AODA) is implemented through the Integrated Accessibility Standards Regulation (O. Reg. 191/11), establishing requirements across customer service, information/communication, employment, transportation, and design of public spaces.

## Overview

The AODA establishes a framework for developing, implementing, and enforcing accessibility standards in Ontario, with the goal of achieving full accessibility by 2025. The Integrated Accessibility Standards Regulation (IASR) consolidates requirements across five standards areas that directly affect venue operations in the province.

## Five IASR Standards Areas

The Customer Service Standard requires organizations to provide accessible customer service, including training staff on interacting with people with various disabilities, allowing service animals and support persons, and providing notice of temporary service disruptions. The Information and Communication Standard mandates that organizations create, provide, and receive information and communications in accessible formats, including accessible websites (WCAG 2.0 Level AA), accessible documents, and accessible feedback processes.

The Employment Standard covers the full employment lifecycle from recruitment through return-to-work, requiring accessible hiring processes, individualized workplace emergency response information, and documented accommodation plans. The Transportation Standard applies to conventional and specialized transportation services relevant to venues that operate shuttle services or have transit connections.

## Design of Public Spaces Standard

The Design of Public Spaces Standard (DOPS) establishes accessibility requirements for newly constructed and redeveloped outdoor public spaces, including recreational trails, outdoor eating areas, outdoor play spaces, exterior paths of travel, accessible parking, and service counters. For venues, this standard has particular impact on exterior approaches, parking facilities, outdoor event areas, and plaza spaces.

## Compliance Requirements

Large organizations (50+ employees) face the most comprehensive compliance obligations, including filing accessibility compliance reports, developing multi-year accessibility plans, and making plans publicly available. Staff training requirements mandate that all employees and volunteers receive training on the IASR and the Ontario Human Rights Code as it relates to persons with disabilities. Training records must be maintained.

## Multi-Year Accessibility Plans

Organizations must develop, maintain, and post multi-year accessibility plans outlining their strategy for preventing and removing barriers. Plans must be reviewed and updated at least once every five years and must be posted on the organization's website if one exists.

## Relationship to Ontario Human Rights Code

The AODA works alongside the Ontario Human Rights Code, which provides the duty to accommodate to the point of undue hardship. The Human Rights Code takes precedence where there is a conflict, meaning AODA compliance does not necessarily satisfy all accommodation obligations. Venues must meet both AODA standards and Human Rights Code requirements.
