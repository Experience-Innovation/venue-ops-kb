# Accessible Ticketing and Seating

DOJ 2010 regulations (28 C.F.R. 36.302(f)) require venues to sell accessible seating through the same channels and phases as other seats, at all price levels, with up to three companion seats per wheelchair space, dispersed across viewing angles and price categories.

## Overview

The ADA's accessible seating and ticketing requirements ensure that individuals with disabilities have equal access to venue events. ADA Sections 221 and 802 of the 2010 Standards for Accessible Design establish minimum numbers of wheelchair spaces and companion seats based on total seating capacity, along with technical specifications for sightlines, floor surfaces, and integration with the overall seating bowl.

## Seating Requirements

Wheelchair spaces must be dispersed throughout the venue, providing a choice of viewing angles and price levels comparable to those available to other patrons. The number of required wheelchair spaces scales with total venue capacity, from a minimum of one space for venues with 25 or fewer seats to six spaces plus one for each additional 150 seats beyond 501. Companion seats must be provided adjacent to each wheelchair space, with venues required to offer up to three companion seats per wheelchair space when requested.

## Sightline Requirements

Wheelchair seating locations must provide lines of sight over standing spectators for events where spectators are expected to stand. This requirement has significant design implications for arena and stadium seating bowls, often requiring elevated platforms or integration into raked seating sections to ensure unobstructed views.

## Same-Channel Ticketing Mandate

Venues must sell tickets for accessible seating through the same methods, during the same hours, and during the same stages of sales (including pre-sales, general on-sale, and day-of-event) as all other tickets. Accessible seats cannot be held back from general sales or restricted to a separate purchasing process. Online ticketing platforms must identify and offer accessible seating options through the same interface used for all other seat selection.

## Hold-and-Release Policies

Unsold accessible seats may be released for sale to the general public under specific conditions, but venues must keep a sufficient supply of accessible seating available at all times up to the event. When accessible seats are released and sold to non-disabled patrons, the venue is not required to displace those patrons if a wheelchair user subsequently requests accessible seating, provided no other accessible seats remain available.

## Online Ticketing Accessibility

Ticketing websites and mobile applications must be accessible to people with disabilities, enabling independent selection and purchase of accessible seats. This includes compatibility with screen readers, keyboard navigation, and clear identification of accessible seating locations and their features within the seat selection interface.
