# Gender-Neutral and Inclusive Restrooms

Implementation of gender-neutral and all-gender restroom facilities in venues, driven by municipal building code amendments, human rights legislation, and guest inclusivity goals, with considerations for privacy, signage, and retrofit approaches.

## Overview

Gender-neutral and all-gender restroom facilities have become an increasingly important element of venue inclusivity programming. The shift is driven by a combination of municipal building code amendments, human rights legislation recognizing gender identity protections, and evolving guest expectations around inclusive facility design.

## Building Code Context

The two primary US model plumbing codes now contain explicit all-gender facility provisions. The International Plumbing Code (IPC), adopted in 37 US states serving approximately 76% of the US population, introduced multi-user all-gender facility provisions in the 2021 edition — Section 403.1.1 Exception 2 requires fixture counts calculated at 100% of total occupant load (rather than the default 50/50 male/female split) when a facility is designed for all genders ([[Source-0261-ICC-IPC-All-Gender-Restroom-Provisions]]). The 2024 IPC added signage requirements (§403.4) and lockable egress door provisions for all-gender facilities.

The Uniform Plumbing Code (UPC), adopted in approximately 12-13 US states, introduced explicit all-gender provisions in the 2024 edition, including Section 420.3 requiring full-height privacy compartments (floor to ceiling, no gaps) — a materially stricter privacy standard than the IPC ([[Source-0262-IAPMO-UPC-All-Gender-Provisions]]). IAPMO Manual 1 provides supplementary design guidance for code officials and designers implementing all-gender toilet facilities in assembly occupancies ([[Source-0263-IAPMO-Manual-1-Toilet-Room-Design]]).

## Human Rights Legislation Drivers

In Canada, Bill C-16 (Royal Assent June 19, 2017) amended the Canadian Human Rights Act and Criminal Code to add gender identity and gender expression as prohibited grounds of discrimination at the federal level ([[Source-0264-Bill-C16-Canadian-Human-Rights-Gender-Identity]]). SOR/2024-118 (effective December 1, 2024) further mandated that federally regulated workplaces must permit all-gender toilet facilities with specified design standards ([[Source-0265-Canada-SOR-2024-118-All-Gender-Toilets-Federal]]). Provincial human rights commissions have issued complementary guidance — the Ontario Human Rights Commission's policy on gender identity and expression establishes obligations for public service providers and facility operators regarding restroom access ([[Source-0266-OHRC-Gender-Identity-Expression-Policy]]). At the municipal level, the City of Edmonton's Gender-Inclusive Washroom Guide provides implementation standards for public buildings ([[Source-0267-Edmonton-Gender-Inclusive-Washroom-Guide]]).

In the United States, gender identity protections vary by jurisdiction. A January 2025 executive order reversed federal-level protections, while state and municipal laws continue to provide variable coverage. Accessibility requirements under the 2010 ADA Standards for Accessible Design remain applicable to all restroom facilities regardless of gender designation ([[Source-0168-2010-ADA-Standards]]).

## Design Approaches

Single-occupancy conversions represent the simplest retrofit approach, converting existing single-stall restrooms to all-gender use through signage changes. Multi-stall all-gender designs require enhanced privacy features including full-height partitions (floor to ceiling), individual locking stalls with no gaps, and shared handwashing areas. New construction provides the greatest design flexibility, allowing purpose-built all-gender facilities with individual enclosed rooms opening onto a shared common area.

## Signage Approaches

Signage design for all-gender restrooms has moved toward pictogram-based and function-based approaches that describe the facilities available (toilet, urinal, baby-changing station, accessible features) rather than designating user gender. This approach provides clear wayfinding information while maintaining inclusivity.

## Retrofit Considerations

Existing venues face specific challenges in implementing gender-neutral facilities, including plumbing fixture count requirements under building codes, structural limitations on partition upgrades, ventilation requirements for enclosed stalls, and balancing the conversion of existing gendered restrooms against total facility capacity. BMO Field in Toronto provides an example of a venue that has implemented all-gender restroom facilities as part of broader inclusivity initiatives.
