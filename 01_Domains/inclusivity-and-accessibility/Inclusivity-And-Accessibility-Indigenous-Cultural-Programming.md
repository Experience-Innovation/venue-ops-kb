# Indigenous Land Acknowledgment and Cultural Programming

Indigenous land acknowledgment practices and cultural programming in Canadian venues, including territorial acknowledgment protocols, Indigenous artist showcases, community consultation processes, and integration of Indigenous perspectives into venue programming.

## Overview

Indigenous land acknowledgment and cultural programming have become integral components of venue operations across Canada, reflecting both the recommendations of the Truth and Reconciliation Commission (TRC) and evolving expectations around institutional responsibility toward Indigenous communities. For venues — particularly those on Treaty lands or traditional territories — these practices represent a substantive commitment that extends beyond symbolic gestures.

## Territorial Acknowledgment Protocols

Effective territorial acknowledgments are venue-specific, referencing the particular Indigenous nations and treaty territories on which the facility is located. Generic or template-based acknowledgments are increasingly recognized as insufficient. Best practice involves consultation with local Indigenous communities to develop acknowledgment language that accurately reflects the specific territorial history and relationship. Acknowledgments are typically delivered at the opening of events, displayed on venue signage, and included in printed and digital materials.

## Truth and Reconciliation Commission Calls to Action

The TRC's 94 Calls to Action, released June 2, 2015 and maintained by the National Centre for Truth and Reconciliation (NCTR), include specific recommendations relevant to venue operations ([[Source-0268-NCTR-TRC-94-Calls-to-Action]]). Calls to Action 62-65 address education for reconciliation, requiring age-appropriate curricula on residential schools and Indigenous contributions — directly relevant to venue operators offering public programming and interpretive content. Calls to Action 67-70 address museums and archives, with implications for venues incorporating Indigenous cultural exhibitions.

Call to Action #87 specifically addresses sports and reconciliation, calling on all levels of government to develop "a Canadian strategy for sports reconciliation" in collaboration with Aboriginal peoples ([[Source-0271-NCTR-ReconciliACTION-Plan-CTA-87-91]]). This call has direct operational implications for arenas, stadiums, and convention centres hosting sporting events and public gatherings.

Ten years after their release, the Calls to Action remain substantially incomplete. The Assembly of First Nations reported in September 2025 that no new Calls to Action were completed in the 2024-2025 year, with approximately 13-15 completed in total ([[Source-0270-AFN-TRC-Progress-Report-2025]]) — significantly below the Government of Canada's characterization that 85% are "completed or well underway" ([[Source-0269-RCAANC-TRC-Implementation-Tracker]]). This contested implementation landscape is the essential backdrop for venue operators' reconciliation commitments.

## Indigenous Artist Programming

Venues are increasingly incorporating Indigenous artists, performers, and cultural practitioners into their programming schedules. This includes dedicated Indigenous arts showcases, integration of Indigenous performances into regular programming, and commissioning of Indigenous public art for venue spaces. Effective programming involves meaningful partnerships with Indigenous communities rather than tokenistic inclusion.

## Community Consultation Processes

Establishing and maintaining relationships with local Indigenous communities, nations, and organizations is foundational to authentic Indigenous cultural programming. Consultation processes typically involve identifying the appropriate nations and governing bodies for the venue's territory, establishing ongoing dialogue and formal partnership agreements, seeking guidance on appropriate protocols for events and ceremonies, and ensuring fair compensation for Indigenous knowledge holders and cultural practitioners.

## Multilingual Services and Broader Inclusivity

Indigenous cultural programming connects to broader inclusivity efforts including multilingual services that recognize Indigenous languages alongside official languages, and the provision of faith and prayer spaces that accommodate diverse spiritual practices including Indigenous ceremony.

## Venue Implementation Examples

BC Pavilion Corporation (PavCo), operator of BC Place Stadium and Vancouver Convention Centre, has integrated reconciliation commitments into its accessibility planning, including territorial acknowledgment practices and Indigenous cultural programming across venue operations ([[Source-0272-PavCo-Accessibility-Plan-BC-Place-VCC]]).
