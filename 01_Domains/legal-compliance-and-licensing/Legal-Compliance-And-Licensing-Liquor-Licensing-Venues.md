# Liquor Licensing for Venue Operations

The complex landscape of liquor licensing for venues, including multi-point licenses for large facilities, catering endorsements, special event permits, and the jurisdictional fragmentation that makes alcohol compliance one of the most complex regulatory areas in venue operations.

## Licensing Complexity

Liquor licensing for venues operates across a jurisdiction-fragmented landscape with no federal or national uniform standard in either the US or Canada. Every state and Canadian province maintains its own licensing authority, license categories, permit structures, and enforcement machinery.

### US State-Level License Categories

In California, the Department of Alcoholic Beverage Control issues over 75 license types. Venue-relevant licenses include Type 47 (On-Sale General Eating Place), Type 48 (On-Sale General Public Premises), Type 57 (Special On-Sale General for fairgrounds and exposition halls), and the Type 90 Music Venue License created by SB 793 (2022) for arenas and concert halls ([[Source-0273-California-ABC-License-Types]]). Each fixed bar in a separate room requires a duplicate permit, making licensing costs proportional to the number of active bars.

Texas operates under a unique legally mandated three-party structure for public entertainment facilities under Alcoholic Beverage Code Chapter 108 (§108.75): facility owner, independent concessionaire (holding the retail Mixed Beverage permit), and manufacturer/wholesaler — each with defined boundaries preventing cross-ownership or purchasing influence ([[Source-0274-Texas-Public-Entertainment-Facilities-Act]]).

Nevada administers liquor licensing at the county and city level rather than the state level. Clark County's per-bar fee model ($900 semi-annual per additional bar) makes licensing costs directly proportional to the number of active bars at a venue ([[Source-0278-Las-Vegas-Clark-County-Alcohol-Licenses]]).

### Canadian Provincial License Categories

In Alberta, the AGLC Class B license is the primary category for arenas, stadiums, and convention centres, with area expansion extensions enabling multi-point service from a single license ([[Source-0275-AGLC-Liquor-Licensee-Handbook]]). Ontario's AGCO administers the Liquor Sales Licence (LSL) structure with a Stadium Endorsement for tiered-seating venues, uniquely requiring a municipal resolution prerequisite with no US equivalent ([[Source-0276-AGCO-Liquor-Licence-Structure-Ontario]]). British Columbia's LCRB Liquor Primary (LP) license covers all licensed service areas in a venue under a single license, explicitly naming stadiums and convention centres as eligible facilities ([[Source-0277-BC-LCRB-Liquor-Policy-Manual]]).

## Multi-Point Licensing Challenges

A large stadium or convention centre may have 50+ individual points of alcohol sale (concession stands, bars, restaurants, suites, hospitality areas). Jurisdictions handle this differently:
- **Per-bar permit model** (California, Nevada) — separate duplicate permits or per-bar endorsement fees for each service point
- **Single-premises blanket model** (British Columbia LP, Alberta Class B with extensions) — one license covering all service areas within the venue footprint
- **Hybrid model** (Ontario LSL + Stadium Endorsement, Texas MB + facility structure) — a primary license with venue-specific endorsements or concessionaire structures

## Event-Specific Permitting

Convention centres and fairgrounds hosting events with alcohol service may require event-specific permits, particularly when the event operator (not the venue) is selling alcohol, service extends beyond the permanent license footprint, or the event involves tastings, sampling, or self-pour systems with different regulatory requirements.

## Compliance Management

Effective liquor license compliance in venues requires centralized license management, calendar systems for renewals and inspections, staff certification tracking (responsible beverage service), incident documentation, and regular self-audits against operating conditions.
