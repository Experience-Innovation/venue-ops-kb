# Naming Rights and Trademark Management

Venue naming rights agreements grant sponsors trademark license rights over venue branding, requiring trademark registration, written co-branding licenses with quality control provisions, and protection against unauthorized third-party use under the Lanham Act.

## Overview

Naming rights agreements are among the largest single-source revenue contracts in the venue industry, with major arena and stadium deals spanning 10-20+ years and valued in the hundreds of millions of dollars. These agreements are fundamentally trademark license arrangements — the sponsor's brand becomes synonymous with the venue, creating mutual brand equity that must be carefully governed to protect both parties' intellectual property.

## Multi-Year Consideration Structure

- Naming rights deals typically run 10-20 years, with total consideration often exceeding $100 million for major professional sports venues
- Payment structures include annual installments, escalation clauses tied to CPI or fixed percentages, and performance bonuses linked to attendance or media exposure metrics
- Early termination provisions typically address sponsor bankruptcy, reputational damage (morals clauses), and venue closure or relocation scenarios

## Trademark Registration and Protection

- Both the sponsor's marks and the combined venue name should be registered as trademarks with the USPTO (or CIPO in Canada) to secure federal protection
- Registration establishes constructive notice of ownership and the legal presumption of validity — critical for enforcement actions
- The Lanham Act (15 U.S.C. 1051 et seq.) provides the federal framework for trademark protection, including civil remedies against infringement, counterfeiting, and dilution
- Venue operators must actively monitor for unauthorized use of the venue name by ambush marketers, counterfeit merchandise producers, and unauthorized ticket resellers

## Co-Branding Governance

- Written co-branding licenses define the scope of permissible use for both the sponsor's marks and the venue's marks across all media: signage, digital, broadcast, merchandise, and wayfinding
- **Quality control provisions** are mandatory in trademark licenses — without them, a licensor risks "naked licensing," which can result in abandonment of trademark rights
- Style guides, approval workflows, and usage standards should be annexed to the naming rights agreement and updated periodically
- Third-party sublicensing (e.g., a venue tenant using the venue name in their own marketing) requires explicit authorization in the agreement

## Transition and Rebranding

- When naming rights agreements expire or are terminated, venue operators face significant rebranding costs: signage replacement, digital asset updates, wayfinding changes, and public communication campaigns
- Transition periods are typically negotiated into the agreement, allowing the outgoing sponsor's branding to be phased out over 60-180 days
- Legacy reference provisions may allow the venue to continue referencing the former name in historical or archival contexts
