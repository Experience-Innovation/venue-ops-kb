# Public Performance Licensing for Venues

Under 17 U.S.C. 106, venues publicly performing music must obtain blanket licenses from PROs (ASCAP, BMI, SESAC), with each covering only its represented catalog; streaming and recording require separate synchronization and mechanical licenses.

## Overview

The Copyright Act (17 U.S.C. 106) grants copyright holders the exclusive right to publicly perform their works. Any venue where music is played — whether live performances, DJ sets, background music, or pre-show playlists — must secure public performance licenses or face statutory damages of up to $150,000 per willful infringement. Performance rights organizations (PROs) administer blanket licenses on behalf of their member songwriters and publishers.

## Blanket License Model

- **ASCAP (American Society of Composers, Authors and Publishers):** Represents over 900,000 members; licenses are venue-specific and priced based on capacity, frequency of music use, and whether admission is charged
- **BMI (Broadcast Music, Inc.):** Represents over 1.3 million songwriters; blanket license structure parallels ASCAP with venue-category-based pricing
- **SESAC:** Operates as a selective, invitation-only PRO; represents a smaller but significant catalog including major country, Christian, and Latin music catalogs
- Each PRO covers only its own represented catalog — a venue must hold licenses from all three to ensure full coverage across the breadth of commercially available music

## Joint and Several Liability

- Under copyright law, venue operators bear joint and several liability for unauthorized public performances occurring on their premises
- This liability extends regardless of whether the venue directly selected the music — if a hired DJ, band, or tenant performs unlicensed music, the venue can be held liable
- Rental agreements and performer contracts should explicitly allocate licensing responsibility and include indemnification clauses

## Streaming and Recording Rights

- Public performance licenses do not cover streaming, broadcasting, or recording of live events
- **Synchronization licenses** are required when music is paired with visual content (e.g., video recordings of events, livestreams)
- **Mechanical licenses** are required for reproduction and distribution of musical works (e.g., event recordings sold or distributed)
- These additional licenses must be negotiated directly with publishers or through agencies such as the Harry Fox Agency

## Contract Allocation of Licensing Responsibilities

- Venue rental agreements should specify which party (venue, promoter, event organizer) is responsible for securing performance licenses
- Best practice is for the venue to maintain its own blanket licenses for background and ambient music, while requiring promoters and event organizers to secure licenses for headline performances and any additional rights (streaming, recording)
- Written documentation of licensing responsibility allocation reduces exposure to infringement claims
