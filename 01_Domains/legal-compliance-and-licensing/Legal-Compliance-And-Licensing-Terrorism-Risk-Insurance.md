# Terrorism Risk Insurance Act (TRIA)

Federal backstop program (2002, reauthorized through December 31, 2027) requiring commercial P&C insurers to offer terrorism coverage, with aggregate loss trigger of $200 million and $100 billion annual cap.

## Overview

The Terrorism Risk Insurance Act was enacted in 2002 following the September 11, 2001 attacks, which caused approximately $40 billion in insured losses and led commercial insurers to broadly exclude terrorism coverage. TRIA created a federal backstop that requires property and casualty insurers to make terrorism coverage available, with the federal government sharing losses above defined thresholds.

## Program Mechanics

- **Aggregate industry loss trigger:** Federal backstop activates only when certified terrorism losses exceed $200 million in aggregate across the industry
- **Annual program cap:** $100 billion — losses beyond this cap are not covered by the federal backstop
- **Insurer deductible:** Each insurer bears a deductible equal to 20% of its direct earned premiums for the prior calendar year before federal sharing begins
- **Federal share:** Above the insurer deductible, the federal government covers 80% of losses (insurer retains 20%)
- **Mandatory make-available:** Insurers must offer terrorism coverage on the same terms, amounts, and limitations as their underlying commercial policies

## Certified vs. Non-Certified Acts

- **Certified acts of terrorism** require formal certification by the Secretary of the Treasury, in concurrence with the Secretary of State and Attorney General
- Only certified acts trigger the federal backstop — non-certified acts (e.g., domestic lone-wolf attacks below the aggregate trigger) fall outside TRIA coverage
- The certification requirement creates a coverage gap for smaller-scale or domestic incidents that do not meet the $200 million aggregate threshold

## 2019 Reauthorization (Through 2027)

- The Terrorism Risk Insurance Program Reauthorization Act of 2019 extended the program through December 31, 2027
- Maintained the $200 million aggregate trigger and $100 billion cap
- Continued the federal recoupment mechanism requiring post-event surcharges on commercial policyholders to reimburse the federal government

## Stand-Alone Political Violence Products

- For venue operators seeking coverage beyond TRIA's scope, stand-alone political violence insurance products cover both certified and non-certified acts
- These products typically cover active assailant events, civil unrest, sabotage, and other political violence scenarios regardless of federal certification status
- Pricing varies by venue type, capacity, location, and security posture
