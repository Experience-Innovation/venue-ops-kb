# Digital Signage and Marquee Regulations

Municipal regulatory frameworks governing large venue digital signs and marquees, covering sign class definitions, permitted locations, size and height limits, brightness caps (0.3 foot-candles above ambient at night), content restrictions, animation dwell times, and comprehensive sign program requirements.

## Overview

Large venues rely on digital signage and marquees for event promotion, wayfinding, sponsorship display, and brand identity. These signs are governed by municipal sign codes that regulate their size, placement, brightness, content, and animation characteristics. Regulatory frameworks vary by municipality, but common elements include sign class definitions, maximum dimensions, brightness standards, and content transition requirements. Large venue developments often require comprehensive sign programs that consolidate all signage under a single development permit.

## Key Components

### Municipal Sign Code Frameworks

- Municipal land use bylaws and zoning codes contain sign regulations specific to commercial and entertainment districts
- Calgary's Land Use Bylaw 1P2007 provides a representative framework with sign class definitions, permitted locations, and dimensional limits
- Edmonton and Toronto maintain similar but distinct regulatory frameworks, reflecting the municipality-specific nature of sign regulation
- Sign class definitions categorize signs by type (freestanding, fascia, projecting, canopy, electronic message centre) with different rules for each class

### Brightness Standards

- The International Sign Association (ISA) and Sign Research Foundation (SRF) established a brightness standard of 0.3 foot-candles above ambient light measured at the property line as the threshold for acceptable night-time brightness
- Edmonton specifies a 400 nits maximum for electronic signs during night-time hours
- Calgary applies a 3.0 lux measurement standard at the property line
- Brightness levels must be automatically adjusted based on ambient light conditions, typically using photocell sensors

### Animation and Content Restrictions

- Electronic message centre (EMC) signs are subject to dwell time requirements — the minimum duration each static image must be displayed before transitioning
- Typical dwell times range from 6 to 10 seconds depending on the jurisdiction
- Full-motion video may be prohibited or restricted to specific sign classes or zoning districts
- Content restrictions may prohibit messages that simulate traffic signals, emergency vehicles, or create safety hazards for motorists

### Comprehensive Sign Programs

- Large venue developments typically require a comprehensive sign program (CSP) that covers all exterior signage under a unified development permit
- CSPs allow venues to negotiate site-specific sign standards that may exceed standard bylaw limits in exchange for coordinated design and reduced visual clutter
- The CSP approach is common for stadium, arena, and convention centre developments where multiple large-format signs serve different functions (event promotion, wayfinding, sponsorship)

### Residential Proximity Requirements

- Signs near residential areas are subject to additional restrictions including overnight de-energizing requirements
- Buffer distances from residential zones may reduce permitted sign size and brightness
- Overnight de-energizing (typically between 11 PM and 6 AM) is commonly required for electronic signs within a specified distance of residential properties

## Venue Type Variations

Stadiums and arenas typically have the most complex signage programs due to large exterior LED displays, scoreboard-visible signage, and sponsorship obligations. Convention centres balance promotional signage with wayfinding requirements across large campus footprints. Amphitheatres and fairground venues may operate under temporary sign permits for seasonal or event-specific signage that differs from permanent installation regulations.
