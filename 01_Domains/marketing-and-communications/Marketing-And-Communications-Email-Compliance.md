# Email Marketing Regulatory Compliance

Regulatory frameworks governing commercial email for venues: CAN-SPAM Act (US opt-out model with seven core requirements) and CASL (Canadian opt-in model with express/implied consent requirements), carrying distinct compliance obligations and penalty structures.

## Overview

Venues operating email marketing programs must comply with distinct regulatory frameworks depending on the jurisdiction of their recipients. The United States CAN-SPAM Act and Canada's Anti-Spam Legislation (CASL) represent fundamentally different regulatory philosophies — opt-out versus opt-in — and carry different penalty structures. Venues with audiences spanning the US-Canada border must comply with both frameworks simultaneously, applying the more restrictive standard (CASL) as the baseline.

## Key Components

### CAN-SPAM Act (US Federal)

The CAN-SPAM Act establishes seven core requirements for commercial email:

- Accurate header information — the "From," "To," and routing information must be truthful
- Non-deceptive subject lines — the subject line must accurately reflect the message content
- Advertisement identification — the message must be clearly identified as an advertisement
- Physical postal address — every commercial email must include the sender's valid physical postal address
- Opt-out mechanism — every message must include a clear, conspicuous way to opt out of future emails
- Opt-out processing within 10 business days — opt-out requests must be honored within 10 business days
- Third-party monitoring — senders are responsible for the actions of companies they hire to handle email marketing on their behalf

### CASL (Canada Federal)

Canada's Anti-Spam Legislation imposes a consent-based framework:

- Prior consent required — senders must have either express consent (explicit opt-in) or implied consent (existing business relationship within the past two years) before sending commercial electronic messages
- Sender identification — every message must clearly identify the sender and include contact information
- Unsubscribe mechanism — every message must include a functioning unsubscribe mechanism that remains operational for at least 60 days after the message is sent
- Implied consent has a limited duration — it expires after two years from the last purchase or business inquiry

### Key Differences

- CAN-SPAM is an opt-out model — senders can email anyone until they opt out
- CASL is an opt-in model — senders must have consent before the first email
- CASL carries significantly higher penalties — administrative monetary penalties can reach multi-million-dollar levels for serious violations
- CAN-SPAM penalties are enforced by the FTC on a per-violation basis

### Cross-Border Implications

- Venues with audiences in both countries must maintain compliance with both frameworks
- CASL's stricter opt-in requirement effectively becomes the operational baseline for venues serving cross-border audiences
- Email list segmentation by jurisdiction enables differentiated consent management
- In-venue Wi-Fi email capture programs must present appropriate consent language based on the venue's jurisdiction

## Venue Type Variations

All venue types conducting email marketing face the same regulatory requirements. The compliance burden scales with list size, sending frequency, and cross-border audience composition. Venues near the US-Canada border or those hosting international events face the most complex compliance landscape and should default to CASL-compliant opt-in consent as the standard practice.
