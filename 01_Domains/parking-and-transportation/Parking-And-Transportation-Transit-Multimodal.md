# Transit and Multimodal Venue Access

Integration of public transit, bicycle infrastructure, and pedestrian access into venue transportation planning, including transit agency coordination, incentive programs, free bicycle valet services, and pedestrian flow management for safe crossings.

## Overview

Multimodal transportation planning reduces vehicle congestion around venues by distributing patron arrivals across transit, cycling, and pedestrian modes. Effective multimodal integration requires advance coordination with transit agencies, investment in supporting infrastructure, and active promotion of alternatives to single-occupancy vehicle travel. The FHWA special events framework emphasizes multimodal planning as a core component of event transportation management.

## Key Components

### Transit Agency Coordination

- Venue operators coordinate with local transit authorities to increase bus and rail service frequency on event days
- Special event transit routes or express services connect major transit hubs directly to venue entrances
- Joint marketing of transit options with event tickets reduces parking demand and improves arrival distribution
- Transit integration into stadium and arena access planning includes dedicated bus staging areas and pedestrian connections from rail stations

### Transit Incentive Programs

- Some venues bundle transit passes with event tickets to shift mode share away from private vehicles
- Discounted or free transit on event days has been used to reduce parking pressure at venues with constrained site access
- Incentive programs are most effective when combined with clear wayfinding from transit stops to venue gates

### Bicycle Infrastructure

- Bicycle valet programs provide free, staffed secure parking for cyclists attending events
- MLB Green club initiatives have promoted bicycle valet services as part of broader sustainability programs at ballparks
- Dedicated cycling routes to venue sites reduce conflicts between cyclists and vehicle traffic
- Bike-share station placement near venue entrances supports last-mile connections from transit

### Pedestrian Flow Management

- Safe crossing design at intersections surrounding venues protects pedestrians during high-volume ingress and egress
- Dedicated pedestrian corridors separate foot traffic from vehicle movements on venue access roads
- Crowd chokepoint mitigation at transit station exits and pedestrian bridges prevents dangerous congestion
- Signal timing adjustments at surrounding intersections prioritize pedestrian phases during event periods

## Venue Type Variations

Arenas and stadiums in urban locations typically have the strongest transit integration due to proximity to existing rail and bus networks. Suburban venues face greater challenges in offering viable transit alternatives and may rely more heavily on shuttle services from remote park-and-ride lots. Venues with dedicated rail stations (such as those built adjacent to light rail lines) see the highest transit mode shares.
