# Union Labor Jurisdictions in Venues

Large venues operate under multiple collective bargaining agreements with unions including IATSE (stagehands/technicians), UNITE HERE (concessions/hospitality), Teamsters (freight/logistics), and building trades, each with distinct jurisdictional scope.

## Overview

Major venues commonly operate under three to six or more simultaneous collective bargaining agreements, each governing a distinct scope of work. Understanding and respecting jurisdictional boundaries is fundamental to venue operations — crossing jurisdictional lines triggers grievances, work stoppages, and financial penalties. Effective staffing plans map every task to the correct union jurisdiction before event day.

## IATSE Jurisdiction

- The International Alliance of Theatrical Stage Employees (IATSE) holds exclusive jurisdiction over technical stage work in most unionized venues across North America
- Scope typically includes: rigging, lighting, sound, video/projection, scenic construction and movement, wardrobe, and props
- Local agreements define specific scope — IATSE locals vary in their coverage of related tasks such as forklift operation in loading docks or LED wall assembly
- Minimum crew calls, overtime structures, meal penalty provisions, and turnaround times are defined in each local's CBA
- Department head positions (head carpenter, head electrician, head rigger) are typically filled from the local's membership roster

## UNITE HERE Jurisdiction

- UNITE HERE represents workers in concessions, food service, hospitality, and housekeeping in many major venues
- Scope typically includes: concession stand operations, premium catering service, suite attendants, kitchen staff, and custodial/housekeeping
- Contract structures often include service charge distribution formulas, tip pooling rules, and health benefit provisions tied to hours worked
- In venues with outsourced F&B (e.g., Aramark, Levy, Delaware North), the CBA may be between the union and the concessionaire rather than the venue operator

## Teamsters Jurisdiction

- The International Brotherhood of Teamsters holds jurisdiction over freight, logistics, trucking, and loading dock operations in many venue markets
- Scope typically includes: truck loading/unloading, freight movement from dock to stage, forklift operation (where not covered by IATSE), and warehouse operations
- Touring production advance sheets must account for Teamsters jurisdiction in dock operations to avoid jurisdictional disputes with IATSE over equipment movement

## CBA Structures

- **Recognition clauses** define the bargaining unit and the specific work covered
- **Scope of work provisions** delineate what tasks fall under the agreement — the most common source of jurisdictional disputes
- **Wage scales** are typically structured by classification (e.g., department head, journeyman, apprentice) with separate rates for regular time, overtime, and premium time (holidays, sixth/seventh consecutive days)
- **Grievance and arbitration procedures** provide the dispute resolution mechanism — most CBAs mandate binding arbitration as the final step
- **Management rights clauses** reserve the employer's right to direct operations, assign work, and determine staffing levels within the constraints of the agreement

## Right-to-Work Considerations

- In U.S. right-to-work states, employees cannot be required to join a union or pay dues as a condition of employment, even in a unionized workplace
- This affects union density and bargaining dynamics but does not eliminate the CBA — the agreement still governs wages, conditions, and scope for represented positions
- Venue operators in right-to-work jurisdictions may have more flexibility in staffing but must still comply with any applicable CBA provisions

## Jurisdictional Mapping in Staffing Plans

- Pre-event staffing plans should map every anticipated task to the correct union jurisdiction
- Advance coordination with union business agents or stewards prevents day-of disputes
- Touring production riders frequently specify labor requirements that may conflict with local jurisdictional boundaries — resolution requires pre-advance negotiation between the tour's production manager and local union representatives
