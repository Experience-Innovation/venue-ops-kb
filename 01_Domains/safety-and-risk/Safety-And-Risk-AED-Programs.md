# AED Programs and Placement

Automated External Defibrillator programs for venues, covering placement density, maintenance, staff training, and the regulatory landscape across US and Canadian jurisdictions.

## Overview

AED (Automated External Defibrillator) programs are critical for venues hosting large gatherings. Sudden cardiac arrest survival decreases by approximately 10% for every minute without defibrillation, making the collapse-to-shock time target of 5 minutes or less essential. AED programs at venues integrate with the broader mass gathering medical staffing framework established by Sanders et al. (1986) and the NAEMSP Position Statement ([[Source-0291-Sanders-1986-Mass-Gathering-Medical-Care]], [[Source-0292-NIH-StatPearls-EMS-Mass-Gatherings-2023]]).

## Placement Standards

Effective AED placement in venues follows evidence-based principles designed to minimize the collapse-to-shock interval ([[Source-0008-NIH-StatPearls-EMS-Mass-Gatherings]]):

- **3-minute access target:** AED accessible within 3 minutes from any location in the venue, consistent with the BLS 4-minute response time benchmark ([[Source-0291-Sanders-1986-Mass-Gathering-Medical-Care]])
- **Co-location with first aid stations:** AEDs placed at every fixed medical point to ensure availability when medical staff are already responding
- **High-traffic zone prioritization:** Additional AED units positioned at entrances, concourses, concession areas, and premium seating areas where crowd density is highest
- **Zone-based trained operators:** Each event sector has designated AED-trained staff, ensuring no reliance on a single responder crossing the venue
- **Vertical distribution:** Multi-level venues require AEDs on each floor or level, as elevator transit time can exceed the collapse-to-shock window
- **Outdoor staging areas:** Queue lines, parking lots, and tailgating areas require AED coverage, particularly at outdoor venues where distances from fixed medical stations are greatest
- **Event-day positioning:** For temporary or reconfigured venue layouts, AED positions are reviewed and adjusted based on the specific event floor plan. Locations documented on venue medical plans and reviewed 30+ days pre-event

## Staff Training and Response Protocols

AED programs require more than device placement — the training and response protocol framework determines actual outcomes ([[Source-0008-NIH-StatPearls-EMS-Mass-Gatherings]]):

- **Training certification:** All designated AED operators must hold current CPR/AED certification (American Heart Association, Red Cross, or equivalent). Refresher training is recommended at minimum annually, with event-day briefings for temporary staff
- **Activation protocol:** Upon cardiac arrest recognition, the nearest trained responder retrieves the AED while a second responder begins CPR and a third activates EMS. Clear role assignments prevent delay
- **Integration with medical staffing model:** AED response integrates with the tiered medical staffing model. BLS-level responders initiate AED use; ALS responders provide advanced cardiac care upon arrival within the 8-minute target ([[Source-0291-Sanders-1986-Mass-Gathering-Medical-Care]])
- **Post-event data download:** AED units with data recording capability are downloaded after every deployment for quality review and EMS documentation
- **Post-use debriefing:** All AED deployments trigger a formal incident debrief including response time analysis, protocol adherence review, and patient outcome documentation

## Regulatory Landscape

### United States
- All 50 states have Public Access Defibrillation (PAD) laws with Good Samaritan immunity for AED use by trained bystanders
- California requires AEDs in buildings with occupancy exceeding 300
- New York requires AEDs in public assembly buildings and state buildings
- Florida mandates AEDs in schools
- Seven states (New York, Pennsylvania, Maryland, Georgia, Iowa, Oregon, and Washington D.C.) have codified mass gathering medical requirements that may include AED provisions ([[Source-0292-NIH-StatPearls-EMS-Mass-Gatherings-2023]])

### Canada
- Manitoba: Mandatory since January 31, 2014 (first province to legislate)
- British Columbia: Mandatory under Bill M 219 (2024)
- Ontario: Bill 141 phased implementation
- Alberta: No mandatory AED legislation (voluntary placement)

## Maintenance

NFPA 99 governs AED maintenance schedules. Key maintenance requirements include:

- Monthly visual inspections (readiness indicator check, electrode pad expiry, battery status)
- Post-use servicing including electrode pad replacement and data download
- Annual comprehensive inspection per manufacturer specifications
- Battery replacement per manufacturer lifecycle (typically 2-5 years depending on model)
- EMS registration of AED locations with local emergency dispatch to enable directed bystander response
- Documented maintenance logs retained for regulatory compliance and liability protection
