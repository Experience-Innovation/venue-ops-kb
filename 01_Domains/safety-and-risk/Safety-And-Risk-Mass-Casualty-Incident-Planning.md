# Mass Casualty Incident (MCI) Planning

Pre-event planning frameworks for mass casualty events at venues, including triage systems, casualty collection points, hospital coordination, and escalation triggers.

## Overview

Mass Casualty Incident (MCI) planning prepares venues for events where the number of casualties overwhelms available medical resources. Planning includes tiered trigger criteria, pre-designated triage zones, ambulance staging, hospital coordination, and family reunification areas.

## Triage Systems — START vs. SALT Comparison

### START Triage (1983)

START (Simple Triage and Rapid Treatment) was developed in 1983 by the Newport Beach Fire and Marine Department and Hoag Hospital, Newport Beach, California. Key co-developer: Victoria Cleary, EMS Manager ([[Source-0295-CHEMM-START-Adult-Triage-Algorithm]]). START uses four triage categories: Deceased/Expectant (Black), Immediate (Red), Delayed (Yellow), and Walking Wounded/Minor (Green). The assessment sequence follows Respiration, Perfusion, Mental Status (RPM). A 1996 modification by Benson et al. substituted radial pulse for capillary refill, improving accuracy in cold temperatures ([[Source-0295-CHEMM-START-Adult-Triage-Algorithm]]). START remains the most widely used mass casualty triage system in the United States.

### SALT Triage (2008)

SALT (Sort, Assess, Lifesaving Interventions, Treatment/Transport) was developed in 2008 by a CDC-sponsored expert working group and endorsed by ACEP, ACS Committee on Trauma, American Trauma Society, NAEMSP, NDLSEC, and STIPDA ([[Source-0296-SALT-MCI-Triage-Endorsement-2008]]). SALT uses five categories (Dead, Immediate, Delayed, Minimal, Expectant), differentiating it from START by separating Expectant from Dead and Minimal from Delayed. A key innovation is allowing lifesaving interventions (hemorrhage control, airway management, chest decompression) before category assignment ([[Source-0296-SALT-MCI-Triage-Endorsement-2008]]). SALT covers all ages and all hazards.

### START vs. SALT Comparison

| Dimension | START | SALT |
|---|---|---|
| Origin year | 1983 | 2008 |
| Origin body | Newport Beach Fire Dept./Hoag Hospital | CDC-sponsored working group |
| Categories | 4 (Deceased, Immediate, Delayed, Minor) | 5 (Dead, Immediate, Delayed, Minimal, Expectant) |
| Lifesaving interventions before tagging? | No | Yes (hemorrhage control, airway, chest decompression) |
| Population scope | Originally adults | All ages, all hazards |
| MUCC compliance | No | Yes |
| Primary adoption | Widely used in US EMS systems | Increasingly mandated as national standard |
| Assessment sequence | RPM (Respirations, Perfusion, Mental status) | Global sort, individual assess, LSI, assign category |

SALT is compliant with the Model Uniform Core Criteria (MUCC), the proposed US national standard for mass casualty triage. START is not MUCC compliant ([[Source-0297-MUCC-Mass-Casualty-Triage-Guideline]]). SALT replaces START in jurisdictions that have adopted the MUCC framework ([[Source-0297-MUCC-Mass-Casualty-Triage-Guideline]]).

## Incident Commander Qualifications — NFPA 1026

NFPA 1026 (2024 Edition) Chapter 4 establishes Incident Commander Job Performance Requirements including incident size-up and command establishment, IAP development, resource management, communication operations, ICS integration, and demobilization ([[Source-0298-NFPA-1026-2024-Incident-Commander]]). The 2024 edition added a new requirement for continuing education and professional development ([[Source-0298-NFPA-1026-2024-Incident-Commander]]).

NFPA 1026 aligns with NIMS ICS; FEMA IS-100/200/700 provide the foundation, while IS-300/400 approach the NFPA 1026 IC JPR level. Pro Board or IFSAC certification provides the formal professional qualification layer ([[Source-0298-NFPA-1026-2024-Incident-Commander]]).

No jurisdiction has been identified that mandates NFPA 1026 compliance specifically for private venue operators. Venue operators typically face ICS/NIMS requirements through permit conditions, special event licensing, or contractual obligations with jurisdictional fire authorities ([[Source-0298-NFPA-1026-2024-Incident-Commander]]).

## Tiered Response

- **Code Yellow:** Minor MCI, local resources adequate
- **Code Orange:** Moderate MCI, mutual aid requested
- **Code Red:** Major MCI, full regional activation

## Staffing and Resources

Pre-event MCI escalation plans must address ambulance staging, helicopter landing zones where applicable, casualty collection points, and pre-arranged agreements with receiving hospitals. Medical plan pre-approval is recommended 30 days prior to events.
