# Alcohol-Related Liability and Dram Shop Exposure

Dram shop liability creates direct legal exposure for venues serving alcohol when a visibly intoxicated patron subsequently causes injury or death to a third party. In dram shop states, the injured third party can sue both the intoxicated person and the establishment that served them. This liability framework makes responsible beverage service not just a best practice but a direct risk management imperative for venue security and F&B operations [[Source-0433-R24-Legal-Liability]].

## US Dram Shop Variations

Dram shop statutes vary significantly by state. Some states impose strict liability (the act of serving an intoxicated person is sufficient), while others require negligence (the venue knew or should have known the patron was intoxicated). A minority of states have no dram shop liability (Nevada, Virginia). In states with dram shop laws, venue liability can extend to injuries occurring off-premises hours after the patron departs — creating security exposure well beyond the venue's physical boundary [[Source-0433-R24-Legal-Liability]].

## Canadian Provincial Liability

In Canada, commercial host liability for alcohol-related incidents operates under provincial legislation and common law duty of care. The standard generally requires reasonable steps to prevent over-service. Alberta's Gaming, Liquor and Cannabis Act and Ontario's Liquor Licence and Control Act impose licence-holder obligations that, when breached, can support civil liability claims [[Source-0433-R24-Legal-Liability]].

## Security Integration

Venue security operations intersect with alcohol liability at multiple points: monitoring concourse behavior for signs of intoxication, managing ejections of intoxicated patrons (with duty-of-care obligations to ensure the ejected person is not placed in danger), enforcing cut-off times, and documenting incidents for potential litigation defense. Security and F&B teams must operate under coordinated protocols — security identifies behavioral indicators, F&B staff execute service decisions [[Source-0433-R24-Legal-Liability]].

## Training and Documentation

Most jurisdictions require alcohol service staff to hold responsible beverage service certification (TIPS, ServSafe Alcohol, Smart Serve in Ontario, ProServe in Alberta). Venue security staff benefit from the same training to identify intoxication indicators during patron monitoring. Documentation of training completion, incident reports, and service refusals provides the evidentiary foundation for litigation defense [[Source-0433-R24-Legal-Liability]].
