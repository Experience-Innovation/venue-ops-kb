# Anchor Tenant Agreement Frameworks

## Overview

The anchor tenant agreement is the foundational legal instrument governing the relationship between a venue operator and the primary occupant — typically a professional sports franchise — in arenas and stadiums. The single most consequential structural choice in these agreements is whether the arrangement is characterized as a license or a lease, a distinction that determines who retains operational control of the facility during and between tenant occupancy periods ([[Source-0351-IAVM-Venue-Executive-Competency-Standards|IAVM CVE Standards]]).

## License vs. Lease: The Defining Legal Distinction

A lease conveys a possessory real property interest to the tenant, granting exclusive control during the term, the right to exclude the landlord, and protection under landlord-tenant statutory law including security-of-tenure provisions. A license, by contrast, conveys only a contractual permission to use the premises — no property interest transfers, the licensor retains operational control, and the agreement terminates by its own contractual terms rather than through eviction proceedings ([[Source-0351-IAVM-Venue-Executive-Competency-Standards|IAVM CVE Standards]]).

The industry has moved decisively toward license models over the past 25 years for three primary reasons: they preserve operator control of non-game-day scheduling, they enable the venue to generate revenue from other events without tenant interference, and they avoid landlord-tenant statutory protections that would complicate enforcement ([[Source-0351-IAVM-Venue-Executive-Competency-Standards|IAVM CVE Standards]]).

## Case Study: MSG/Knicks Arena License Agreement

The Madison Square Garden/New York Knicks Arena License Agreement (dated April 15, 2020) is among the most detailed publicly available examples of a license-model anchor tenant agreement. The agreement explicitly states that it "shall be construed as a grant of a license" and "shall not operate to vest in the Knicks any ownership or leasehold interest, or other real estate interest, in the Arena." The 35-year term (April 2020 through June 2055) provides substantial stability while avoiding the landlord-tenant framework entirely ([[Source-0351-IAVM-Venue-Executive-Competency-Standards|IAVM CVE Standards]]).

The Knicks pay a fixed license fee that started at approximately $21.8M (prorated) and escalates at 3% annually — a structure that transfers the revenue risk of good and poor seasons away from the base facility payment entirely onto the tenant ([[Source-0351-IAVM-Venue-Executive-Competency-Standards|IAVM CVE Standards]]).

## Capital Expenditure Responsibility

Capital expenditure allocation in anchor tenant agreements typically follows three principles: (1) the venue operator bears responsibility for maintaining the facility to the agreed standard (typically defined as first-class or peer-comparable); (2) alterations required solely to comply with new league rules are borne by the tenant; and (3) capital improvements that generate additional premium seating revenue are shared or separately governed ([[Source-0351-IAVM-Venue-Executive-Competency-Standards|IAVM CVE Standards]]).

## Three-Party Governance Structures

The Oklahoma City Paycom Center arrangement illustrates how anchor tenant agreements can operate as a three-party structure, with the city (owner), Thunder (anchor tenant), and Legends/ASM Global (venue manager) each holding defined roles. The base management fee paid to Legends/ASM is $350,000 annually, with an 8% incentive on adjusted gross income over $11M and a compensation cap of 16% of gross adjusted income. The Thunder pays $58,000 per home game to Legends/ASM for game-day services, increasing 3% annually for the first four years ([[Source-0352-SBJ-Legends-ASM-OKC-Arena-Deal|SBJ OKC Arena Deal]]).

## Operational Implications

The choice between license and lease is not semantic — it determines who has authority over scheduling, alterations, facility access, and agreement termination. Venue operators seeking to maximize programming flexibility and non-event-day revenue generation should default to license models for all anchor tenant arrangements. The three-party model (public owner, private manager, franchise tenant) adds governance complexity but provides public accountability and risk distribution that single-party structures lack.
