# Common Area Maintenance Cost Allocation

## Overview

Common area maintenance (CAM) charges are the mechanism by which shared facility costs are allocated among multiple tenants in multi-use venues. These charges fund the operational expenses of spaces used collectively by all occupants, ensuring that no single tenant bears a disproportionate share of shared infrastructure costs ([[Source-0365-Wall-Street-Prep-CAM-Charges|Wall Street Prep CAM]]).

## CAM Cost Categories

In arena and convention centre contexts, CAM typically covers ([[Source-0365-Wall-Street-Prep-CAM-Charges|Wall Street Prep CAM]]):

- **Utilities for common areas:** HVAC, lighting, and water for lobbies, corridors, restrooms, and shared spaces
- **Security for shared spaces:** Patrol and monitoring of areas outside individual tenant-controlled zones
- **Routine cleaning and maintenance:** Daily cleaning of lobbies, corridors, and shared circulation areas
- **Insurance on shared infrastructure:** Property and liability coverage for common building elements
- **Grounds maintenance:** Exterior landscaping, parking lot maintenance, and snow/ice removal

## Proportional Allocation Formula

The standard CAM calculation allocates costs proportionally to each tenant's share of gross leasable area ([[Source-0365-Wall-Street-Prep-CAM-Charges|Wall Street Prep CAM]]):

**Tenant CAM = (Tenant RSF / Gross Leasable Area) x Annual CAM Charge**

This formula ensures that tenants occupying larger portions of the facility contribute proportionally more to shared costs. The simplicity of the formula, however, can create equity issues when tenants have significantly different usage patterns — a tenant that operates 300 days per year generates more wear on common areas than one operating 80 days per year, yet both pay based on square footage rather than usage intensity.

## Capital Expenditure Exclusions

Capital expenditures are typically excluded from CAM calculations; they are handled through separate capital reserve funds or direct cost-sharing agreements. This distinction matters operationally because capital improvement decisions — such as a new scoreboard, HVAC upgrades, or locker room renovations — require different governance processes than routine maintenance expenses ([[Source-0365-Wall-Street-Prep-CAM-Charges|Wall Street Prep CAM]]).

In multi-tenant venues, major capital improvements that benefit multiple tenants may require approval from all benefiting parties before proceeding, creating potential coordination challenges when tenant priorities diverge ([[Source-0365-Wall-Street-Prep-CAM-Charges|Wall Street Prep CAM]]).

## Application in Anchor Tenant Agreements

The MSG/Knicks agreement addresses capital allocation through explicit provisions: the licensor bears responsibility for maintaining the arena to NBA arena standards at its cost, but the Knicks must fund any alterations required solely by new NBA rule enactments after the agreement's commencement. Alterations intended to generate additional premium seating revenues for both parties are governed by a separate joint revenue-sharing process ([[Source-0351-IAVM-Venue-Executive-Competency-Standards|IAVM CVE Standards]]).

## Technology Infrastructure as Shared Cost

In modern multi-use venues, technology infrastructure — scoreboards, ribbon boards, Wi-Fi, POS networks, DAS cellular systems, broadcast infrastructure — represents both a significant capital investment and a source of governance complexity when multiple tenants expect priority access or control. The OKC arena model — where the concessionaire contributes 1% of annual gross sales to a capital fund for F&B equipment replacement — demonstrates how technology maintenance costs can be shared through a tiered contribution model rather than a single owner-pays-all approach ([[Source-0352-SBJ-Legends-ASM-OKC-Arena-Deal|SBJ OKC Arena Deal]]).

## Operational Implications

CAM charge allocation is a frequent source of tenant disputes, particularly when the cost basis expands to include items that tenants view as venue operator responsibilities rather than shared costs. Best practice requires explicit enumeration of included and excluded costs in the tenant agreement, with annual reconciliation and transparent reporting of actual expenses against budgeted charges.
