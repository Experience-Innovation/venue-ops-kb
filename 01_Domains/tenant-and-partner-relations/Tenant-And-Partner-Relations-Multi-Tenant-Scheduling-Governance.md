# Multi-Tenant Scheduling Governance

## Overview

Multi-tenant venues hosting both NBA and NHL franchises face the most intensive scheduling governance challenges in the public assembly venue industry. Both seasons run from October through June with significant overlap, creating a compressed calendar that requires formal coordination mechanisms at both league and venue levels ([[Source-0351-IAVM-Venue-Executive-Competency-Standards|IAVM CVE Standards]]).

## Contractual Priority Rules

NBA league rules give NBA teams contractual priority in shared arenas — a provision embedded in arena license agreements with both tenants. This priority affects not only game scheduling but also practice times, loading dock access, and back-of-house space allocation. The contractual priority does not mean NHL teams are excluded, but rather that scheduling conflicts are resolved in the NBA team's favor ([[Source-0351-IAVM-Venue-Executive-Competency-Standards|IAVM CVE Standards]]).

## League-to-League Coordination

The NBA and NHL work intensively together to coordinate scheduling, particularly for cities with both franchises sharing a facility. This coordination extends beyond game dates to include pre-game access windows, changeover times between configurations, and the sequencing of non-game events (concerts, family shows, corporate events) around both teams' schedules. The coordination complexity multiplies when playoff scenarios extend one or both teams' seasons into overlapping windows ([[Source-0351-IAVM-Venue-Executive-Competency-Standards|IAVM CVE Standards]]).

## Physical Infrastructure Solutions

Physical infrastructure investments have emerged to reduce the operational friction of multi-tenant scheduling:

- **Convertible seating systems:** The Utah Delta Center is undergoing a major renovation to install a convertible lower bowl system that can shift the seating by nearly 12 feet, allowing the venue to seamlessly transition between basketball and hockey configurations
- **Expanded back-of-house facilities:** The Washington Wizards/Capitals' Capital One Arena features what is described as the largest game-day locker room compound in the NBA in an arena where the practice facility is not attached — three times the size of previous accommodations — demonstrating how multi-sport venue operators invest in back-of-house infrastructure to satisfy both anchor tenants

These infrastructure investments represent capital solutions to what is fundamentally a governance problem — reducing the operational time and cost of venue changeovers so that both tenants can be accommodated within tighter scheduling windows ([[Source-0351-IAVM-Venue-Executive-Competency-Standards|IAVM CVE Standards]]).

## Back-of-House Space Allocation

Back-of-house space allocation in multi-sport venues requires a dedicated scheduling governance layer. Loading dock access, staging areas, locker rooms, and production spaces must be sequenced around multiple tenants' event calendars — compounded by the fact that touring productions, sports teams, corporate events, and cleaning/changeover crews may all require overlapping access windows ([[Source-0351-IAVM-Venue-Executive-Competency-Standards|IAVM CVE Standards]]).

## Governance Recommendations

Venues managing multiple anchor tenants should establish clear governance provisions addressing:

- Which tenant has scheduling priority and under what conditions
- Minimum changeover times between events
- Back-of-house space allocation rules during overlapping windows
- Dispute resolution for scheduling conflicts
- Cost allocation for accelerated changeovers when tight scheduling requires additional labor
