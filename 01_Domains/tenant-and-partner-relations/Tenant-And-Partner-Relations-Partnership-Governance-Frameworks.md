# Partnership Governance Frameworks

## Overview

The fundamental governance dynamic in any venue is determined first by who owns the building and who is accountable for operations. Governance structures vary significantly between publicly-owned and privately-owned venues, with each model creating distinct accountability relationships, decision-making authority distributions, and escalation paths ([[Source-0351-IAVM-Venue-Executive-Competency-Standards|IAVM CVE Standards]]).

## Publicly-Owned with Private Manager

This model — the most common for arenas and convention centres — involves a city, county, authority, or Crown agency owning the asset while a private venue management company operates it under a management agreement. The Overland Park Convention Center illustrates the standard structure: the city engages the operator as "an independent contractor to perform management services," acting on the city's behalf and as agent for the city in operational matters, subject to the city's retained authority over significant decisions. The operator manages day-to-day operations, enters contracts in the city's name, and maintains all licenses and permits in the city's name. Upon termination, the city or its successor manager assumes responsibility for all existing bookings and contracts ([[Source-0366-Overland-Park-Convention-Center-Agreement|Overland Park CC Agreement]]).

The Ontario Crown agency model (MTCC/OCC) extends this further: a legislated mandate defines the operational purpose; a board of directors (with provincial and municipal government appointees) provides governance oversight; the Ministry of Tourism, Culture and Sport reviews and approves annual business plans, issues mandate letters, and tables annual reports in the legislature. The auditor's critique — that the Ministry failed to ensure the centres operated in alignment with their tourism mandate rather than optimizing for their own financial profitability — illustrates the accountability gap that emerges when public oversight is insufficiently engaged with operational decisions ([[Source-0360-Ontario-Auditor-General-MTCC-OCC|Ontario AG MTCC/OCC Audit]]).

## Privately-Owned and Owner-Operator

AEG-owned venues, Oak View Group's developing portfolio, and Live Nation's 377-venue network operate with the simplest governance structure: the owner makes operational decisions as owner, subject to tenant agreements but not to a public board. Accountability flows to investors rather than elected officials ([[Source-0357-Quartr-Live-Nation-Breakdown|Quartr Live Nation]]).

## Public-Private Partnerships (P3)

New arena and entertainment district development increasingly employs formal P3 structures in which the public sector provides land, financing, and long-term operating subsidy while a private operator provides management expertise, development capital, and booking relationships. Saskatoon's arena district negotiation — where OVG360 proposed a 30-year management contract in exchange for $15M in development funding and a fee structure capturing up to 15% of profits above $4M annually — illustrates both the appeal and governance complexity of P3 arrangements. City council's March 2026 rejection of that proposal reflects continued public sector ambivalence about ceding operational control to private operators, even when projected revenues are favorable ([[Source-0367-CBC-Saskatoon-Arena-P3-Rejection|CBC Saskatoon Arena]]).

## Joint Operating Committees

Multi-tenant venues and public authority/private operator venues typically establish Joint Operating Committees (JOCs) to manage shared operational decisions. In sports venues with multiple anchor tenants, a JOC commonly involves representatives from each franchise and the venue operator meeting regularly to coordinate scheduling, capital improvements, and service standards. Decision-making authority typically follows a tiered structure: routine operational matters delegated to management level; capital expenditures above a threshold requiring committee approval; fundamental changes requiring consent of all parties ([[Source-0351-IAVM-Venue-Executive-Competency-Standards|IAVM CVE Standards]]).

## Dispute Resolution Mechanisms

Anchor tenant license agreements typically specify a tiered dispute resolution process: operational escalation (management to management), executive escalation (CEO level), formal mediation, and ultimately binding arbitration. In the MSG/Knicks agreement, the NBA has rights to notice of and opportunity to cure Knicks defaults — an unusual third-party governance layer where the professional sports league serves as a co-enforcer of the facility agreement ([[Source-0351-IAVM-Venue-Executive-Competency-Standards|IAVM CVE Standards]]).

For public-sector venues with private managers, dispute escalation ultimately involves the public authority board or municipal council as the final decision-making body. This creates a longer decision timeline but adds democratic accountability — and a political dimension — to operational disagreements ([[Source-0360-Ontario-Auditor-General-MTCC-OCC|Ontario AG MTCC/OCC Audit]]).

## Operational Implications

Governance framework selection has downstream effects on every operational function — from booking policy and pricing strategy to capital investment decisions and vendor selection. Public ownership creates accountability structures that private ownership does not, but also introduces political dynamics, longer decision cycles, and potential misalignment between financial optimization and public mandate fulfillment.
