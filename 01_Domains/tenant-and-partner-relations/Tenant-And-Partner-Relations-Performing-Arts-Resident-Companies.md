# Performing Arts Resident Companies

## Overview

The performing arts centre (PAC) resident company relationship is structurally distinct from commercial tenant models found in arenas and stadiums. The IAVM defines a resident company as "most often an arts organization whose rent is either fully or partially subsidized by the PAC," which may also benefit from PAC marketing and PR activities on its behalf. Resident company agreements typically include priority access to the PAC calendar — enabling the company to schedule its season before the calendar opens to commercial rental presenters ([[Source-0368-IAVM-PAC-Standard-Performance-Reporting|IAVM PAC Handbook]]).

## Hybrid Tenant-Partner Model

In practice, resident company agreements function as hybrid arrangements between tenant and program partner. The relationship differs from commercial tenancy in several key dimensions ([[Source-0368-IAVM-PAC-Standard-Performance-Reporting|IAVM PAC Handbook]]):

- **Subsidized rental rates:** Resident companies typically pay below-market rates in exchange for volume commitments and programming that fulfills the PAC's cultural mission
- **Calendar priority:** Resident companies schedule their seasons first, before the calendar opens to commercial presenters
- **Marketing support:** The PAC may provide marketing and PR services on behalf of the resident company
- **Revenue arrangements:** Ticketing and revenue splits vary significantly by institution

## Cross-Subsidy Economics

PACs must balance three categories of programming that have fundamentally different financial characteristics ([[Source-0368-IAVM-PAC-Standard-Performance-Reporting|IAVM PAC Handbook]]):

1. **Resident companies:** Priority access, subsidized rates, fulfills cultural mandate but generates less revenue per event
2. **Commercial rental presenters:** Market-rate rentals, generate higher revenue per event, fill calendar gaps around resident seasons
3. **Centre-presented programming:** The PAC controls and profits from directly, retaining full revenue and absorbing full risk

The IAVM PAC handbook classifies events by presenter type for performance reporting purposes, distinguishing between centre presentations, resident companies, non-profit rentals, and commercial rentals — each with different financial characteristics and governance relationships ([[Source-0368-IAVM-PAC-Standard-Performance-Reporting|IAVM PAC Handbook]]).

## Scheduling and Revenue Implications

From a scheduling and operations standpoint, PAC operators face an inherent tension: resident companies expect calendar priority and subsidized rates, while commercial rental presenters pay market rates and may generate more revenue per event. The scheduling challenge is to fill remaining calendar capacity with revenue-generating rentals while protecting the resident company's programming needs.

Commercial renters at mid-sized Canadian centres may pay structures such as 11% of gross ticket sales (net of tax) plus a capital reserve fund charge per ticket, or a minimum rental — whichever is greater. This pricing model effectively cross-subsidizes resident companies and community programming through higher commercial rental rates.

## Performance Measurement

The IAVM PAC Standard Performance Reporting handbook provides standardized KPIs for performing arts centres, including Revenue per Patron, Revenue per Event, Occupancy Cost per Square Foot, and institutional financial health ratios. These tools allow PAC executives to benchmark performance against peer institutions and demonstrate public value to boards and funding bodies ([[Source-0368-IAVM-PAC-Standard-Performance-Reporting|IAVM PAC Handbook]]).
