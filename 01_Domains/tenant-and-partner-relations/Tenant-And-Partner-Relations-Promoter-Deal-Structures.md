# Promoter Deal Structures

## Overview

The relationship between venue operators and concert promoters is fundamentally a risk allocation conversation. Three primary deal structures define the live entertainment market, each distributing financial risk, operational responsibility, and revenue capture differently between the venue and the promoter ([[Source-0353-Prism-FM-Concert-Cost-Breakdown|Prism.fm Concert Cost Breakdown]]).

## Flat Rental (Hard Ticket)

The venue charges the promoter a fixed rental fee regardless of attendance or revenue performance. The venue retains concessions, parking, and service charge revenues. The promoter's only revenue source is ticket sales, meaning the promoter absorbs 100% of the show risk. This is the standard model at independent and third-party venues — the venue generates predictable income while the promoter assumes the full financial risk of filling seats ([[Source-0353-Prism-FM-Concert-Cost-Breakdown|Prism.fm Concert Cost Breakdown]]).

## Co-Promotion

Multiple parties divide costs, responsibilities, and profits. A local promoter might handle venue logistics and community marketing while a national promoter provides financial resources and artist relationships. Profit splits are negotiated based on investment level and responsibilities — one partner might handle venue costs and production while another manages marketing and ticketing. This model has enabled shows at venues and budgets that would be impossible for single promoters ([[Source-0353-Prism-FM-Concert-Cost-Breakdown|Prism.fm Concert Cost Breakdown]]).

## Four-Wall Deal

The venue "rents all four walls" to the promoter, including equipment, staff, and infrastructure. The promoter effectively operates the building for the event, bearing all costs and retaining most revenues. This model gives maximum flexibility to touring productions but requires sophisticated promoters capable of full venue operations ([[Source-0353-Prism-FM-Concert-Cost-Breakdown|Prism.fm Concert Cost Breakdown]]).

## Vertically Integrated Operator Model

Live Nation, as the world's largest promoter, operates across all three models depending on whether it is using its own venues or third-party facilities. At its 377 owned venues (as of 2024), Live Nation primarily operates in a co-promotion/internal model, generating revenue from concessions, parking, premium seating, rental income, and ticket service charges ([[Source-0354-Live-Nation-2024-Annual-Report|Live Nation 10-K]]).

## Operational Implications

For venue operators, the choice of deal structure directly affects staffing requirements, revenue predictability, and operational control. Flat rental provides the most predictable venue income but limits upside potential. Co-promotion aligns incentives between venue and promoter but requires transparent financial reporting from both parties. Four-wall deals transfer maximum control — and maximum risk — to the promoter, making them suitable only for well-capitalized promoters with proven operational capacity.
