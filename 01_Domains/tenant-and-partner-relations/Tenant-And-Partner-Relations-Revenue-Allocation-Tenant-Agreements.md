# Revenue Allocation in Tenant Agreements

## Overview

Revenue allocation in anchor tenant agreements follows a consistent logic: the team controls revenues that derive from team identity (tickets, team merchandise, broadcasting rights, team-specific sponsorships), while the operator controls revenues that derive from venue ownership (premium seating licenses, naming rights, F&B concessions, non-team programming). In practice, the boundary is negotiated extensively, with both parties receiving shares of "game-day shared" assets ([[Source-0351-IAVM-Venue-Executive-Competency-Standards|IAVM CVE Standards]]).

## MSG/Knicks Revenue Split Model

The MSG/Knicks agreement illustrates standard revenue splits across major streams ([[Source-0351-IAVM-Venue-Executive-Competency-Standards|IAVM CVE Standards]]):

- **General admission tickets:** Knicks retain 100% — sell and retain all revenue
- **Suite licenses:** Percentage share on game nights; operator has exclusive licensing for all events
- **F&B concessions:** Revenue share percentage; operator manages all operations
- **Team merchandise:** Tenant retains majority of net revenue; operator retains commission on in-arena sales
- **Non-team merchandise:** Operator retains 100%
- **Courtside/team signage:** Tenant retains 100%
- **Arena game-shared signage:** Revenue share; operator manages exclusively
- **Naming rights:** Revenue share; operator has exclusive licensing
- **Broadcasting rights:** Tenant retains 100%; operator provides facilities only

## OKC Three-Party Revenue Model

The Oklahoma City Paycom Center arrangement demonstrates an alternative model where the city (owner), Thunder (anchor tenant), and Legends/ASM Global (venue manager) operate under a three-party structure. The Thunder retains all ticket, concessions, merchandise, and premium seating license revenue from its business at the arena, as well as building naming rights. The base management fee paid to the venue manager is $350,000 annually, with an 8% incentive on adjusted gross income over $11M and a compensation cap of 16% of gross adjusted income ([[Source-0352-SBJ-Legends-ASM-OKC-Arena-Deal|SBJ OKC Arena Deal]]).

## Revenue Category Specificity

Vague language about "shared" revenues is a primary source of dispute in anchor tenant agreements. Every monetizable category — signage, digital boards, suites, F&B, parking, naming rights, data — requires a specific allocation rule, and "game-day" versus "non-game-day" distinctions must be explicit throughout ([[Source-0351-IAVM-Venue-Executive-Competency-Standards|IAVM CVE Standards]]).

## Operational Implications

Revenue allocation decisions made in the anchor tenant agreement have cascading effects on operational budgets, capital investment decisions, and service provider contracts. When F&B revenue is shared, for example, both parties have incentive to invest in service quality. When one party retains 100%, the other party's operational motivation for that revenue stream may diminish. Revenue allocation also determines which party controls the data generated by each revenue stream — a growing governance concern as venues demand full customer data ownership from ticketing and POS platforms.
