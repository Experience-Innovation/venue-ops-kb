## Executive Summary

The venue and hospitality technology space represents one of the most underpenetrated podcast verticals for a niche B2B show. Momentus Technologies runs an in-house "Innovators Series" of video interviews, Tripleseat maintains its own podcast, and Bizzabo fields an "Event Experience" series — but no independent, professionally produced podcast serves the broader venue professional community. That gap is the strategic opportunity. This report provides a complete operational playbook: a 90-day launch timeline, executive guest booking tactics, sponsorship model comparisons calibrated to sub-10,000 download tiers, a curated sponsor pipeline, conference integration strategy, and evidence-backed growth channel rankings specific to B2B professional content in 2025–2026.

## Section 1: B2B Podcast Launch — First 90 Days Framework

### The Strategic Foundation

B2B podcasting in 2025–2026 has matured well beyond brand awareness. The most successful niche professional shows are designed as relationship and pipeline engines, not content marketing experiments. For a venue/hospitality niche show, the core success metric in the first 90 days is not download volume — it is *access*: to venue professionals, to C-suite guests, and to sponsors evaluating a channel before it proves scale.[^1][^2]

Early success for a new B2B podcast looks like: consistent publishing, 40–50%+ average episode completion rates, audience composed of ICP-aligned downloads, and at least one measurable pipeline or relationship outcome per month. These benchmarks are achievable before 500 downloads per episode if execution is deliberate.[^2]

### Pre-Launch Phase (Weeks –8 to –1)

Before a single episode publishes, the infrastructure that drives the launch matters more than the recording quality:

- **Define the listener persona precisely**: job title, venue type (arena, convention center, performing arts center, university), primary pain points in 2026 (AI adoption, cost management, premium experience delivery), and media consumption habits
- **Build the email list first**: a lead magnet (e.g., "2026 Venue Technology Buyer's Guide" or "VenueConnect Session Recap") captures the ICP audience before launch and provides a launchpad for the first episode push[^1]
- **Batch-record 6–12 episodes**: this eliminates the production scramble in weeks 1–4 and allows the first 30 days to be spent entirely on promotion, not recording[^3]
- **Create a guest promo kit template**: every guest should receive ready-to-share social assets (audiograms, quote cards, LinkedIn copy) the day their episode drops — this transforms guests into distribution partners at zero cost[^4]
- **Establish a video-first workflow from day one**: publishing audio-only is a strategic error in 2026; recording talking-head video for YouTube and clipping 30–90 second segments for LinkedIn Shorts is now table-stakes for B2B show growth[^5]

### 90-Day Launch Timeline

| Week/Month | Milestone | Key Activities | Success Metric |
|---|---|---|---|
| Weeks –8 to –6 | Strategy & setup | Define ICP, naming, cover art, choose hosting platform, batch-record 3 episodes | Show concept documented; 3 episodes recorded |
| Weeks –5 to –3 | Guest pipeline & lead magnet | Build tiered guest wishlist (see Section 2), create email lead magnet, soft-launch LinkedIn newsletter | 20 guests in outreach pipeline; 100+ email subscribers |
| Week –2 to –1 | Pre-launch warm-up | Tease trailer on LinkedIn and YouTube; personally DM first 50 ICP followers; submit to Apple, Spotify | Trailer published; directories approved |
| **Week 1–2 (Launch)** | 3-episode drop | Publish 3 episodes simultaneously (launch strategy); activate guest sharing; personal outreach to warm network | 200+ downloads in first 7 days; 50+ LinkedIn video views per clip |
| Weeks 3–6 | Cadence establishment | Weekly episode rhythm; 3–5 LinkedIn clips per episode; guest thank-you sequence | 60%+ of guests share episode content; 40%+ completion rate |
| Weeks 7–10 | First cross-promotion | Identify 2–3 complementary shows for promo swaps; pitch first sponsor in founding partner framing (see Section 3) | First promo swap live; first sponsor conversation booked |
| Weeks 11–13 | Conference prep | Submit speaker/recording session proposal for VenueConnect 2026 (Aug 9–12, Indianapolis); book conference guests [^6] | At least 2 conference recording sessions confirmed |
| Month 4 (Day 90) | Review & pivot | Analyze completion rates, top-performing episode topics, guest-to-share rate; update editorial calendar | 500+ average downloads/episode or 1 signed sponsor |

## Section 2: Guest Booking Playbook — Executive-Level Venue Leaders

### Why Standard Outreach Fails with C-Suite Guests

VP-level and above venue professionals receive more podcast outreach than they did three years ago. Generic cold pitches are ignored. The playbook that consistently delivers results in 2025–2026 is built on four principles: specificity, social proof, low friction, and professional benefit framing.[^7][^8]

Momentus Technologies' Innovators Series offers a live proof point from within the venue industry. Their YouTube series features working interviews with convention center directors, stadium executives, and venue operators discussing real operational challenges — and the guests they book include executives like Kerry Painter (CVE, CMP, CEM, Executive Director of Raleigh Convention Center) and Gary Firlit Jr. (Director of Business Analytics). The guest booking approach that works for this tier is exactly the model that should be mirrored: peer-to-peer framing, authority context, and a clearly defined episode focus.[^9][^10]

### The Tiered Guest Approach

Start with C-tier to build credibility. Organize your wishlist into three tiers:[^11]

- **C-tier (Months 1–2)**: Emerging voices — directors-level and below at mid-size venues, association committee members, rising speakers at IAVM regional events, vendor-side practitioners (e.g., customer success leaders at venue technology companies who interact with operators daily)
- **B-tier (Months 2–4)**: Well-known but reachable — EVPs and VPs at mid-large venues, IAVM committee chairs, frequent VenueConnect speakers, Tripleseat Ignite panelists
- **A-tier (Months 5+)**: Dream guests — CEOs and GMs of major convention centers, IAVM Chair-level leaders, keynote speakers at VenueConnect and PCMA Convening Leaders

Do not cold-pitch A-tier guests before you have 10+ published episodes, at least 3–5 social proof moments (guests sharing your show to their networks), and a media kit.[^11]

### Executive-Level Outreach That Works

| Strategy | Evidence of Effectiveness | Implementation Approach |
|---|---|---|
| **Warm introduction from a previous guest** | Most effective path to C-suite; leverages existing trust[^12] | After each guest recording, ask: "Who else in your peer network would make a great guest?" — request a direct intro, not a contact |
| **Conference pre-booking** | Executives are pre-committed to being "on" at conferences; conversation time is expected[^13] | At VenueConnect 2026, approach target guests in person; offer to record a short segment at the event, not a full future episode commitment |
| **LinkedIn-first relationship building** | Executives who see you engaging authentically with their content are 3-5x more likely to respond to a pitch[^14] | Engage substantively (comments, not just likes) with target guests' content for 2–4 weeks before pitching |
| **"You were mentioned" hook** | Creates irresistible curiosity and a reason to respond | Reference a quote from a conference talk, published article, or LinkedIn post in your outreach |
| **ABM-style personalized pitch** | Mass-blast pitching doesn't work; personalized pitches to niche audiences do[^7] | Every pitch references a recent event (their conference talk, their venue's opening, a challenge their sector is facing) and proposes a specific episode angle |
| **Low-commitment ask** | Executives' core objection is time, not interest | Frame first ask as "30 minutes, recorded remotely, we handle everything" — not a "collaboration" or "ongoing partnership" |
| **Guest as thought leader positioning** | Executives say yes when it builds their brand[^15] | Explicitly state distribution plan: "your episode will be featured to IAVM's network and our LinkedIn audience of [X] venue professionals" |

### Pitch Structure (Under 150 Words)

> Hi [Name], I host [Show Name], a podcast for venue and hospitality leaders navigating the technology and operational shifts reshaping the industry. I recently heard your panel at [Conference] — your point about [specific topic] was exactly the conversation our audience needs more of.
>
> I'd love to feature you for a 30-minute recorded conversation on [specific episode angle tied to their work]. We publish video to YouTube and LinkedIn, and I'll send you ready-to-share clips to distribute to your own network.
>
> Would you be open to a quick email exchange to align on focus and timing? I can work around your conference schedule — including VenueConnect in Indianapolis.

## Section 3: Sponsorship Model Comparison — Sub-10,000 Downloads

### The CPM Problem for Niche B2B Shows

CPM-based pricing actively disadvantages new and niche shows. At the industry average of $25–40 CPM for host-read ads, a show doing 1,500 downloads per episode earns $37.50–$60 per mid-roll slot — not enough to maintain sponsor interest. The strategic pivot is to reframe value entirely: venue management technology buyers represent large contract decisions, and a CPM-equivalent of $100–125 is defensible for a show delivering exactly the right audience.[^16][^17]

B2B and professional services podcasts command $50–100+ CPM, with a B2B podcast generating 8,000 downloads legitimately commanding $1,000 flat-rate per episode — equating to $125 CPM, which is premium by any market standard.[^17]

### Sponsorship Model Comparison

| Model Type | Typical Revenue | Audience Threshold | Fit for Venue Niche | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Programmatic / CPM** | $15–30 CPM pre-roll; $25–40 CPM mid-roll[^16] | 5,000+ downloads/episode for meaningful revenue | Poor at launch | Only viable once audience proves consistent volume; low per-episode yield at sub-3,000 downloads |
| **Host-read flat-rate** | $150–250/episode at under 1,000 downloads; scales to $500–1,000+ at 2,000–5,000 downloads[^18] | Any size | Excellent | Simple budgeting for sponsors; no download volatility risk; easiest first sell |
| **Affiliate / CPA** | 10–20% commission on $30–200 products; 5–10% on $200+[^18] | Works at any size | Moderate | Best for SaaS products with trial-to-purchase tracking; Momentus, Cvent, AudienceView all have demo pipelines |
| **Lead-based / B2B sponsor** | $25–100 per qualified lead; 20 leads per episode = $500–2,000 regardless of download count[^18] | Any size | Excellent for launch | Ideal for venue tech companies wanting demo requests; deliver via gated resource or episode-specific landing page |
| **Bundled multi-channel** | 30–50% premium over standalone episode mention[^18] | Any size | Very good | Bundle podcast mention + LinkedIn post + newsletter feature; total package pricing of $600–1,500/month for smaller shows |
| **Category exclusivity / Founding Sponsor** | 2–3x standard rate for exclusivity; e.g., $500–750/month as a founding partner lock-in[^19] | Pre-launch through 5,000 downloads | Excellent for launch | One sponsor per category (e.g., "venue management software"); locked in before competitors can claim position; 6–12 month minimum |
| **Sponsored webinar / content integration** | $6,000 (IAVM Allied Member)[^20] | Any size | Premium tier | Model borrowed from IAVM's own webinar sponsorship pricing; positions podcast as educational media brand |

### Founding Partner Model — The Right First Pitch

For a niche B2B show launching in the venue space, the **Founding Partner model** is the optimal first sponsorship structure. The pitch is:

- **What it is**: A named category exclusivity deal (e.g., "Official Venue Management Software Partner") for the show's first 12–24 months
- **What they get**: Category lock-out, host-endorsed integration, co-branding on all assets, 2 mid-roll reads per episode, LinkedIn co-promotion, a dedicated quarterly "deep dive" episode on a topic they help shape, plus access to listener survey data
- **What you charge**: $500–750/month at launch (adjusts upward at episode 20 or 2,000+ downloads), billed quarterly
- **Why it works**: It removes the CPM conversation entirely; sponsors pay for strategic positioning and a first-mover competitive advantage that becomes worthless once a competitor takes it[^19][^17]

### Conference Recording Logistics

At each conference, the production approach should be:
- **Portable recording setup**: One camera, clip-on lavalier mics, branded interview backdrop (lightweight pop-up) — total under $2,000 in gear
- **Pre-booked 20-minute slots**: Email confirmed guests before the conference, book specific time slots (15–20 min interviews), record in a quiet corner or side room
- **Create "conference recap" episodes**: Compile 3–4 conversation clips into a single episode that captures the energy and themes of the event — this content has high shareability among attendees
- **Social amplification during the event**: Post 30-second clips to LinkedIn in real time — this builds FOMO and drives follow-follows from other conference attendees

### Cross-Promotion Execution

**Podcast swaps** are the highest-leverage growth tactic available in 2026. Feed swaps (where one full episode or segment is shared on both shows' feeds) outperform standard promo reads by a factor of **10–40x in listener conversion rates**. The average new listener acquisition from cross-promos is approximately 0.75% of the partner show's audience per swap — meaning a swap with a show at 3,000 downloads/episode should yield 20–25 new permanent listeners.[^56][^57]

**Execution sequence**:
1. Listen to 3–5 episodes of the target show; engage publicly with the host on LinkedIn for 2–3 weeks before pitching
2. Pitch the swap with specific proposed episode angles for both shows — not an open-ended "let's collaborate"
3. Start with trailer swaps (lowest friction), graduate to guest swaps, then full feed swaps as trust builds
4. Track performance with unique Bitly links or UTM codes in show notes to measure actual conversion

**Newsletter + Community swaps**: If the show has not yet built audience for a traditional swap, approach hospitality community newsletters and LinkedIn newsletters (e.g., IAVM News with 8,100 subscribers) for mention-for-mention exchanges, referral partnerships, or paid placement in their newsletter at their standard ad rates ($1,600–$2,750/placement).[^20]

### LinkedIn Algorithm — 2025–2026 Critical Mechanics

LinkedIn's algorithm as of 2025–2026 (powered by the 360Brew LLM) operates on three fundamental signals:[^59][^58]

1. **Topic authority**: The system categorizes your profile as an expert in a specific domain. For a venue management podcast, every post should reinforce the same topical cluster (venue technology, event operations, hospitality leadership). Mixed-topic posting dilutes the authority signal.[^14]
2. **Authentic engagement**: The algorithm now detects AI-generated comments and engagement pods. Genuine conversation in comments — substantive replies, not "Great point!" — signals community builder status and gets prioritized distribution.[^58]
3. **Dwell time**: Posts that hold attention (video, multi-page carousels, LinkedIn articles with table of contents) receive greater distribution. The algorithm measures seconds-on-content before scroll.[^66]

**Practical implication**: Post 3x/week from the host's personal profile (not company page), each post built around a single insight from that week's episode. Company page posts receive significantly lower organic reach than personal profiles. Posting alone is insufficient — 15–30 minutes daily of genuine engagement with target guest and audience accounts is required to maintain algorithm trust.[^67][^68]

### YouTube B2B-Specific Strategy

YouTube in 2026 serves two functions for a B2B show: (1) search-based discovery through long-form episode uploads with optimized titles/descriptions, and (2) community building through Shorts clips that drive subscribers back to the full episode. The key is separating "algorithm-based content" (discoverable by people who don't know you) from "community content" (deepening loyalty among existing audience). Both are needed but serve different growth mechanics.[^61][^63]

One 30-minute podcast episode, systematically repurposed, generates: 8–10 short-form video clips for LinkedIn, 2–3 email newsletter segments, a long-form SEO blog post, YouTube Shorts and a full video upload. At a 4 episodes/month cadence, this is 32–40 LinkedIn video clips, 8–12 newsletter segments, and 4 SEO-indexed blog posts — all from a single production session per episode.[^63]
