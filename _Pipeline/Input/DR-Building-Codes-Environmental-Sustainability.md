# Building Codes, Environmental Regulations & Sustainability Certifications for Large Assembly Venues

*Research current as of April 2026. Applicable to convention centres, arenas, stadiums, concert halls, and multi-purpose event facilities in the United States and Canada.*

***

## Executive Summary

Large assembly venues face a layered compliance environment spanning structural building codes (IBC), mechanical and ventilation standards (ASHRAE), federal and state/provincial accessibility law (ADA/AODA), and an expanding ecosystem of voluntary sustainability certifications (LEED, Green Globes, TRUE, ISO 20121, EIC). Regulatory momentum since 2020 has tightened indoor air quality requirements, accelerated refrigerant transitions, and extended mandatory energy benchmarking to smaller buildings. Venues pursuing green certification are increasingly stacking programs — LEED for building performance, TRUE for zero waste, and ISO 20121 for event management — to satisfy both operational goals and the requirements of major event hosts, including the International Olympic Committee.

***

## 1. Building Code Requirements: Assembly Occupancy (IBC 2021/2024)

### 1.1 Occupancy Classifications

The *2021 International Building Code* (IBC), Chapter 3, divides assembly occupancy into five sub-groups based on use. Understanding the correct classification is the starting point for all fire protection, egress, and structural requirements:[^1]

| IBC Group | Description | Venue Examples |
|-----------|-------------|----------------|
| **A-1** | Fixed seating for performing arts or motion pictures | Concert halls, theatres, cinemas |
| **A-2** | Food and drink consumption (alcoholic beverage assumptions apply) | Banquet halls, arena concourses |
| **A-3** | Worship, recreation, lecture, and miscellaneous assembly not in A-1/A-2 | Convention meeting rooms, lecture halls |
| **A-4** | Indoor sporting events (viewing) | NHL arenas, NBA courts, boxing arenas |
| **A-5** | Outdoor sporting events | Stadiums, amphitheatres, race tracks |

Spaces occupied by fewer than 50 people may remain classified under Group B (Business), but any space reaching or exceeding 50 occupants triggers Group A classification. Mixed-occupancy buildings with a total public assembly occupant load greater than 2,500 people are assigned **Risk Category III**, triggering heightened structural and wind/seismic design requirements.[^2][^3]

### 1.2 Occupant Load Calculations (IBC Table 1004.5)

Occupant load determines virtually every downstream code requirement — sprinkler thresholds, exit counts, egress widths, and ADA seating ratios. Key assembly load factors from IBC Table 1004.5:[^4][^5]

- **Fixed seating:** Count of individual seats; bench-type seating = 1 person per 18 linear inches (457 mm)
- **Concentrated use (chairs only, not fixed):** 7 net sq ft per person
- **Unconcentrated use (tables and chairs):** 15 net sq ft per person
- **Standing space:** 5 net sq ft per person

For a 50,000 sq ft convention exhibit hall with tables and chairs, the calculated occupant load would be approximately 3,333 persons (50,000 ÷ 15), immediately placing the space in the highest egress tier.

### 1.3 Fire Protection and Sprinkler Requirements

Automatic sprinkler systems are mandatory under IBC Section 903.2.1 for virtually all Group A assembly occupancies of any meaningful size:[^6]

- **A-1, A-3, A-4 (and combined A occupancies):** Sprinklers required throughout when occupant load reaches **300 or more**, or when multiple A-group areas share exit access with a combined load ≥ 300
- **A-2 (food and beverage):** Sprinklers required when fire area exceeds **5,000 sq ft** OR occupant load exceeds **100** persons, or when located on a floor other than the level of exit discharge
- **A-5 (outdoor/open-air):** Sprinklers required throughout all enclosed accessory areas exceeding 1,000 sq ft

Sprinkler inclusion unlocks significant code benefits: increased allowable building height, larger allowable floor areas, and reduced egress width multipliers — critical for multi-level arenas.[^6]

### 1.4 Means of Egress Requirements (IBC Chapter 10)

The number of required exits scales directly with occupant load:[^7][^8]

| Occupant Load | Minimum Exits Required |
|---------------|------------------------|
| 1–49 | 1 (with restrictions) |
| 50–500 | 2 |
| 501–1,000 | 3 |
| Over 1,000 | 4 |

Exit width calculations per IBC Section 1005:[^8]

- **Doors, gates, corridors, ramps (other than stairs):** 0.20 inches per occupant (non-sprinklered); 0.15 inches per occupant (sprinklered)
- **Stairways:** 0.30 inches per occupant (non-sprinklered); 0.20 inches per occupant (sprinklered)
- **Minimum door clear width:** 32 inches (36 inches nominal)
- **Minimum corridor width:** 44 inches

When multiple exits are provided, each exit must be sized to accommodate **50% of the total occupant load** (IBC Section 1005.1), ensuring safe evacuation even if one exit becomes blocked.[^9]

Assembly occupancies receive a **travel distance allowance** of 250 ft in sprinklered buildings (200 ft non-sprinklered), per IBC Table 1017.2.[^7]

### 1.5 Emergency Lighting Requirements (IBC Sections 1008, 1013)

Emergency lighting is required in all assembly occupancies requiring two or more exits:[^10][^11]

- **Normal occupancy illumination:** Minimum **1 foot-candle (11 lux)** at the walking surface along the entire means of egress
- **Stairways in use:** Minimum **10 foot-candles (108 lux)** at the walking surface
- **Emergency power duration:** Minimum **90 minutes** continuous operation upon normal power failure
- **Emergency power activation:** Automatic upon primary power supply failure; must illuminate all interior exit access stairways, ramps, exit corridors, exit discharge, and specified rooms (electrical rooms, fire command centers, fire pump rooms, generator rooms, and public restrooms >300 sq ft)

**Assembly Exception (IBC Section 1008.2):** For auditoriums, theatres, concert and opera halls, illumination at the walking surface may be reduced during performances to not less than **0.2 foot-candle (2.15 lux)**, provided the required level is automatically restored upon activation of the fire alarm system.[^12]

Exit signs must meet IBC Section 1013 and be internally or externally illuminated; tactile exit signs with braille are required in accessible egress paths.

## 2. ADA and AODA Accessibility Requirements

### 2.1 ADA Accessible Seating (United States)

The Americans with Disabilities Act (ADA), through the 2010 ADA Standards (Section 221 and Section 802), establishes the following for assembly venues:[^13][^14]

- **Minimum wheelchair spaces:** At least **1% of total seating capacity**, with a minimum of 6 wheelchair spaces
- **Companion seating:** One companion seat required immediately adjacent to each wheelchair space
- **Dispersed seating:** When more than 300 total seats are provided, wheelchair locations must be dispersed throughout all seating areas, offering a **choice of admission prices and views** comparable to the general public
- **Lines of sight:** Must be comparable to those provided to other spectators; in venues where audiences stand during performances, wheelchair users must have sightlines over standing spectators
- **Wheelchair space dimensions (Section 802):** Single space = minimum 36 inches wide; side-by-side spaces = minimum 33 inches wide each
- **Assistive listening systems:** Receivers equal to **4% of total seats** must be available and signed
- **Parking:** Accessible spaces (96 inches wide) with signed access aisles at same grade as space
- **Accessible routes:** Must connect parking, accessible entrances, concessions, restrooms, and all seating areas to the stage/performance area
- **Elevators (Section 407):** Minimum 68 inches wide × 51 inches deep; accessible panels with tactile characters and braille required

For stadiums, arenas, and grandstands specifically, wheelchair spaces and companion seats must be **dispersed to all levels** that include seating served by an accessible route.[^13]

### 2.2 AODA (Ontario, Canada)

The *Accessibility for Ontarians with Disabilities Act, 2005* (AODA) and its **Integrated Accessibility Standards Regulation (IASR), O. Reg. 191/11**, apply to all organizations serving the public in Ontario, including assembly venues. Key requirements for venues:[^15][^16]

- **Design of Public Spaces Standard (DOPS):** Governs exterior public spaces — accessible parking, walkways, rest areas, and outdoor public eating areas. A comprehensive 2024 review of DOPS standards was initiated by a Standards Development Committee, with final recommendations submitted in early 2025[^15]
- **Customer Service Standard:** Venues must accommodate assistive devices, service animals, and support persons; staff training on AODA is mandatory
- **Information and Communications Standard:** Event materials, signage, and websites must be accessible (WCAG 2.0 Level AA for websites)
- **Built environment:** The Ontario Building Code (OBC) and AODA govern different spaces — OBC governs interior barrier-free design; AODA's DOPS governs exterior public spaces[^17]
- **CSA B651:** While not enforced in Ontario, this standard (Accessible Design for the Built Environment) is considered best practice and is referenced by the National Building Code of Canada[^17]

## 3. HVAC and Indoor Air Quality Standards

### 3.1 ASHRAE 62.1-2022: Ventilation for Acceptable Indoor Air Quality

ANSI/ASHRAE Standard 62.1-2022 is the foundational ventilation standard for commercial assembly buildings, incorporated by reference in the International Mechanical Code (IMC) and required as a prerequisite for LEED certification. The standard provides three compliance paths:[^18]

1. **Ventilation Rate Procedure (VRP):** Prescriptive — outdoor airflow = (people rate × occupant density) + (area rate × floor area), using Table 6-1 rates specific to each occupancy type
2. **Indoor Air Quality Procedure (IAQP):** Performance-based — demonstrate contaminant concentrations remain below specified limits
3. **Natural Ventilation Procedure:** For naturally ventilated spaces meeting geometric and wind criteria

Assembly-specific ventilation rates (ASHRAE 62.1-2022, Table 6-1) typically specify:
- Sports arena floor areas: ~20 CFM/person plus area component
- Spectator areas: ~7.5 CFM/person plus 0.06 CFM/sq ft
- Conference/meeting rooms: 5 CFM/person plus 0.06 CFM/sq ft

**Demand-Controlled Ventilation (DCV):** Addendum ab to ASHRAE 62.1-2022 permits DCV systems to modulate outdoor airflow based on real-time occupancy (using CO₂ sensors as a proxy), but outdoor airflow cannot fall below the area-based component when spaces are unoccupied. DCV is required in California (CBC Section 120.1) for spaces with design occupant densities ≥ 25 persons per 1,000 sq ft served by HVAC systems meeting capacity thresholds.[^19][^20]

### 3.2 CO₂ Monitoring

CO₂ monitoring is the dominant post-COVID verification tool for ventilation adequacy in assembly venues:[^21]

- **Baseline thresholds:** Levels below **600 ppm** indicate excellent ventilation; levels above **1,000–1,100 ppm** above outdoor air concentrations indicate inadequate ventilation and should trigger corrective action[^22]
- **Health Canada guidance:** Recommends CO₂ monitoring as a ventilation proxy for occupied public buildings; emphasizes NDIR (non-dispersive infrared) sensors as the preferred measurement technology[^23]
- **Belgium mandate:** Belgium became the first country to mandate CO₂ measurement in all closed public spaces following COVID-19; the broader scientific consensus increasingly supports regulatory CO₂ monitoring requirements[^21]
- **U.S. status:** No federal mandate for continuous CO₂ monitoring in assembly venues as of April 2026, though several jurisdictions (New York City, certain school districts) have enacted requirements; ASHRAE Standard 241 indirectly incentivizes monitoring through the Building Readiness Plan process

### 3.3 ASHRAE Standard 241-2023: Control of Infectious Aerosols

Published in June 2023, ASHRAE Standard 241 is a landmark post-COVID standard establishing minimum requirements for reducing infectious aerosol transmission risk in occupied buildings. Key provisions:[^24][^25]

- **Infection Risk Management Mode (IRMM):** An operating mode triggered during periods of elevated disease risk (declared by the Authority Having Jurisdiction or at owner/operator discretion, e.g., during influenza season). Standard 241 requirements apply specifically during IRMM
- **Equivalent Clean Airflow (ECAi):** The standard sets minimum equivalent clean airflow per person — combining outdoor air, filtration, and air cleaning (including UV-C germicidal irradiation) — to substantially reduce long-range transmission risk. This "pathogen-free equivalent" approach allows flexible compliance strategies
- **Building Readiness Plan:** Facilities must develop a documented Building Readiness Plan assessing existing ventilation performance, identifying IRMM strategies, and establishing maintenance schedules[^26]
- **Current status:** Standard 241 is currently a voluntary standard of care (not yet adopted into model codes as of April 2026), but it is increasingly cited in building contracts and insurance requirements

### 3.4 Filtration Standards Post-COVID

- ASHRAE recommends **MERV-13** filters (minimum) for high-occupancy public buildings to capture the range of aerosol particle sizes relevant to respiratory disease transmission[^27]
- The 2025 edition of ASHRAE 62.2 (residential) increased minimum filtration from MERV-6 to **MERV-11**, signaling directional movement in the commercial standard as well[^28]
- ASHRAE Standard 52.2 governs filter testing methodology and provides MERV ratings from 1–16 for mechanical filters, plus MERV-A for electrostatically charged filter media

### 3.5 ASHRAE 90.1-2022: Energy Standard

ANSI/ASHRAE/IES Standard 90.1-2022 is the energy efficiency baseline for commercial buildings, including large assembly venues. The 2022 edition (incorporating 80+ addenda over the 2019 edition) introduced:[^29]

- **First mandatory on-site renewable energy requirement** in a U.S. model energy standard (Section 11)
- New thermal bridging requirements for building envelopes
- Mechanical System Performance Path with Total System Performance Ratio (TSPR) metric for HVAC
- Significant efficiency increases for commercial rooftop units

ASHRAE 90.1 is the referenced energy standard for LEED v4.1 BD+C (baseline: ASHRAE 90.1-2016; enhanced compliance using 90.1-2019 or 2022 earns additional credits).[^30]

## 4. LEED Certification for Existing Venue Buildings

### 4.1 Applicable Rating Systems

For existing convention centres and arenas, the primary pathway is **LEED v4.1 Operations and Maintenance (O+M)**. For new construction or major renovation, **LEED v4.1 Building Design and Construction (BD+C)** applies.[^31][^32]

**LEED v4.1 O+M Structure:**
- **7 Mandatory Prerequisites** (policy-based best practices)
- **5 Performance Prerequisites** (minimum scores in each of 5 categories — must score ≥ 40/100 in each)
- **90 Performance Points** (automatically awarded by comparing building performance against global peers via the Arc platform)
- **10 Optional Strategy Credits** (up to 10 additional points for LEED-specific strategies)
- **Certification Levels:** Certified (40–49 total), Silver (50–59), Gold (60–79), Platinum (80+)[^33][^31]

**Five Performance Categories (and their relative weight):**

| Category | Key Metrics | Typical Venue Focus Areas |
|----------|-------------|---------------------------|
| **Energy** | 12 consecutive months of energy use | LED upgrades, HVAC optimization, renewable energy |
| **Water** | 12 months of water metering | Cooling tower efficiency, irrigation, low-flow fixtures |
| **Waste** | 12 months of waste diversion data | Zero-waste operations, composting programs |
| **Transportation** | Occupant/visitor transportation survey | Transit access, EV charging, bike infrastructure |
| **Indoor Environmental Quality (IEQ)** | TVOC/CO₂ measurements + occupant satisfaction survey | Ventilation upgrades, thermal comfort, daylighting |

### 4.2 Key Credits Applicable to Convention Centres and Arenas

Under LEED v4.1 O+M, points are primarily performance-driven, but the following strategy credits are particularly relevant to large assembly venues:[^34][^35]

- **EA Credit: Optimize Energy Performance** — Demonstrates energy performance improvement versus ASHRAE 90.1 baseline
- **EA Credit: Renewable Energy** — On-site solar/wind generation or renewable energy certificate procurement; Mercedes-Benz Stadium's 4,000-panel solar array is an exemplar[^36]
- **WE Credit: Outdoor Water Use Reduction** — Critical for venues with large green space or artificial turf with irrigation
- **MR Prerequisite: Ongoing Purchasing** — Sustainable purchasing policy for food/beverages and cleaning products
- **MR Credit: Solid Waste Management** — Points scale with waste diversion rate; TRUE certification is complementary
- **SS Credit: Site Management** — Integrated pest management, no mercury-containing pesticides
- **EQ Credit: Occupant Comfort Survey** — Surveying event staff on thermal, acoustic, and air quality conditions

For new construction (BD+C), **Rainwater Management** (reducing or eliminating stormwater runoff through green infrastructure), **Light Pollution Reduction**, and **Heat Island Reduction** are relevant site credits.[^37]

### 4.3 Certification Cost and Timeline

**Registration and Certification Fees (USGBC/CAGBC):**
- Fees are tiered by building gross floor area and LEED member status, ranging from a minimum of approximately $2,900 to over $1 million for very large projects[^38]
- Additional "soft" costs — consulting, commissioning, documentation — typically range from **$3–$5 per square foot** and may add 1%–6% to total project cost[^38]
- For existing buildings, LEED v4.1 O+M is significantly less expensive because performance data replaces prescriptive documentation; one estimate places it at **$1–$4 per sq ft** (BOMA BEST comparable)[^39]

**Timeline:**
- Standard GBCI certification review: **20–25 business days** after submission
- Expedited review (additional fee): **10–12 business days**[^40]
- Total project timeline for O+M (data collection, arc platform setup, documentation): typically **12–24 months** from initiation, driven primarily by the 12-month continuous performance data requirement
- LEED certification is valid for **3 years**, with annual data reporting required to maintain active status[^41]

### 5.2 ASTM/APEX Standards for Sustainable Events (E2700 Series)

The APEX/ASTM Environmentally Sustainable Event Standards are a collection of **9 independent standards** developed collaboratively by the Events Industry Council (then CIC) and ASTM International, championed by the EPA and the Green Meeting Industry Council:[^42]

- **9 Standards:** Accommodations, Audio-Visual, Communication & Marketing Material, Destinations, Exhibits, Food and Beverage, **Meeting Venue**, On-Site Office, Transportation
- **8 Impact Areas per Standard:** Staff Management and Environmental Policy, Communications, Waste, Energy, Air Quality, Water, Procurement, Community Partners
- **4 Performance Levels:** 1 (entry), 2, 3, 4 (exemplary)
- **Certification Options:** 1st-party (self-declaration), 2nd-party (client verification), 3rd-party (independent audit)
- The **Meeting Venue Standard** is the most directly applicable to convention centres and arenas, addressing venue-level energy management, waste diversion, water conservation, and indoor air quality

### 5.3 EIC Sustainable Event Standards

The Events Industry Council's revised Sustainable Event Standards (updated 2022, with 2024 revisions in review) operate as a **points-based system** with eight sector-specific standards:[^50][^48][^49]

- **Standards:** Event Organizer, Accommodation, AV/Production, Destination, Exhibition Services, Food & Beverage, Venue, and a new **Integrated Property** standard (combining Accommodation + Venue + F&B for full-service properties)
- **Certification Levels:** Foundations (education only) → Bronze (50% of points) → Silver (65%) → Gold (80%) → Platinum (90%)
- **Assessment Categories (9):** Organizational management; marketing, communications and engagement; climate action; water management; materials and circularity; supply chain management; DEI; accessibility; and social impact
- Applicants can earn bonus points for innovation or exemplary performance to reach higher levels

**Real-world example:** WEFTEC 2024 (20,000+ attendees, Ernest N. Morial Convention Center, New Orleans) achieved EIC **Platinum** certification — progressing from Gold in 2023 to Platinum in 2024.[^51]

### 5.4 Green Sports Alliance and Play to Zero

The Green Sports Alliance (GSA) is a membership organization rather than a certifying body per se; its **Play to Zero** program recognizes annual performance achievements through awards rather than tiered certification:[^43][^45]

- **Play to Zero Award Categories:** Energy Champion (100% renewable energy or offsets), Waste Leader (50%+ landfill diversion), Water Steward, and Decarbonization Leader
- **2024 awardee example:** Allegiant Stadium (Las Vegas Raiders) received the **Energy Champion Award** (100% renewable energy/offsets) and the **Waste Leader Award** (50%+ landfill diversion)[^45]
- The GSA also publishes playbooks (Reuse Playbook, Energy Decarbonization Playbook) and hosted its 14th Annual Summit in 2024[^52]
- ISO 20121 is increasingly being required by GSA-member venues preparing for major events (e.g., SoFi Stadium adopted ISO 20121 for FIFA World Cup 2026 and 2028 Olympics)[^43]

## 7. Environmental Compliance for Large Venues

### 7.1 Stormwater Management

Large venues are subject to federal stormwater regulations under the **Clean Water Act National Pollutant Discharge Elimination System (NPDES)** permit program:[^62]

- **Coverage trigger:** Any project disturbing **≥ 1 acre** (or smaller projects within a larger common plan of development) must obtain NPDES Construction General Permit (CGP) coverage and implement a Stormwater Pollution Prevention Plan (SWPPP)
- **Post-Construction:** Venues must implement Best Management Practices (BMPs) — structural (detention/retention ponds, bioswales, permeable pavement, sand filters) and non-structural (good housekeeping, employee training) — to control post-construction runoff
- **2025 Updates:** EPA modified the 2022 CGP on April 8, 2025, expanding coverage to all Lands of Exclusive Federal Jurisdiction; the proposed 2026 Multi-Sector General Permit (MSGP) to replace the expiring MSGP (February 28, 2026) was also released[^63]
- **Innovative examples:** Mercedes-Benz Stadium installed a 680,000-gallon rainwater harvesting cistern to capture roof runoff for field irrigation and building cooling[^64]

### 7.2 Noise Ordinances

Noise regulation for large venues is primarily a **local/municipal matter**, and venues frequently require special exemption permits for amplified events:[^65]

- **Typical structure:** Municipal bylaws establish daytime and nighttime dB(A) limits at adjacent property lines; amplified outdoor events typically require advance permit applications
- **Calgary example (relevant for Alberta venues):** Community Standards Bylaw 32M2023 caps outdoor noise; major events (≥5,000 capacity) must apply for a noise exemption permit. The city is currently proposing a bylaw amendment to allow up to **64 dB** outside Scotia Place (the new downtown arena) until midnight for 5–10 concerts annually, up from the 50 dB/10pm current standard[^66][^67]
- ASHRAE 55 and ISO standards govern interior acoustic comfort (HVAC noise); large HVAC systems serving arenas require specific attention to HVAC-generated noise in occupied seating areas

### 7.3 Light Pollution Regulations

Outdoor lighting for venue parking, signage, and event programming is increasingly regulated through municipal adoptions of the **IDA-IES Model Lighting Ordinance (MLO)**:[^68][^69]

- **Lighting Zones (LZ0–LZ4):** Municipalities assign zones from LZ0 (no ambient lighting, e.g., natural areas) to LZ4 (high-intensity commercial). Most large urban venue campuses fall in LZ3 or LZ4
- **Key MLO requirements:** Full-cutoff or shielded fixtures to minimize uplight and light trespass; automatic lighting reduction of ≥ 30% after curfew hours; color temperature guidance (warm-white LED ≤ 3,500K for outdoor general lighting recommended)
- **LEED connection:** LEED BD+C includes a **Light Pollution Reduction credit** (Climate Pledge Arena did not earn this credit in its LEED scorecard)[^37]
- Venue scoreboards, tower lighting, and event signage often require special municipal permits when they exceed zone lighting power allowances

### 7.4 Refrigerant Management (EPA AIM Act)

The **American Innovation and Manufacturing (AIM) Act of 2020** mandated an 85% phasedown of HFC refrigerants by 2036 through a schedule that is now actively reshaping venue HVAC compliance:[^70]

- **January 1, 2025:** New HVAC equipment using **R-410A** (dominant in commercial systems for 20 years) can no longer be manufactured or imported. New installations must use approved low-GWP alternatives (R-454B, R-32, R-466A)[^70]
- **January 1, 2026:** EPA's **HFC Leak Repair and Management Rule** came into effect, imposing mandatory leak detection and repair on any refrigerant-containing appliance with a **full charge of 15 pounds or greater** of HFC or substitute with GWP > 53. This expands scope significantly from the prior 50-lb threshold for ODS systems[^71]
- **All technicians** purchasing, handling, or recovering refrigerants from HVAC systems must hold current **EPA Section 608 certification**[^70]
- **Cost impact:** R-410A service refrigerant is projected to increase **3–4× in price by 2028** as supply is restricted; venues with large legacy R-410A chiller plants face significant service cost escalation by 2029[^70]
- The 2024 IBC, IFC, and IMC were amended to permit new **A2L refrigerants** (mildly flammable, low-GWP alternatives) for human comfort applications[^72]

### 7.5 Hazardous Materials: Cleaning Chemicals

Venue cleaning operations must comply with:

- **OSHA Hazard Communication Standard (HazCom, 29 CFR 1910.1200):** Safety Data Sheets (SDS) and worker training for all chemical products
- **EPA Safer Choice Program:** Voluntary certification for cleaning products (relevant for venues pursuing LEED MR credits for sustainable purchasing)
- **LEED MR Prerequisite:** Under LEED v4.1 O+M, venues must maintain a **sustainable purchasing policy** covering cleaning products, with preference for EPA Safer Choice, Green Seal GS-37, or UL ECOLOGO-certified products

## 8. Energy Benchmarking and Reporting Mandates

### 8.1 ENERGY STAR Portfolio Manager

**EPA ENERGY STAR Portfolio Manager** is the universal benchmarking tool for North American commercial buildings, serving as both the U.S. national standard and Canada's national benchmarking tool:[^73][^74]

- **Coverage:** All property types are eligible to benchmark; the 1–100 ENERGY STAR score is available for eligible property types (scores account for weather normalization and operational characteristics)
- **ENERGY STAR Certification:** Buildings scoring **75 or higher** are eligible to apply for the ENERGY STAR label, indicating top 25% energy performance in their peer group[^75]
- Nearly **25% of U.S. commercial building space** is actively benchmarked in Portfolio Manager[^74]
- Convention centres and stadiums can benchmark using the "Other/Specialty" or "Stadium" property type categories
- **Ontario (Canada):** All privately owned commercial, industrial, and multi-residential buildings **≥ 50,000 sq ft** must benchmark annually using ENERGY STAR Portfolio Manager under Ontario's Energy and Water Reporting and Benchmarking (EWRB) initiative; the City of Toronto supplements provincial requirements with additional enforcement through Municipal Code Chapter 367[^76]

### 8.2 Municipal Benchmarking Laws (U.S.)

The following jurisdictions have benchmarking requirements most likely to affect large venue operators:[^77][^78]

### 8.3 Carbon Disclosure Requirements

**SEC Climate Disclosure Rule (March 6, 2024):** Applies to publicly traded companies (including REITs and publicly traded venue operators/teams):[^79][^80]

- **Large Accelerated Filers:** Begin disclosing material Scope 1 and Scope 2 GHG emissions for fiscal year 2025 (first reports in 2026). Scope 3 disclosure was eliminated from the final rule
- **Required disclosures:** Climate-related risks with material financial impact; governance and oversight structures; risk management processes; material climate targets and goals; aggregate financial impacts of severe weather events
- **Assurance:** Limited assurance on GHG emissions phased in beginning FY 2026 for large accelerated filers; reasonable assurance by FY 2033
- **International venues:** Canada's proposed mandatory climate disclosure framework (aligned with ISSB IFRS S1/S2) is in advanced development; Alberta has its own **Carbon Levy** and reporting requirements under the Technology Innovation and Emissions Reduction (TIER) Regulation for facilities emitting ≥ 100,000 tonnes CO₂e/year
