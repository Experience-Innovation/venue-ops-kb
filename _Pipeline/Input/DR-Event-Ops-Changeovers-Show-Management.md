# Event Operations, Changeovers, and Show Management in Large Venues (April 2026)
## Executive summary
Large multi-use venues – convention centres, arenas, stadiums, performing arts centres, theme parks, fairgrounds, and amphitheatres – rely on tightly integrated scheduling, changeover logistics, event‑day control, exhibitor services, settlements, client servicing, loading dock operations, and production services to run profitably and safely. Modern operations are increasingly orchestrated through specialised venue‑management platforms (e.g., Momentus/Ungerboeck, VenueOps, Priava, iVvy) combined with incident‑management and command‑centre software (e.g., 24/7 Software, Raven, WeTrack), with unionised labour (IATSE, Teamsters) and general service contractors (Freeman, GES, Shepard) providing much of the physical execution. Across venue types, the operational fundamentals are similar – accurate long‑range booking, efficient changeovers, disciplined “run of show,” clean financial settlement, and rigorous dock/material‑handling control – but the tempo, labour mix, and revenue model differ significantly between convention‑driven, ticketed‑entertainment, and park/fairground environments.[^1][^2][^3][^4][^5][^6][^7][^8][^9]

## 1. Event scheduling and calendar management
### 1.1 Core concepts and multi‑use complexity
Professional venue management frameworks (e.g., IAVM Venue Professional and Executive Competency Standards) treat booking and scheduling policies as core competencies, emphasising clear guidelines, utilisation optimisation, and integration with staffing and financial planning. Multi‑use facilities (e.g., campus arenas, civic centres) must balance recurring tenants (sports teams, resident arts organisations) with one‑off concerts, conferences, and community events, often under university or municipal policies that reserve “sacred” dates for priority stakeholders. Long‑range league schedules, touring‑cycle offers, and convention bid cycles all intersect, creating conflicts that are typically governed by written booking priorities and hold/confirm rules (e.g., sports and city‑wide conventions at top priority, then major concerts, then smaller rentals).[^10][^11][^4][^6]
### 1.2 Hold / tentative / definite workflows
Industry practice distinguishes between holds (tentative reservations) and definite (confirmed) bookings; training material and venue‑management literature describe holds as non‑binding indications of interest that allow venues to stack multiple potential events against a date until a contract is executed. Momentus (formerly Ungerboeck) explicitly supports multi‑level holds and venue‑specific “booking capability” permissions so some users may only place tentative holds while others can confirm dates, reflecting internal booking authority hierarchies. Recent Momentus product updates also focus on conflict‑checking logic that allows back‑to‑back bookings and contiguous events, reflecting demand for maximising dark‑time utilisation while still protecting changeover windows.[^12][^13][^14][^15][^16]
### 1.3 Platforms and technology
Momentus Enterprise and Elite (formerly Ungerboeck/EBSM) serve convention and exhibition centres, stadiums, arenas, performing arts centres, and campuses with modules for venue booking, CRM, catering, exhibitor ordering, floor‑planning, accounting, and reporting, giving a single view of past, current, and future events. Momentus OneView adds a cross‑venue calendar layer that aggregates availability and confirmed/tentative events across multi‑site portfolios, with promoter‑friendly views that show open dates without exposing confidential event details. Competing SaaS tools such as iVvy, Priava, and VenueOps provide similar capabilities – live availability, cloud‑based calendars, contract and payment workflows, and resource tracking – targeted especially at hotels, convention venues, and mid‑sized event facilities. For day‑of incident logging and safety operations, venues increasingly integrate with platforms like 24/7 Software and WeTrack to centralise incident reports, work orders, and operational logs feeding the main operations centre.[^17][^1][^18][^19][^20][^5][^21][^22][^8][^23]
### 1.4 Long‑range vs. short‑range scheduling and booking windows
Convention centres and city‑wide conferences typically operate on long lead times, with IACC and PCMA case material showing site visits and exhibitor site tours 9–10 months or more before an event and multi‑year bid cycles for major association conventions. Stadium and arena tours book on cycles driven by league schedules and tour routing; sports season calendars can lock prime dates months in advance while concerts and family shows are layered around home stands and playoff contingencies. Performing arts centres often book subscription seasons a year or more in advance, but leave some holds for commercial rentals or short‑notice programming, while theme parks and fairgrounds focus less on date conflicts and more on seasonal operating calendars and overlay events (festivals, Halloween nights), which are reflected in public daily calendars published by Disney, SeaWorld, and other parks.[^10][^24][^25][^26][^27][^6][^28]
### 1.5 Event mix strategy and utilisation
IAVM’s performance reporting frameworks emphasise tracking event days, utilisation, and revenue contributions by segment (tenant sports, touring shows, conferences, community events) so venue executives can balance mission obligations with revenue optimisation. Pollstar/VenuesNow analysis indicates that while stadiums and large arenas have seen strong revenue growth post‑pandemic, smaller theatres and clubs face margin pressures, encouraging multi‑purpose venues to diversify event mix (sports, concerts, e‑sports, non‑traditional events) and maximise per‑event yield. IACC’s Meeting Room of the Future research and MPI commentary highlight a shift toward “experience‑led planning” and non‑traditional venues (museums, stadiums, vineyards), which pushes large venues to make their spaces more flexible and changeover‑friendly to capture corporate and experiential business between anchor events.[^6][^29][^30]

## 2. Changeover operations
### 2.1 Typical conversions by venue type
Arenas regularly convert between ice hockey, basketball, concerts, family shows, and sometimes flat‑floor expos, typically covering ice with insulated decking, removing or retracting dasher boards, deploying basketball floor sections, and reconfiguring telescopic seating. Stadiums convert between sports (e.g., NFL vs. soccer vs. baseball) and large‑scale concerts or motorsports by installing/removing field protection systems (Terraplas), stages, and floor seating, with documented examples such as U.S. Bank Stadium flipping from a concert to an NFL game in just over 24 hours. Convention centres transform exhibit halls into banquets or general sessions by re‑carpeting, re‑setting pipe‑and‑drape, relocating booths, and deploying staging and seating; performing arts centres change from bare stage to orchestra shell to theatrical sets, sometimes with same‑day school shows and evening performances.[^2][^36][^4][^7][^33][^37][^34]
### 2.2 Crew structure, staffing models, and unions
Hussey Seating and arena operations coverage describe dedicated “conversion crews” of around 40 technicians as typical for standard ice‑to‑court changeovers, with up to 60 workers deployed to compress changeovers to under two hours in time‑critical situations. Time‑lapse and arena case studies (e.g., Wells Fargo Center, Ball Arena, Little Caesars Arena) show crews of roughly 25–50 workers completing ice‑to‑basketball conversions in 2.5–3 hours overnight between games, often organised into teams by task (dasher removal, deck install, floor panels, seating moves, cleaning). In North America, much of this labour is supplied by IATSE stagehand locals and other building‑trades or labourers’ unions; IATSE represents over 168,000 technicians, artisans, and craftspeople across theatre, live events, and trade shows, and is actively organising backstage and production workers at arenas and regional theatres.[^38][^39][^40][^33][^41][^37][^42]
### 2.3 Conversion timelines and benchmarks
For NHL/NBA arenas, industry examples indicate standard ice‑to‑basketball conversions in about three hours with a 40‑person crew, with high‑efficiency operations achieving 90‑minute completions during playoff overlaps and other tight turnarounds. Stadium conversions from full concert mode (stage, field seats, floor covering) back to NFL game readiness are typically executed in just over 24 hours, with time‑lapse examples at London Stadium and U.S. Bank Stadium showing removal of seating, retraction of lower bowls, and turf restoration over one to three days depending on complexity. Convention‑to‑banquet or general‑session turns often occur overnight – for example, exhibitor move‑out completing in the evening, followed by overnight cleaning, carpet change, and banquet set to be ready for morning keynotes or luncheons – though detailed public benchmarks are less commonly published and are typically encoded in internal production schedules.[^36][^35][^4][^33][^37][^43][^9]
### 2.4 Equipment and rigging for changeovers
Common arena conversion equipment includes ice deck systems, modular basketball court panels, telescopic seating systems, forklifts and pallet jacks, scissor lifts, and overhead rigging grids with chain hoists for scoreboards, trusses, and banners. Stadium conversions rely heavily on field‑protection tiles (e.g., Terraplas), modular stages, large‑format rigging trusses, cranes or high‑capacity chain hoists, and extensive forklift fleets for floor chairs and barricades, with seating retraction systems enabling multi‑configuration lower bowls. Convention centres and fairgrounds use carpet‑laying machines, pipe‑and‑drape hardware, standard and rough‑terrain forklifts, scissor lifts for banners and signage, and occasionally in‑hall rigging grids; general service contractors typically provide much of this gear as part of material‑handling and decorating contracts.[^2][^44][^45][^7][^33][^37][^43]
### 2.5 Notable changeover achievements
Industry media regularly highlight high‑speed conversions as proof points of venue capability: Hussey Seating cites conversions completed in as little as 90 minutes in elite arenas during playoff overlaps, and multiple time‑lapse videos show full ice‑to‑court transformations between evening and next‑morning events. London Stadium’s transformation videos showcase its ability to host baseball, athletics, monster trucks, concerts, and Premier League football in rapid succession, enabled by retractable seating and modular field systems. These examples underscore that well‑trained crews, optimised workflows, and purpose‑built infrastructure can compress changeover durations dramatically, but also increase safety risks if not managed with strong protocols and supervision.[^35][^33][^46][^43][^9]

## 3. Event‑day operations and show management
### 3.1 Run‑of‑show (ROS) and event timelines
Professional event‑production guides describe the run‑of‑show as the master timeline for an event, listing every segment with start/end times, duration, presenters, and technical cues (audio, lighting, video), and often including buffers of 2–5 minutes between major segments to absorb minor delays. Corporate and entertainment event checklists stress that detailed ROS documents should cover from initial load‑in and rehearsal through the main program and load‑out, with clear assignment of responsibilities, contingency plans (e.g., what gets cut if a speaker overruns), and clear show‑calling language for cueing. Modern tools like Stagetimer and BasicOps support time‑coded ROS documentation and real‑time adjustments, but many venues still use spreadsheets integrated with radio and comms systems.[^47][^48][^49][^50]
### 3.2 Venue operations command centres
Major stadiums, arenas, festivals, and multi‑venue events increasingly run from a central Main Operations Centre (MOC) or tech command centre, acting as the “nerve hub” for event‑time operations. These centres aggregate inputs from CCTV, access control, ticketing, crowd‑density sensors, radio channels, and incident‑management platforms to coordinate multi‑team responses (security, operations, medical, vendors, external agencies) and maintain a single log of incidents and decisions. Case studies such as Qatar’s Aspire Command Center for the 2022 FIFA World Cup demonstrate large‑scale integrations of tens of thousands of cameras and data feeds to manage crowd flow and safety across multiple stadiums, a model that has influenced command‑centre designs for large sports and entertainment venues.[^51][^21][^8]
### 3.3 Communication systems and radio protocols
Two‑way radios remain the backbone of event‑day communication, with etiquette guides and radio‑protocol resources emphasising clear, concise language, waiting for channels to clear, and the use of standard phrases (e.g., “stand‑by,” “go ahead,” “say again,” “over,” “out”) and phonetic alphabets to minimise miscommunication. Modern venues increasingly deploy digital trunked radio systems (e.g., DMR, P25, TETRA) that allow multiple talk‑groups on limited spectrum, support data messaging and GPS tracking, and integrate with incident‑management platforms for logging. Best‑practice guidance stresses training all staff on etiquette and emergency codes, allocating dedicated channels for security, medical, engineering, and housekeeping, and capturing radio traffic related to incidents in central logs.[^52][^53][^54][^55]
### 3.4 Event manager and show management roles
Event managers are responsible for overall planning, budgeting, vendor coordination, and timeline oversight, typically being the first on site and last to leave, supervising setup, monitoring programme execution, and leading post‑event evaluation. Venue‑side event or operations managers focus on integrating client needs with venue policies and constraints, ensuring adherence to safety and emergency procedures, staffing plans, and financial controls, as reflected in IAVM competency standards for monitoring event operations and reviewing bookings, contracts, and activity logs. Client‑side show managers, tour managers, or production managers control the artistic and technical aspects of performances, coordinate with the house production office, and lead load‑in to load‑out sequences in line with technical riders and ROS.[^56][^57][^4][^58][^6]
### 3.5 Pre‑event briefings and post‑event debriefs
Production checklists recommend final team briefings 60–90 minutes before doors open, gathering all key departments (audio, video, lighting, stage, security, front‑of‑house, catering) to review the ROS, highlight critical cues, and reinforce emergency procedures. Post‑event, emerging best practice is a “hot debrief” immediately after the show or the next morning to capture urgent issues, followed by a more structured debrief 2–7 days later once data (ticket scans, sales, incidents) is compiled, with agendas covering what worked, what failed, and action items for improvement. Ticketing and venue‑operations content emphasises maintaining timestamped logs of issues and decisions to support learning and, where necessary, legal defensibility.[^49][^59][^60][^23]
### 3.6 Event‑day workflows: load‑in to load‑out
Across most large‑venue event types, the operational sequence runs: pre‑arrival planning and ROS finalisation; load‑in (docks, staging, rigging, booth or set build); rehearsals and sound checks; guest arrival and event execution; strike and load‑out; post‑event cleaning and reset. Incident‑management best practices for large venues recommend real‑time reporting and dispatch of issues (e.g., spills, security incidents, medical calls) via mobile apps and radios, centralised in software like 24/7 Software so supervisors can spot patterns and allocate resources efficiently. Venues that operate 24/7 (e.g., major stadiums with tours, team operations, and events) emphasise continuous readiness, with night shifts handling deep cleaning, maintenance, and setup for the next event day.[^49][^61][^8][^23][^62]

## 4. Exhibitor services in convention centres
### 4.1 General service contractors (decorators) ecosystem
General service contractors (GSCs) such as Freeman, GES, and similar firms act as the primary decorators and logistics providers for many trade shows, handling installation and dismantling of booths, material handling/drayage, hanging signs and rigging, freight management, and rental of exhibits, carpet, furniture, and sometimes AV. Freeman notes that GSC responsibilities often include generating the Exhibitor Services Manual (ESM), overseeing labour, managing loading docks and storage, and being the exclusive provider for services like material handling, cleaning, and rigging at many shows. Exhibitor manuals from GES and others emphasise that while some services (e.g., electrical, internet, F&B) may be exclusive to the venue itself, GSCs handle most decorator functions, typically under a master contract with show management plus individual exhibitor orders.[^63][^7][^64][^65]
### 4.2 Drayage / material handling definitions and pricing
Drayage (material handling) is defined in exhibitor education resources and ESCA‑aligned glossaries as the movement of freight from the loading dock or advance warehouse to the exhibitor’s booth, storage of empties during the show, and return of materials to the dock for outbound shipping. GES explains that material handling and drayage are interchangeable terms covering unloading at dock or warehouse, transporting to booth, storing empties, returning empties, and reloading carriers, typically provided exclusively by the GSC for a fee. Pricing is usually based on hundredweight (CWT) – cost per 100 pounds – with typical U.S. rates ranging from roughly 60 to 160 currency units per CWT, with higher charges for special handling (loose items, late shipments, off‑target deliveries), as illustrated by exhibitor guides and explanatory articles.[^66][^67][^68][^69][^44][^70][^45]
### 4.3 Booth utility services (electrical, plumbing, internet, compressed air)
Exhibitor manuals note that electrical, telecommunications, and internet services are often exclusive to the facility or designated utility provider, requiring detailed floorplans showing outlet locations, load requirements, and hours of service. Standard offerings include single‑ and three‑phase power drops, dedicated lines for machinery, water and drain service, compressed air connections, and wired/wireless internet, with some venues offering advanced services such as dedicated bandwidth or VLANs for exhibitors. Venues often provide these services through in‑house utility departments or contracted specialists, and orders are channelled through the ESM or online ordering portals, sometimes integrated with venue‑management platforms like Momentus Exhibitor Ordering.[^7][^65][^71][^72]
### 4.4 Exhibitor services manuals and ordering systems
The Exhibitor Services Manual (or Exhibitor Kit) is the primary reference document for exhibitors, containing order forms, rules and regulations, deadlines, and information on exclusive and preferred contractors, and is usually produced by the GSC in collaboration with show management and the venue. GES’s Expresso and similar systems provide online ESM access and picture‑driven ordering interfaces, while Momentus Elite’s Exhibitor Ordering module allows venues to configure price schedules (advance vs. standard) and enable online ordering and payment via an exhibitor store, reducing manual handling of orders. PCMA guidance to organisers recommends benchmarking exhibitor costs (material handling, labour, internet, furnishings) across comparable shows and increasing transparency by making manuals and pricing easily accessible, reflecting exhibitor pressure to control rising costs.[^7][^64][^65][^28][^72]
### 4.5 Move‑in/move‑out, marshalling yards, and dock scheduling
Convention‑centre logistics depend on organised move‑in/move‑out schedules, often managed through marshalling yards that stage trucks before they are dispatched to limited dock doors based on show‑floor readiness. GES explains that marshalling yards track arrival order and dispatch trucks to the docks as doors become available, preventing congestion and improving safety; move‑in may take two to three days while move‑out is compressed and more prone to congestion and errors. Venues like McCormick Place require detailed dock‑door usage plans from contractors and provide dock/elevator schedule forms that must be completed and submitted ahead of events, while some shows (e.g., at Vancouver Convention Centre) use systems such as Voyage Control for online dock‑slot reservations to reduce waiting times.[^73][^74][^75][^76]
### 4.6 Labour rules for exhibition setup (IATSE, Teamsters, others)
Trade‑show union rules and jurisdiction documents show that Teamsters locals often have jurisdiction over freight handling (transit of exhibitor products to the show floor), while IATSE locals may control installation, dismantling, and rigging activities, depending on city and facility. Work‑rule changes at major venues like Chicago’s McCormick Place have historically been contentious, with litigation and negotiated settlements involving Teamsters and other unions over who may handle certain tasks and how exhibitor‑do‑it‑yourself allowances are defined. Exhibitors hiring Exhibitor Appointed Contractors (EACs) must usually file notice and insurance documentation, and must comply with union and facility rules, as outlined in GSC handbooks and manuals.[^77][^78][^79][^40][^65][^71]

## 5. Event settlement and financial reconciliation
### 5.1 Overview of settlement processes
Settlement is the process of reconciling all revenues and expenses associated with an event and distributing funds among the venue, promoter, artists, vendors, and other stakeholders. Ticketing‑system vendors and industry guides emphasise the need for comprehensive ticket sales reports (by ticket type, price, discounts, comps, refunds) and clear documentation of all expenses (venue rent, staffing, production, marketing) and revenues (ticket sales, facility fees, F&B, merchandise, parking) before meeting with partners for settlement. Festival and fair settlement practices stress daily reconciliations for vendors and bars so final payouts at event end become confirmation steps rather than complex calculations, reducing disputes.[^81][^56][^57][^82][^83]
### 5.2 Ticket accounting and manifest reconciliation
Concert settlement guides and ticketing resources describe reconciling box‑office statements and ticket manifests to confirm total tickets sold, revenue by price category, discounts applied, and numbers of complimentary or sponsor tickets issued. Platforms like ThunderTix and others offer settlement reports that aggregate sales, fees, and taxes and support multi‑event performance analysis, while venue agreements may specify when settlement must occur (e.g., within a defined number of business days after the event) and the basis for settlement (gross vs. net of taxes and facility fees). Transparency and agreement on audited attendance and revenue figures is crucial for subsequent royalty, performance‑rights, and tax reporting.[^84][^85][^82][^83][^86]
### 5.3 F&B and vendor settlement (caterers, concessions, festivals)
Venue and festival practice typically sees F&B operators paying commissions on gross sales to the venue or event organiser (e.g., 15–25 percent), sometimes combined with minimum guarantees or exclusive rights. Festival settlement guidance recommends laying out commission percentages clearly in vendor contracts, reconciling gross sales per vendor from POS or cashless systems, deducting agreed commissions and fees, and paying out net amounts quickly – ideally on the final night or within a short window – supported by clear written settlement statements. For contract caterers managing in‑house F&B in convention centres or arenas, management contracts often require daily reporting of sales and payroll figures to the facility and may tie management incentives to post‑event evaluations and budget performance.[^81][^87][^88][^89]
### 5.4 Artist and promoter settlement structures
Industry education (Pollstar, artist‑settlement blogs, promoter training) describes standard deal structures such as flat guarantees, guarantees versus percentage of net or gross box office receipts, promoter‑profit deals, and full co‑promotes. In a typical “guarantee versus percentage” deal, the artist receives either a guaranteed amount or a negotiated percentage of net box office receipts (NBOR) after taxes and certain expenses (e.g., facility fees), whichever is greater; for example, a 2,000 guarantee versus 65 percent of NBOR after 6.5 percent sales tax, with escalators at certain ticket‑sales thresholds. Promoter‑profit deals add a promoter profit margin (often 10–15 percent of eligible expenses) into the expense line before determining the “split point,” after which overages are split (e.g., 85/15 in favour of the artist), giving the promoter a built‑in profit plus a share of upside while artists participate strongly in over‑performance.[^90][^91][^57][^92][^93]
### 5.5 Settlement timelines and standards
While timelines vary by contract, festival settlement best practices recommend reconciling vendor and partner finances daily and completing final settlements as soon as possible after the event, often within the last festival day or a few days thereafter. University and theatre box‑office agreements sometimes allow the venue to control ticket funds and delay settlement with lessees until after reconciliations and any deductions (e.g., damages, additional labour) are finalised, with conditions documented in rental contracts. Venues with high event frequency often standardise internal settlement checklists and templates to streamline training and reduce risk of errors, and some leverage specialised settlement software or modules embedded in their venue‑management platforms.[^94][^81][^86][^89][^95]

## 6. Client services and event coordination
### 6.1 Venue‑side vs. client‑side roles
On the venue side, event coordinators or event managers act as primary liaisons to clients, translating contracts and event specifications into internal work orders, BEOs, and staffing plans, and coordinating with departments such as F&B, engineering, security, housekeeping, and ticketing. On the client side, corporate or association planners, tour managers, or production managers define event objectives, programme content, and technical requirements and work with venues to align expectations, risk management, and budgets. Clear delineation of responsibilities – often documented in Event Specification Guides (ESGs) and contracts – is essential to avoid gaps or duplication.[^96][^4][^58][^6]
### 6.2 Banquet Event Orders (BEOs)
Banquet Event Orders (BEOs), sometimes called Catering Event Orders (CEOs), are standardised operational documents in hotels and venues that detail every aspect of a catered event: event basics, guest counts, room layouts, AV needs, menus and dietary notes, service timing, pricing, and special instructions. BEOs act as both internal execution guides and legal references, coordinating kitchen, banquet service, bar, AV, and accounting teams, and are typically reviewed and signed by both client and venue to confirm final details and guarantees. Modern BEO workflows often run through cloud‑based event‑management systems (e.g., Cvent, Social Tables, Tripleseat), allowing real‑time edits, automatic time‑stamping, and distribution to vendor teams via mobile devices.[^97][^98][^99][^100]
### 6.3 Event Specification Guide (ESG) standards
The Convention Industry Council (now Events Industry Council) created the APEX Event Specifications Guide (ESG) template as a four‑part standard (narrative, function schedule, function sheets, room diagrams) to consolidate all key event information in a consistent format for venues and suppliers. ESG guidelines stress capturing detailed requirements for each function (room sets, F&B, AV, staging, security) and provide instructions for planners on how to complete templates so venues can interpret them reliably. ESG adoption underpins smoother pre‑event meetings and reduces miscommunication, especially for complex multi‑day conferences spread across multiple venues.[^101][^60][^96]
### 6.4 Pre‑event planning meetings, site visits, and relationship management
PCMA and MPI resources emphasise early site inspections (including virtual tours), joint planning meetings, and inclusive design discussions (accessibility, diversity, sustainability) as best practices when selecting and preparing venues. For major exhibitions, organisers sometimes hold exhibitor site visits 9–10 months before the show to help exhibitors plan hotels, branding, and budgets and to jointly explore cost‑control opportunities with key vendors. Venue‑client relationship management increasingly hinges on transparency about costs (e.g., material handling, labour, internet) and truthful attendance reporting, as well as post‑event follow‑up and support.[^102][^28][^103][^104][^30]
### 6.5 Post‑event surveys, NPS, and debriefs
Event managers commonly use attendee and client surveys (including Net Promoter Score) alongside internal debriefs to evaluate success and identify improvements, with feedback covering logistics, F&B quality, AV performance, staff professionalism, and value for money. TicketFairy and other event‑ops sources argue that combining quantitative data (ticket scans, sales, incident counts) with qualitative feedback in structured post‑event reviews helps venues refine staffing models, technology investments, and policies (e.g., curfew enforcement, entry screening) over time. In contracted catering relationships, some RFPs and management agreements explicitly tie caterer incentives or penalties to post‑event evaluation scores and budget adherence, reinforcing the importance of structured feedback loops.[^81][^87][^59][^58]

## 7. Loading dock and material‑handling operations
### 7.1 Dock scheduling and management systems
Major convention centres and exhibition organisers increasingly use dock‑scheduling and yard‑management tools to assign truck arrival times and dock doors in advance, reducing congestion and emissions. Solutions like Voyage Control (used at the Vancouver Convention Centre) and similar systems allow exhibitors and contractors to reserve dock time slots, while marshalling yards act as buffer zones where trucks check in, receive number cards, and wait to be dispatched to the venue according to readiness and door availability. Logistics best‑practice guidance emphasises clearly designated staging, unloading, and trailer‑parking zones, combined with RFID/GPS tracking and real‑time communication to keep freight flowing smoothly.[^105][^74][^75][^106]
### 7.2 Freight elevators, forklifts, and MHE
Exhibitor manuals typically require contractors to submit dock and service‑elevator usage plans and highlight constraints such as shared freight elevators and maximum load capacities. Material handling staff rely on fleets of forklifts, pallet jacks, tuggers, and dollies to move freight from docks to booths and back, with trained, certified operators responsible for safety. In multi‑level venues (e.g., urban convention centres, theatres), freight elevators become critical path resources that must be scheduled to avoid bottlenecks during peak move‑in and move‑out times.[^69][^44][^73]
### 7.3 Marshalling yards and move‑in/out sequencing
GES and logistics articles describe marshalling yards as centralised hubs that control truck access to limited dock doors, dispatching in arrival order and according to show‑floor readiness. Move‑in typically spreads over two to three days to allow gradual build‑out, whereas move‑out is compressed into fewer hours, increasing the risk of queueing, errors, and safety incidents if not well‑managed. Yard staff coordinate with dock marshals and show‑floor supervisors via radios and sometimes yard‑management software to adjust sequencing dynamically based on delays, late arrivals, or extended loading times.[^74][^80][^76][^106]
### 7.4 Receiving protocols and chain of custody for high‑value items
Trade‑show logistics guides for small businesses recommend using experienced trade‑show trucking companies that understand rigid show schedules and specialised handling requirements for high‑value displays and equipment. Security and IT asset‑disposition chain‑of‑custody guidance, while not event‑specific, illustrates best practices relevant to high‑value or sensitive items at venues: using employees rather than ad‑hoc contractors, background‑checked staff, clearly identified vehicles, locked and monitored trucks, controlled‑access storage, comprehensive inventorying at pickup and drop‑off, and signed transfer documentation at each stage. Event security firms describe confiscated prohibited items being logged, documented, and stored in secure containers with chain‑of‑custody records and clear release protocols, illustrating how similar documentation principles apply to touring artist equipment or high‑value exhibition materials.[^107][^108][^109][^110]

## 8. Production services provided by venues
### 8.1 House rigging and automation
Arenas, theatres, and convention centres typically provide a combination of permanent rigging grids, catwalks, and house steel along with chain hoists, trusses, and sometimes automated rigging systems, often under exclusive or strongly controlled arrangements for safety reasons. IATSE locals frequently provide certified riggers for overhead work, with union rules and facility guidelines specifying who may attach loads to building steel, operate hoists, and approve rigging plots. Exhibitor and decorator manuals emphasise that rigging, especially overhead sign hanging, is often an exclusive service of the GSC or venue, further centralising control over safety‑critical lifting operations.[^39][^45][^40][^7][^65]
### 8.2 House audio, lighting, and video
Venues commonly maintain house PA systems for sports presentations, paging, and basic events, along with in‑house or preferred‑provider inventories of portable sound, lighting, and projection equipment for rentals and smaller shows. Larger concerts and broadcast events typically carry their own production, tying into house infrastructure such as power distribution, rigging points, cable paths, and sometimes house LED ribbons or scoreboards. Performing arts centres may maintain orchestra shells, repertory lighting rigs, soft goods, and plot templates and offer technical packages to visiting companies that supplement touring gear.[^2][^4][^7][^65][^9]
### 8.3 Power distribution for touring shows
Exhibitor and venue manuals describe utility services including electrical distribution panels, cam‑lock connections for show power, and dedicated feeder runs to stages or exhibit areas, often requiring advance coordination and floorplans. Touring productions tie into these services via company or vendor‑supplied distribution racks, ensuring appropriate load balancing and redundancy, with venue electricians and union labour often responsible for making and breaking main power connections. For trade shows, dedicated power drops to booths are arranged via order forms, with labour charges applied for installation beyond standard packages.[^66][^39][^40][^65][^71]
### 8.4 Stage management and production offices
For concerts and large events, venues typically provide production offices equipped with desks, internet, printers, and communication lines, supporting tour managers and production coordinators in handling schedules, accounting, and crew logistics. Stage management functions – whether provided by venue staff, local crew, or visiting companies – include calling the show based on the ROS, coordinating with FOH (front‑of‑house), backstage, and command centres, and managing rehearsals, sound checks, and changeovers between acts. Major venues sometimes embed show‑calling and tech direction within their operations departments, especially for in‑house produced events and ceremonies.[^2][^49][^56][^21][^58][^6]
