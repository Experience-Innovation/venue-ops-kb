# Large Venue Food & Beverage Compliance: Health Safety, Licensing & Supply Chain Standards (April 2026)

## Executive Summary

Large venue food and beverage operations — spanning permanent concessions, portable stands, catering kitchens, and multi-point-of-sale bars — face an overlapping matrix of federal, state/provincial, and municipal requirements. As of April 2026, four developments demand immediate operator attention: the enforcement of FSMA Section 204 traceability rules (compliance deadline extended to July 2028); California SB 1383 large-venue organic waste enforcement (active since January 2024); Alberta Bill 201's transformation of tip and wage rules (December 2025); and a wave of post-pandemic liquor licensing reforms in major US states and Canadian provinces. This report synthesizes all six compliance domains into actionable, jurisdiction-specific guidance.[^1][^2][^3][^4][^5]

***

## 1. Health Department Inspection Requirements

### Federal Baseline: FDA Food Code 2022

The FDA Food Code (most current version: January 18, 2023) is the foundational model for all state and local food service regulations in the United States. It is not binding federal law but is adopted — in whole or in part — by state and local jurisdictions. The 2022 edition introduced notable updates relevant to venues: updated risk designations for Priority Foundation violations, new rules on Time as a Public Health Control (TPHC), and expanded provisions for food donation from food service operations.[^6][^7][^8]

The FDA Food Code applies to **all retail and food service operations**, including venue concessions, catering, restaurants, vending operations, and temporary food stands. It does **not** apply to food manufacturing or processing plants.[^7]

### Temporary vs. Permanent Food Service in Venues

The FDA Food Code defines a **Temporary Food Establishment (TFE)** as one that operates for no more than 14 consecutive days in conjunction with a single event. This distinction carries significant regulatory consequences for venue operators:[^9]

| Feature | Permanent Food Facility | Temporary Food Establishment |
|---|---|---|
| Permit Type | Annual food service permit | Event-specific TFE permit (single or multi-event) |
| Physical Requirements | Full plumbing, permanent sinks, sealed floors | Portable sinks/holding tanks accepted; dirt floors permitted if graded to drain [^10] |
| CFPM Requirement | At least one Certified Food Protection Manager per facility [^11] | At minimum one Food Handler Permit holder on-site at all times [^12] |
| Advance Notice | Pre-operational inspection required before opening [^13] | Application typically required 30–60 days in advance [^14][^15] |
| Menu Restrictions | Full menu as approved | Menu limited to pre-approved items on application [^14] |
| Inspection Timing | Routine, complaint-based, re-inspection | Approval inspection 1 hour before event start [^16][^17] |

**Key rule for venues with multiple food service points:** Under model food code provisions, a separate permit is required for **every type of food establishment** at a location, unless establishments share common ownership *and* involve only minimal preparation of potentially hazardous food. A venue running a full concession stand, a restaurant-style seated service, and a catering kitchen would typically require separate permits for each operation.[^13]

### Jurisdiction-Specific Inspection Frameworks

**California** enforces the California Retail Food Code (CRFC) (California Health and Safety Code §113700 et seq.), administered by county Environmental Health Departments. Multiple contiguous food facilities under the same management at an amusement park or venue are deemed "one food facility" for CFPM certification purposes. LA County requires Temporary Food Facility applications at least **30 days before** an event; late applications (under 14 days) incur a 25% surcharge.[^11][^15]

**Texas** follows the Texas Food Establishment Rules (TFER), based on the FDA Food Code. Under SB 1008 (89th Legislature, effective September 1, 2025), local government permit fees for temporary food events are **capped at the DSHS fee of $52**, and local jurisdictions cannot impose duplicate food manager certification fees if a manager is already state-certified. A statewide mobile food vendor permit takes effect July 1, 2026. Each food facility must employ at least one Certified Food Manager.[^18][^19][^20]

**Florida** uses a risk-based inspection schedule under Florida Statutes §381.0072: high-risk facilities (full preparation, large volumes) inspected **quarterly**; moderate risk **semi-annually**; low risk **once per year**. Theaters with limited food (beverages, popcorn, hot dogs, nachos) fall under the Florida Department of Health rather than the Department of Business and Professional Regulation.[^21]

**Alberta, Canada** requires a Food Handling Permit from Alberta Health Services (AHS) for all commercial food establishments including caterers. If **six or more food handlers** (including servers) are on premises in a single shift, a certified food safety professional must be on-site at all times. For special events, vendors must be ready for AHS approval inspection **one hour before** the event and must sanitize temporary equipment every four hours during the event.[^22][^23][^16]

**Ontario, Canada** requires food operators at events to hold a valid Food Service Premises permit from the local Public Health Unit. All food preparation prior to the event must occur in an inspected facility; only final assembly and cooking is permitted on-site.[^24]

**British Columbia** similarly requires Mobile Food Premises permits from regional health authorities, with three operational tiers (A, B, C) based on food handling complexity.[^25]

### Certified Food Protection Manager (CFPM) Standards

The ServSafe Manager Certification — accredited by the American National Accreditation Board (ANAB) — is the primary pathway for CFPM qualification in the US. The exam comprises **90 questions** over two hours; certification is valid for **five years**. Food Handler certificates (40 questions) are valid for three years. ANSI/ANAB accreditation is mandatory in California; non-accredited certifications are not accepted.[^26][^27][^11]

## 2. Liquor Licensing Structures for Large Venues

### US State-by-State Overview

The US liquor licensing landscape is highly fragmented — each state, and often each municipality, sets its own rules. For large venues specifically:[^28]

| Jurisdiction | Key License Type | Multi-Point-of-Sale | Special Event/Temp | Responsible Service Requirement |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **New York** | On-Premises License (various types); new For-Profit Club License (Ch. 342, Aug. 22, 2025)[^1] | Proposed 24-hr venue license for 20,000+ sq ft event spaces (still in committee)[^1] | Existing on-premises license covers ancillary event service areas | TIPS/TiPS or equivalent; no statewide mandate |
| **California** | ABC On-Sale General (Type 47); On-Sale Beer & Wine (Type 41) | Each point-of-sale must be under the licensed premises footprint | ABC catering authorization for off-premises events | Responsible Beverage Service Training (RBST) mandatory — ABC RBS certification required [^29] |
| **Florida** | Series 4COP-S (Special Hotel License)[^28]; Series 2-COP (beer/wine) | Hotel license applies throughout property; separate licenses per outlet otherwise | Temporary license via Division of Alcoholic Beverages and Tobacco | No statewide RBST mandate; operator-led training recommended |
| **New Jersey** | Reformed in 2024: 1,300+ new licenses added; new categories for large shopping malls[^2] | Under reform, large malls and event venues can obtain new license types | Special event permit process | TIPS or equivalent encouraged |
| **Texas** | Mixed Beverage Permit (MB); Mixed Beverage Restaurant Permit (R); TABC-regulated | Separate permit per service area; venue operator may hold multiple permits | TABC Temporary Authorization (requires licensed caterer) | TABC TEAM or equivalent; seller training mandated |
| **Illinois** | Local municipality licenses; state oversight | Venue must obtain separate licenses for each licensed service area | Special Event Retailer Permit | State Employee Training (no single statewide mandate; Chicago has local requirements) |

### Canadian Provincial Overview

| Province | Key License/Permit | Catering Authority | Responsible Service |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Ontario** | Liquor Sales Licence (AGCO); O. Reg. 746/21[^30] | Caterer's Endorsement: allows off-premises service; no SOP required; 10-day advance notice to AGCO, police, fire, health and building departments[^31] | Smart Serve — **mandatory** for all staff involved in alcohol service[^32] |
| **British Columbia** | Liquor Primary Licence; Food Primary Licence (LCLB) | Special Event Permit (SEP) required for public-place events; 4–6 week application window[^33] | Serving It Right — **mandatory** for all licensees/permittees and staff[^34] |
| **Alberta** | Liquor Licence (AGLC) | Special Event Licence (SEL) required for events at non-licensed venues | ProServe — **mandatory** training for staff who sell or serve liquor |
| **Quebec** | Permit issued by Régie des alcools, des courses et des jeux (RACJ) | Reunion permit (Permis de réunion) for private events | Éduc'alcool training recommended; no single mandated program |

### Multi-Point-of-Sale and Catering Endorsements

Ontario's O. Reg. 746/21 Section 30 permits a **single liquor sales licence to apply to multiple premises**, provided service bars at one premises only service a second premises if specified conditions are met. Ontario's AGCO Caterer's Endorsement allows licensed food service operators to serve at off-premises events without a Special Occasion Permit, subject to 10-day advance notification. Events must be sponsored by someone other than the licence holder, and liquor not sold must be returned to stock.[^30][^31]

BC's Special Event Permit system requires that where an establishment has multiple service areas (some licensed, some not), **adequate physical separation** must exist between liquor-service and non-liquor areas.[^35]

### Responsible Beverage Service (RBS) Requirements

- **Ontario Smart Serve**: mandatory for all alcohol service staff; covers signs of intoxication, refusal of service, and ID verification[^32]
- **BC Serving It Right**: required before operating any licensed establishment; staff must remove unconsumed liquor within 30 minutes of closing; staff may not consume alcohol while working[^34]
- **California RBS**: mandated by ABC; all servers at licensed premises must be ABC-certified; certification covers intervention techniques and legal liability[^29]
- **US TIPS/ServSafe Alcohol**: widely accepted across states; not universally mandated at the federal level but required by many individual state or county ordinances

## 3. HACCP Implementation in Venue Food Service

### Required vs. Voluntary

| Jurisdiction | HACCP Status for Venue F&B |
|---|---|
| **US Federal (FDA)** | Voluntary for retail/food service; FDA Food Code uses HACCP principles as a risk-management framework but does not require formal HACCP plans for food service operations[^6][^8] |
| **US Federal (USDA/FSIS)** | Mandatory for meat/poultry processing facilities; not applicable to venue food service |
| **Canada (SFCR)** | Mandatory for all licensed food businesses under Safe Food for Canadians Regulations (SFCR) Part 4; Preventive Control Plan (PCP) required and must be HACCP-based[^36][^37] |
| **Ontario/Alberta/BC** | Provincial health regulations require food safety management consistent with HACCP principles for commercial operators; formal written HACCP plans are best practice and effectively required for large-scale catering operations[^38] |

### HACCP Requirements Under Canada's SFCR (SOR/2018-108)

Under Part 4 of the SFCR, every licensed food operator must implement preventive controls addressing:
1. Hazard identification and analysis (SFCR s.47)
2. Treatment and processes (SFCR s.48)
3. Establishment maintenance and operation: sanitation, pest control, equipment, employee hygiene (SFCR s.49–52)
4. Investigation, notification, complaints, and recall procedures[^37][^39]

Operators must prepare and maintain a written **Preventive Control Plan (PCP)** documenting how each hazard is controlled. Small food businesses may be exempt from the written PCP requirement but must still meet all applicable preventive control provisions.[^36]

### High-Volume Catering Operations: Key HACCP Protocols

For large-scale venue catering (sports arenas, convention centers, concert venues), the following protocols are operationally critical:

- **Temperature Control**: Hot foods ≥ 60°C (140°F); cold foods ≤ 4°C (40°F); regular monitoring with calibrated thermometers and digital logging[^38]
- **Critical Control Points (CCPs)**: Typically include receiving temperature verification, cold storage monitoring, cooking temperatures, hot-holding, and service temperatures
- **Time as a Public Health Control (TPHC)**: FDA Food Code 2022 added requirements for RTE produce and hermetically sealed foods held up to four hours at ≤ 21°C (70°F)[^7]
- **HACCP Documentation for Events**: Per the National Arenas Association A-Guide, catering operators must submit HACCP documentation and insurance details to the venue **at least 28 days before** the event[^40]
- **Transport Safety**: Foods delivered cold to a venue and served hot must be reheated to a temperature sufficient to kill pathogens; temperature records must be maintained during transport and service[^41]

### Allergen Management

**United States**: The Food Allergen Labeling and Consumer Protection Act (FALCPA, 2004) mandated labeling of eight major allergens. The Food Allergy Safety, Treatment, Education, and Research Act (FASTER Act, 2021) added **sesame** as the ninth major allergen, effective January 1, 2023. Pre-packaged foods must label all nine allergens. Note: most foods sold at food service establishments that are **not pre-labeled** are exempt from FALCPA's labeling requirements; however, best practice (and the expectation under the FDA Food Code's allergen cross-contact provisions) requires food service operators to be able to communicate allergen information to guests.[^42][^43][^44]

**Canada**: The Canadian Food Inspection Agency requires labeling of 14 priority food allergens for packaged foods. For food service, CFIA guidance and Food Allergy Canada's guidelines recommend:
- Detailed ingredient tracking and supplier allergen documentation
- Dedicated preparation areas and equipment for allergen-free meals
- Scheduling allergen-free meal production first
- Staff training on recognition and emergency response[^45][^46]

**Venue-Specific Best Practice**: Implement a written allergen control plan covering risk assessment, ingredient segregation, supplier documentation, workflow design, and staff training. Cross-contamination prevention requires dedicated utensils, color-coded equipment, and strict cleaning protocols between preparations.[^45]

### HACCP Certification Options

| Certification | Provider | Scope | Status |
|---|---|---|---|
| ServSafe Manager | National Restaurant Association / ANAB | US food service; HACCP principles + food safety management | Widely required; 5-year validity[^26] |
| FOODSAFE Level 1/2 | Province of BC (and equivalents) | Canadian food service; BC requires it for Mobile Food Premises Type B/C[^25] | Mandatory in BC for certain operations |
| SGS HACCP for Catering | SGS Canada | International HACCP certification for catering/food service | Voluntary; demonstrates HACCP compliance beyond regulatory minimum[^47] |
| GFSI-Benchmarked Schemes (SQF, BRC, FSSC 22000) | Various | Manufacturing/processing suppliers to venues | Typically applied to venue's food supply chain, not service operations |

## 4. Supply Chain Traceability Requirements

### FSMA Section 204 Food Traceability Rule (21 CFR Part 1, Subpart S)

The FSMA Section 204(d) Food Traceability Rule is the most significant new supply chain compliance requirement for US venue operators sourcing foods on the Food Traceability List (FTL). The original compliance deadline of **January 20, 2026** has been extended by legislation to **July 20, 2028**, providing venues additional runway for implementation.[^3]

**Who is covered**: Any business that manufactures, processes, packs, ships, stores, receives, or imports FTL foods. This includes venue operators and their distributors who handle FTL foods (which include fresh produce, shell eggs, nut butters, fresh-cut fruits and vegetables, ready-to-eat deli salads, and seafood, among others).[^48]

**Core Requirements**:
- Assign and retain a **Traceability Lot Code (TLC)** for every FTL food item[^49]
- Record **Key Data Elements (KDEs)** at each **Critical Tracking Event (CTE)** (harvesting, packing, shipping, receiving, transformation)[^50][^49]
- Maintain a written **Traceability Plan** describing procedures, KDE formats, and contact persons[^50]
- Retain all records in **electronic, sortable format** available to FDA within **24 hours of request**[^51][^49]
- Records must be kept for **at least two years** after creation[^48]

**For venue operators as receivers**: The burden centers on verifying supplier FSMA 204 compliance and storing supplier-provided TLCs and KDEs in an accessible, searchable format.[^49]

### Canadian Food Recall and Traceability Requirements (SFCR SOR/2018-108)

Under the Safe Food for Canadians Regulations, subsections 84–85, every licensed food operator must:
- Prepare and maintain a **written recall procedure** naming a responsible contact person (SFCR s.84(1))[^52]
- **Immediately implement** the recall procedure when a food presents a risk of injury (SFCR s.84(4))[^52]
- Document recall details, including information substantiating effectiveness, and retain for **two years** after recall initiation[^52]

The CFIA manages a **5-step recall investigation process** (hazard identification → risk assessment → decision → recall implementation → effectiveness verification) and assigns Class I, II, or III recall classifications based on health risk. A CFIA food recall includes preparation of a distribution list identifying every client by name, address, lot code, and quantity received.[^53][^54]

**10-Element Recall Program**: Manitoba Agriculture guidance identifies the ten core elements every venue operator's recall plan should incorporate: recall management team, complaint file, recall contact list, tracing system, product amounts documentation, distribution records, recalled product records, recall procedures, effectiveness procedures, and regular plan updates and testing.[^55]

### Supplier Qualification Programs

Best practice for venue operators includes:
- Requiring suppliers to provide allergen documentation, HACCP certifications, and recall procedures (e.g., Gordon Food Service Canada's Supplier Food Safety & Quality Expectations Manual)[^56]
- Conducting periodic supplier audits against GFSI-benchmarked standards
- Maintaining approved supplier lists with documented verification records

### Cold Chain Compliance

Under FSMA's Sanitary Transportation of Human and Animal Food Rule (21 CFR Part 1, Subpart O), shippers, loaders, carriers, and receivers must use sanitary practices during food transport, including proper refrigeration, vehicle cleaning between loads, and temperature maintenance documentation.[^57][^58]

**2025 regulatory updates** emphasize:
- **IoT temperature loggers** for real-time monitoring during transport[^59]
- Temperature specifications: 0–4°C for fresh/chilled produce; -18°C for frozen goods[^59]
- Pre-cooling verification before loading temperature-sensitive foods
- No deviations over 30 minutes at spec temperature are permitted under current 2025 regulatory interpretations[^59]

## 5. Sustainability and Waste Regulations

### US Food Waste Diversion Mandates by Jurisdiction

| Jurisdiction | Law/Ordinance | Scope Applicable to Venues | Effective / Enforced |
|---|---|---|---|
| **California** | SB 1383 (Short-Lived Climate Pollutant Reduction Strategy) | Large venues/events with **>2,000 visitors/day** that charge admission = Tier 2 commercial edible food generator; mandatory organic diversion, food recovery contracts required[^60][^5] | Enforcement (fines) since **Jan 1, 2024** |
| **California (local)** | SB 1383 implemented by local jurisdictions | Restaurants ≥250 seats or ≥5,000 sq ft also Tier 2; penalties require 60-day notice of violation then imposition[^60] | Jan 1, 2024 |
| **Illinois** | SB2816 / Public Act 103-0927 | Permanent facilities hosting events **≥3,500 attendees** (stadiums, convention centers) must offer recycling and composting bins; in counties with composting facilities[^61][^62] | **Jan 1, 2025** |
| **Washington State** | HB 1799 (2022); HB 2301 (2024); HB 1497 (2025) | Businesses generating ≥4 cu yds organic waste/week must arrange collection or on-site management; 75% reduction target by 2030[^62][^63] | Phased 2024–2026 |
| **Maryland** | HB0264 – Organics Recycling and Waste Diversion | Generators of ≥1 ton food residuals/week within 30 miles of composting facility must divert (compost, animal feed, food rescue)[^61] | **Jan 1, 2024** |
| **Connecticut** | Chapter 446d | Mandatory organic diversion for commercial food wholesalers, industrial food manufacturers, supermarkets, resorts, and conference centers[^61] | Ongoing enforcement |
| **Denver, CO** | Ordinance 306 | Sports venues, grocery stores, hotels, airports, and restaurants must offer recycling and organic material diversion[^64] | Effective 2023 |

### Canadian Organic Waste Requirements

Ontario enacted a **landfill ban on organic waste** effective January 1, 2025, making organic diversion mandatory for businesses generating significant food waste. At the federal level, Canada's 2030 zero plastic waste target underpins provincial programs across all jurisdictions.[^65]

### Single-Use Plastics Bans

**Canada (Federal)**: The *Single-use Plastics Prohibition Regulations* (SUPPR) prohibit the manufacture, import, and sale of six categories of SUPs: checkout bags, cutlery, foodservice ware made from or containing problematic plastics, ring carriers, stir sticks, and straws (with limited exceptions for accessibility). Domestic market prohibitions took effect December 20, 2022–2024 on a staggered schedule. The federal Court of Appeal upheld the listing of "Plastic Manufactured Items" on Schedule 1 on January 30, 2026, confirming the regulations remain in force. The government dropped the export ban component in October 2025 amid trade uncertainty. Fines: $400–$2,000 for first violation; $500–$4,000 for continued violations.[^66][^67][^68]

**United States** (state/local):
- Oregon and Rhode Island: bans on polystyrene foam foodservice containers[^62]
- Illinois: polystyrene ban extended to smaller restaurants and government agencies (2025)[^62]
- Delaware: polystyrene ban effective July 1, 2025[^62]
- Many municipal bans on plastic straws and bags in force (Seattle, San Francisco, New York City, etc.)

**Sustainability Certifications for Venues**:

| Certification | Issuer | Focus | Relevance to Venue F&B |
|---|---|---|---|
| LEED (Leadership in Energy & Environmental Design) | USGBC | Whole-building sustainability; covers materials/resources and waste management[^69][^70] | Charlotte Convention Center achieved LEED Gold (2023); only 2 North American venues at Platinum[^69] |
| TRUE Zero Waste | Green Business Certification Inc. (GBCI) | Zero waste for facilities and events; 25+ points to "Certified," up to 69 for Platinum[^71] | Events certification available; covers food scrap composting, upstream vendor packaging requirements[^71] |
| GBAC STAR | ISSA / IAVM | Cleaning, disinfection, and infectious disease prevention[^72] | Promoted by IAVM for venue operators |

## 6. Labor Regulations Specific to Venue F&B

### Tip Pooling Laws

**United States — Federal (FLSA)**

Under the Fair Labor Standards Act (29 CFR Part 531, Subpart D), as updated by the 2021 DOL Final Rule:
- Employers **cannot keep employees' tips** under any circumstances; managers and supervisors are explicitly prohibited from tip pool participation[^73]
- Employers paying the FLSA subminimum direct wage of **$2.13/hr** (tip credit) may establish traditional tip pools **limited to employees who customarily and regularly receive tips** (servers, bartenders, bussers, counter staff)[^74]
- Employers paying the **full federal minimum wage** (no tip credit) may include back-of-house staff (cooks, dishwashers) in expanded "nontraditional" tip pools[^75]
- The **December 2024 DOL Final Rule** (Federal Register, December 17, 2024) restored a modified version of the "80/20 rule" for tipped employees performing dual (tipped and non-tipped) work — the specifics of the dual jobs provision govern when a tip credit can apply[^76]
- Service charges are not tips but count toward wages and overtime[^77]

**State Variations**: Several states prohibit a tip credit entirely (requiring the full state minimum wage regardless of tips), including California, Oregon, Washington, Montana, Minnesota, Alaska, and Nevada. In these states, expanded nontraditional tip pools inclusive of BOH staff are permitted but must be voluntary or clearly disclosed. In California, Arizona, and other states with higher minimum wages, the interplay between state minimum wage and the federal tip credit is preempted by the higher state standard.

**Canada — Provincial**

| Province | Tip Protection | Tip Pooling | Minimum Wage (April 2026) |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Federal (federally regulated industries)** | Tips are property of employees | Pooling permitted if disclosed | **$18.10/hr** (effective Apr 1, 2026)[^78] |
| **Ontario** | Tips belong to employees; employers cannot deduct except for lawful pooling | Permitted; must be fair and documented | $17.60/hr[^78] |
| **British Columbia** | Tips are employee property | Permitted; must comply with ESA | $17.85/hr[^78] |
| **Alberta** | 100% employee property; Bill 201 (Dec 15, 2025): no employer deductions; age-based minimum wages eliminated[^4] | **Voluntary only**, with written employee consent[^4] | $15.00/hr[^78] |
| **Quebec** | Protected under Act Respecting Labour Standards | Permitted | $12.90/hr tipped; $16.10/hr general[^78] |

### Split Shift Regulations

**California**: Under IWC Wage Orders 1-15, Section 4, a **split shift premium** equal to one additional hour at the applicable minimum (or local) wage is owed whenever a worker's schedule is interrupted by non-paid, non-working time established by the employer (longer than a bona fide meal period, within the same workday). The premium is reduced by any wage earned above minimum for that day. Workers who voluntarily pick up an extra shift do not qualify.[^79]

**New York**: The Miscellaneous Wage Order entitles employees whose daily schedule is split to an additional hour at the applicable minimum (or local) wage. Meal breaks during the day do not trigger the premium.[^80][^81]

**Other jurisdictions**: Most other US states and Canadian provinces do not have a specific split shift premium, though general employment standards (minimum rest periods between shifts, overtime thresholds) still apply.

### Minor Labor Restrictions for Concession Workers

**Alberta**:
- Workers under 13 cannot be employed
- Workers aged 13–14 require parental/guardian consent and a permit from Employment Standards; must be in continuous presence of an adult if working as food service employees; prohibited from using dangerous kitchen equipment (deep fryers, slicers, grills, knives)[^82]
- Workers aged 15–17 may work in hospitality but cannot work after midnight without restrictions; cannot work past 12:00 a.m. without meeting specific conditions[^82]

**Manitoba**:
- Workers under 14 cannot work in restaurants[^83]
- Workers under 16 cannot work alone and have hour/time-of-day restrictions; 13-year-olds cannot perform food preparation involving dangerous tools[^84]

**New Brunswick**: Workers under 16 are limited to 6 hours/day, cannot work nights; workers under 14 cannot work in restaurants or construction[^83]

**United States (Federal/FLSA)**: 
- Workers aged 14–15 may work in food service (non-hazardous tasks) but with restrictions on hours (no more than 3 hours on school days; no more than 8 hours on non-school days; only between 7 a.m. and 7 p.m. during school year, extended to 9 p.m. June 1–Labor Day)
- Workers under 18 are **prohibited from operating or cleaning** power-driven meat processing equipment, bakery equipment, and paper balers; hoisting operations are also restricted
- Workers aged 16–17 face fewer FLSA restrictions but state laws often impose additional limits

### Union Considerations in Venue F&B

Large venues in major markets operate under collective bargaining agreements with, most commonly:
- **UNITE HERE**: primary union for hotel, casino, convention, and venue food service and culinary workers in the US and Canada; covers cooks, servers, bartenders, dishwashers, and banquet staff[^85][^86]
- **UFCW (United Food and Commercial Workers)**: prevalent in food service and hospitality in Canada; active in concession and food service contexts[^87][^88]

Union influence is highly market-dependent:[^86]
- **Union-dense markets** (New York, Chicago, Los Angeles, Las Vegas): most large-venue F&B operations are covered by CBAs; city-owned venues may require all workers to be union members by contract
- **Right-to-work states** (e.g., Texas, Florida): less union density in venue F&B, but contracts may still exist at specific venues
- CBAs in venue F&B typically govern: minimum staffing levels, meal break timing (typically after 5 hours), overtime thresholds, tip-pooling arrangements, and jurisdiction over specific types of work

### OSHA Safety Standards for Venue F&B

OSHA's General Industry Walking-Working Surfaces standard (**29 CFR 1910 Subpart D**, including §1910.21–30) applies to all food service operations:[^89][^90]
- Any floor level change of **¼ inch or more** constitutes a tripping hazard requiring remediation[^90]
- Handrails on fixed stairs: 200-lb weight-bearing capacity; mid-point rails required on open platforms with more than 4 risers (29 CFR 1910.23(d))[^89]
- Wet zones (dish pits, bar areas) require rubber drainage mats; mats must fill entire wet zone to prevent edge curling[^91]
- Floor and working surfaces must be kept free of obstructions, hazardous accumulations, and slip-promoting finishes at all times[^91][^89]
- OSHA Form 300 Log must be maintained; slip/trip/fall incidents should be investigated systematically[^90]

Ontario's Industrial Establishments Regulation (O. Reg. 851) similarly requires floor surfaces used by workers to be kept free of obstructions and prohibits finishes that make surfaces slippery. Provincial labour ministries have flagged floor conditions as top inspection targets in restaurant/food service settings.[^91]
