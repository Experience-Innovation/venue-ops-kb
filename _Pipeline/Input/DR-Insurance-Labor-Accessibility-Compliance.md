# Insurance, Labor, and Accessibility Compliance for Large Public Assembly Venues (April 2026)

## Executive Summary

Large public assembly venues in the United States (arenas, stadiums, convention centers, theatres) operate at the intersection of insurance regulation, labor and employment law, accessibility mandates, and intellectual property rights. Operators must satisfy municipal and contractual insurance requirements, comply with federal programs such as the Terrorism Risk Insurance Act (TRIA), adhere to state workers’ compensation systems for event staff, manage multiple union jurisdictions, implement robust Americans with Disabilities Act (ADA) accessibility (including ticketing and web access), and secure proper music and branding rights. This report provides a structured overview with statutory citations, current program parameters through 2027, and practical implications as of April 2026.[^1][^2][^3][^4][^5][^6]

## 1. Insurance Requirements for Public Assembly Venues

### 1.1 Typical General Liability Limits and Municipal Requirements

Municipalities and public venue owners commonly require commercial general liability (CGL) limits of at least 5 million per occurrence for events and tenant users, even though there is no nationwide statutory minimum for private venues. For example, the City of Calgary’s infrastructure certificate bulletin specifies a CGL policy with a bodily injury and property damage limit “not less than Five Million Dollars” per occurrence and requires the city to be added as an additional insured, reflecting a pattern also seen in convention center and trade-show insurance requirements that mandate 5 million in CGL per occurrence.[^7][^8][^9]

Venue-focused event insurers in North America routinely market online event liability policies with options up to 5 million in general liability coverage, indicating that this limit has become a de facto standard for many arenas, theatres, and convention facilities when renting to third-party promoters. Short-term event insurance providers emphasize that most venues require proof of general liability, a certificate of insurance (COI), and specific aggregate limits (for example, 2 million aggregate) as a condition of rental.[^10][^11]

### 1.2 Additional Insured Status and Certificates of Insurance

Municipal owners and large private venue operators almost always require that they be named as an additional insured on the event organizer’s CGL policy, not merely listed as a certificate holder. Insurance compliance guidance stresses that an additional insured endorsement confers actual coverage rights, while being named only on a COI does not amend the policy unless accompanied by a proper endorsement. Municipal forms often require the venue entity to appear as an additional insured and specify clauses such as cross-liability, contractual liability, products and completed operations, and non-owned auto liability.[^12][^13][^14][^7]

Standard practice is for the insured event organizer to provide a COI that reflects the required limits and lists the venue as certificate holder and additional insured, with insurers or brokers issuing venue-specific certificates through online portals. Many municipal or provincial infrastructure departments mandate use of their own certificate forms and explicitly reject generic broker forms, reinforcing that the COI is informational and that the binding requirements are in the underlying contract and endorsements.[^15][^11][^10][^12][^7]

### 1.3 Typical Coverage Components for Large Venues

While the precise coverage program varies by venue and jurisdiction, large public assembly facilities typically maintain or require the following coverages for venue operations and event licensees:

| Coverage type | Typical role for large venues | Typical limit range / notes |
|---------------|-------------------------------|-----------------------------|
| Commercial General Liability (CGL) | Core protection for bodily injury and property damage arising from premises and operations, including spectator claims | 2–5 million per occurrence common; many municipal and convention requirements set 5 million as minimum per occurrence.[^7][^8][^9] |
| Umbrella / Excess Liability | Extends limits above primary CGL, auto, and employers’ liability | Often used to reach 10–25 million total; specific limits negotiated with owner or lender.[^12][^7] |
| Liquor Liability | Required where alcohol is sold or served; may be separate or within CGL | Frequently mandated by venue policy for concessionaires; specific limits vary and may match CGL per-occurrence limit.[^16] |
| Auto Liability (Owned/Non-owned) | Covers shuttle operations, valet, and hired/non-owned autos related to events | Municipal forms often require non-owned auto liability with minimum sub-limits, e.g., 2 million.[^12] |
| Property (Building and Contents) | Protects venue physical plant, equipment, and tenant improvements | Limits based on replacement cost; often combined with business interruption and boiler/machinery coverages.[^17] |
| Terrorism (TRIA-eligible) | Covers certified acts of terrorism under TRIA on commercial property, liability, and business interruption lines | Must be offered by insurers for TRIA-eligible lines; venues typically accept or decline via TRIA disclosure forms.[^1][^3][^17] |
| Event Cancellation / Non-appearance | Protects promoters and sometimes venues from revenue loss due to covered perils cancelling events | Market-driven; limits tied to projected gross revenue per event; not mandated by law.[^16] |
| Workers’ Compensation and Employers’ Liability | Statutory coverage for venue employees and, in some states, required for contractors | Limits and requirements set by state law; employers’ liability limits commonly 1 million or more but not dictated by TRIA.[^18][^19] |

## 2. Terrorism Risk Insurance Act (TRIA) and Venue Applicability

### 2.1 Statutory Framework and Program Status

The Terrorism Risk Insurance Act of 2002 (TRIA), as amended and codified in a note to 15 U.S.C. 6701, created a federal backstop for commercial property and casualty insurers to share losses from certified acts of terrorism. TRIA established the Terrorism Risk Insurance Program (TRIP), administered by the U.S. Department of the Treasury’s Federal Insurance Office, which has been reauthorized multiple times and currently runs through December 31, 2027 under the Terrorism Risk Insurance Program Reauthorization Act of 2019.[^3][^20][^4][^1]

Under TRIA, insurers offering commercial property and casualty policies in specified TRIA-eligible lines must make available coverage for insured losses resulting from certified acts of terrorism, and policyholders must be offered the option to purchase this coverage subject to a separate premium and federal disclosures. The program is triggered only if the Secretary of the Treasury, in consultation with the Secretary of Homeland Security and the Attorney General, certifies an act as terrorism and aggregate insured losses exceed a program trigger threshold (currently 200 million), with insurer deductibles and federal co-insurance applying up to an aggregate 100 billion cap per calendar year.[^21][^2][^17][^20][^3]

### 2.2 Certified vs. Non-Certified Terrorism Events

TRIA applies only to “certified acts of terrorism,” defined in Section 102(1) of the Act as violent acts dangerous to human life, property, or infrastructure that result in damage within the United States (or certain U.S. facilities abroad) and are committed on behalf of foreign or domestic interests to coerce the civilian population or influence government policy, with insured losses exceeding 5 million. Treasury and state bulletins emphasize that terrorism losses from events that are not certified—either because they do not meet the statutory definition or because total losses do not reach the program trigger—may still be covered under standard property and liability policies if terrorism is not otherwise excluded.[^2][^17][^20][^21]

The Insurance Information Institute explains that commercial property owners such as office buildings, factories, shopping malls, and apartment buildings must be offered terrorism coverage under TRIA for relevant lines, and that standard homeowners policies already include terrorism damage within all-risk property coverage absent specific exclusions. For large venues, this means TRIA-certified terrorism coverage is typically available as an endorsement to CGL and commercial property policies, while non-certified events (for example, smaller-scale attacks or politically motivated violence below program thresholds) may fall under non-terrorism peril wording or specific stand‑alone political violence products depending on policy language.[^22][^17]

### 2.3 Market Availability and Program Effectiveness Through 2027

Treasury’s 2024 program effectiveness report indicates that terrorism risk insurance remains broadly available and affordable, with direct earned premiums for terrorism coverage from 2003–2023 estimated at approximately 56.7 billion (1–2 percent of premiums in TRIP-eligible lines), and with insurer and policyholder participation remaining strong across sectors including real estate, hospitality, and religious and public assembly facilities. The report notes that TRIA limits aggregate exposure for both insurers and the federal government by prohibiting federal payments once insured terrorism losses (including insurer and government shares) exceed the 100 billion annual cap, at which point insurers may not be obligated to pay additional terrorism-related claims beyond that cap.[^4][^21]

Industry guidance for brokers and agents underscores that insurers participating in TRIA must offer terrorism coverage to commercial policyholders, but insureds may affirmatively reject coverage in writing if they accept excluded terrorism risk or purchase alternative stand-alone programs. Large venues in major markets or symbolic locations are typically encouraged to purchase TRIA-backed terrorism coverage due to high aggregation risk and potential concentration of employees and spectators, while smaller community facilities may rely on standard property and liability coverages with or without TRIA endorsements depending on risk appetite.[^17][^21][^3][^22][^4]

## 3. Workers’ Compensation for Venue and Event Staff

### 3.1 Core Legal Requirements and Employee vs. Contractor Status

Workers’ compensation in the United States is governed by state law, but nearly all states require employers with at least one or a small number of employees to carry workers’ compensation insurance covering job-related injuries and diseases, including for event staff at arenas and theatres. The federal Fair Labor Standards Act (FLSA) employment relationship guidance clarifies that classification as an “independent contractor” versus “employee” is determined under an “economic realities” test in 29 C.F.R. part 795, examining factors such as control, opportunity for profit or loss, investment, permanence, and whether the work is integral to the business, rather than labels or 1099 status.[^18][^19][^23]

OSHA and state agencies stress that describing a worker as an independent contractor does not control legal status if the employer retains control over means and methods of performance; misclassification can trigger wage, tax, and workers’ compensation liabilities for venues and promoters. Guidance from legal clinics and worker centers likewise emphasizes that workers who depend on a single venue or promoter, follow its schedules and rules, and use its tools or uniforms are likely employees, not independent contractors, regardless of written agreements.[^24][^25][^26][^19]

### 3.2 NCCI and Other Workers’ Compensation Classification Codes

For states using the National Council on Compensation Insurance (NCCI) system, workers’ compensation premiums are based on class codes that reflect job duties and associated risk levels, with distinct codes often applying to theatre, arena, and amusement operations. Key codes relevant to large venue operations include:[^27][^18]

| NCCI / WCIRB code | Description (abridged) | Typical application in venue context |
|-------------------|------------------------|--------------------------------------|
| 9154 | Theater NOC – All Other Employees; includes managers, stagehands, box office staff, ushers, motion picture operators, refreshment stand workers and security where not separately classified.[^28][^29] | Non-performing theatre employees at performing arts centers, live theaters, and cinema operations inside venues. |
| 9155 / 9156 (jurisdictional variants) | Theaters – Motion picture or dance/opera/theater companies (performers).[^30] | Actors, dancers, and other performers for resident companies operating in venue spaces. |
| 9178 | Athletic sports or park – non-contact sports (players, coaches, trainers).[^31][^30] | Professional or semi-professional athletes and non-contact sports staff using the venue, often under separate team policies. |
| 9180 | Amusement device operation NOC – operation and maintenance of rides and similar attractions; includes ticket sellers and collectors at permanent amusement operations.[^32][^33] | Amusement device operations in theme-park style venues or attractions associated with stadiums (e.g., rides, fan zones). |
| 9015 | Building or property management – all other employees responsible for care, custody, and maintenance of facilities, including security, doormen, and maintenance personnel.[^34][^35][^36] | In‑house building operations and maintenance for arenas, stadiums, and convention centers managed as real estate assets. |

State-specific rating bureaus (for example, California’s WCIRB and New York’s independent bureau) publish their own class code schedules and may use similar or identical code numbers but with state-specific wording and rates. Venue operators typically classify employees according to principal duties, with separate codes assigned if workers regularly perform distinct roles (for example, a stagehand who also drives trucks may require split classification between a theatre code and a driver code).[^28][^30][^37][^18]

### 3.3 Multi-State Compliance for Touring Staff

Touring productions and national acts create multi-state workers’ compensation exposure where tour personnel travel and perform in multiple jurisdictions, sometimes with differing extraterritorial and reciprocity rules. Industry and insurance advisory materials explain that extraterritoriality determines whether the “home” state policy follows employees who temporarily work in other states, while reciprocity describes whether the “receiving” state recognizes the home-state coverage; misalignment can leave gaps where a touring worker could file a claim in a state not listed on the employer’s policy.[^38][^39]

NCCI-form policies allow listing primary states in Item 3.A and “other states” in Item 3.C to extend coverage to states where employees may travel, but insurers may not be licensed in all states and some states (for example, monopolistic fund states) require separate policies from state funds. Multi-state coverage guidance recommends that employers with touring staff identify all likely performance states, understand each state’s extraterritorial and reciprocity rules, and adjust policies or purchase additional coverage so that every jurisdiction either recognizes home‑state coverage or is specifically listed on the policy.[^23][^40][^38]

### 3.4 Contractors, Vendors, and Subcontracted Labor

Venue operators often rely on third-party contractors for security, concessions, cleaning, and temporary event labor, but state workers’ compensation laws and contract terms may still allocate liability back to the venue if contractors fail to carry coverage. Many venue contracts require contractors to maintain workers’ compensation for all employees and to provide proof of coverage, and some states treat general contractors or owners as statutory employers with secondary liability if subcontractors are uninsured. To reduce exposure, venues typically: (1) require contractor COIs for workers’ compensation; (2) mandate indemnity clauses and additional insured status for employers’ liability where permitted; and (3) coordinate with staffing agencies that specialize in temporary event and stage labor.[^16][^41][^38][^23]

## 4. Union Labor in Venues

### 4.1 Major Unions and Typical Jurisdictions

Large venues frequently operate under multiple collective bargaining agreements (CBAs) with unions representing stage technicians, hospitality workers, freight and drivers, and building trades. Commonly involved unions and their typical roles include:[^42][^43][^44]

| Union | Typical venue jurisdiction | Illustrative evidence |
|-------|----------------------------|-----------------------|
| IATSE (International Alliance of Theatrical Stage Employees) | Stagehands, riggers, lighting, sound technicians, carpenters, wardrobe, and sometimes ushers and box office staff in theatres, arenas, and performing arts centers.[^42][^43][^45] | Local agreements recognize IATSE as the exclusive bargaining agent for stage employees of municipal theatres and specify that all stage-related technical work, including load‑in, set‑up, operation, and strike, falls under IATSE jurisdiction.[^46][^45][^47] |
| UNITE HERE | Concessions, catering, bartenders, suite attendants, and stadium foodservice workers in sports complexes and convention centers.[^44][^48] | UNITE HERE locals have negotiated “historic” contracts covering stadium concessions workers across multiple venues, providing wage floors, healthcare eligibility, and benefits for workers at major sports complexes.[^44][^48] |
| Teamsters | Freight handling, trucking, drayage, and sometimes event logistical labor such as loading/unloading equipment at convention centers and arenas.[^49][^50] | Work-rule compilations for major convention centers describe Teamsters as having jurisdiction over freight handling, including unloading trucks, moving freight to booths, and drayage, with other trades responsible for installation.[^50][^49] |
| Building and Construction Trades (Carpenters, Electricians, Laborers, etc.) | Construction, renovation, and sometimes maintenance work on stadiums and large venues under project labor agreements (PLAs) and prevailing wage laws.[^51][^52] | PLAs for stadium improvements, combined with the Davis–Bacon Act, require contractors to pay locally prevailing wages and to use specified building trades unions for covered construction, alteration, and repair projects.[^51][^53][^54]

### 4.2 Collective Bargaining Agreement Structures and Scope

Venue-related CBAs typically define recognition, scope, wage scales, hours of work, and grievance/arbitration processes for covered employees. For example, a municipal theatre agreement recognizes IATSE local members as the sole bargaining agent for “all stage employees” at specified city venues, lists detailed stage functions (load‑in, set‑up, operation of lighting and sound, rigging, temporary seating, etc.) as bargaining-unit work, and restricts contracting out of such work. Similar IATSE agreements at performing arts centers and theatres specify exclusive jurisdiction over “all theatre activity of a technical nature normally performed by Stage or Production Employees,” with wage and benefit schedules by classification and seniority.[^46][^43][^45][^47]

UNITE HERE stadium foodservice contracts with large concessionaires (such as Aramark) often cover all concessions stands, premium clubs, and catering operations across multiple stadiums in a sports complex, establishing minimum hourly wages (for example, 20 per hour with increases to 24 per hour by 2029), healthcare eligibility standards, paid holidays, and work rules. Teamsters agreements with sports and entertainment employers similarly govern freight and logistics work at multiple venues, with provisions limiting contracting out when it would reduce bargaining-unit hours.[^44][^48][^49][^55]

### 4.3 Jurisdiction Rules, Right-to-Work, and Non-Union Venues

Union jurisdiction varies significantly by city and state: many large coastal and Midwest markets expect union crews for most backstage and trades work, while right‑to‑work states may not legally require union labor but still use union crews for specialized rigging or to align with promoter expectations. Trade guidance for exhibitors emphasizes that each venue has its own “union work rules and jurisdictions,” often requiring union riggers for overhead work, union freight handlers for truck loading/unloading, and union decorators or carpenters for booth construction, with limited self‑help allowed for exhibitor employees.[^56][^50]

Venue operators must map these jurisdictional rules into their staffing plans and contracts, often requiring touring producers to use house unions for specific tasks (for example, rigging, lighting, and local crew) while allowing production to bring key touring staff as supervisors or department heads. Failure to respect jurisdiction can generate grievances, work stoppages, or additional costs, especially where PLAs and city ordinances require use of signatory contractors for public venues receiving public funds.[^43][^51][^52][^56]

### 4.4 Prevailing Wage Requirements for Public Venues

The Davis–Bacon Act, codified at 40 U.S.C. 3141 et seq., requires payment of locally prevailing wages and fringe benefits to laborers and mechanics employed on federal government and federally assisted construction contracts over 2,000 for public buildings and public works, which include stadiums and other public facilities when federally funded. The U.S. Department of Labor explains that Davis–Bacon prevailing wages are set through wage determinations listing base hourly rates plus fringe benefits for each classification, and that contractors must pay at least these rates to workers performing work within each classification.[^53][^57][^58][^59]

Recent Davis–Bacon rule updates clarify that union-negotiated wage rates can become “prevailing” when they represent at least 30 percent of wage survey results in a given area, increasing the likelihood that union CBA rates set the floor for publicly funded venue projects. For local governments receiving federal infrastructure or recovery funds for venue construction or renovation, Davis–Bacon and the Contract Work Hours and Safety Standards Act (CWHSSA) together require prevailing wage payment and overtime for covered projects above specified contract value thresholds.[^51][^54][^58]

## 5. ADA Title III Compliance for Entertainment Venues

### 5.1 Title III General Obligations and Public Accommodation Status

Title III of the Americans with Disabilities Act, codified at 42 U.S.C. 12182, prohibits discrimination on the basis of disability in the full and equal enjoyment of goods, services, facilities, privileges, advantages, or accommodations of any place of public accommodation by any person who owns, leases, or operates such a place. The statute defines public accommodations to include a broad category of privately owned entities affecting commerce, including theaters, stadiums, auditoriums, convention centers, health clubs, and other places of exhibition or entertainment.[^5][^60][^61]

Title III’s specific prohibitions include imposing eligibility criteria that screen out individuals with disabilities, failing to make reasonable modifications in policies, practices, or procedures where necessary to provide goods and services, and failing to remove architectural barriers where such removal is readily achievable, or to provide auxiliary aids and services such as captioning absent undue burden or fundamental alteration. The Department of Justice’s 2010 ADA Title III regulations (28 C.F.R. part 36) and the 2010 ADA Standards for Accessible Design implement these statutory requirements for new construction, alterations, and barrier removal at assembly areas.[^62][^6][^63][^64][^5]

### 5.2 Ticketing and Accessible Seating (DOJ 2010 Standards)

The DOJ’s 2010 Title III regulations include detailed ticketing rules at 28 C.F.R. 36.302(f), defining “accessible seating” as wheelchair spaces and companion seats that comply with sections 221 and 802 of the 2010 Standards, and imposing requirements on how such seats are sold and managed. The regulation requires venues to make tickets for accessible seating available for purchase during the same sales phases and through the same channels as other seats; to identify accessible seats on seating maps and materials to the same degree of specificity; and to sell accessible seating at all price levels offered to the general public.[^65][^66][^67]

Under 28 C.F.R. 36.302(f)(4), for each ticket for a wheelchair space purchased by or for an individual with a disability, a public accommodation must make available for purchase up to three additional tickets for companion seats contiguous in the same row, where available, and is not required to provide more than three contiguous seats per wheelchair space. DOJ interpretive guidance notes that accessible seating may only be released to the general public when all non-accessible tickets (or all tickets in a given price category or section) have been sold, and that venues must honor tickets purchased on the secondary market by patrons who need accessible seating where comparable accessible seats are available at the time of the event.[^68][^66][^65]

### 5.3 Seating in Assembly Areas and Companion Seating Dispersion

Section 221 of the 2010 ADA Standards specifies scoping for wheelchair spaces and companion seats in assembly areas, including dispersal across different viewing angles and price categories for stadiums and arenas. DOJ’s assembly-area commentary at 28 C.F.R. 36.308 explains that accessible seating must be provided in each area with distinct services or amenities (such as club seating areas with different food service levels) and that, where accessible seating cannot be provided in every specialty seating area due to infeasibility, equivalent amenities must be provided at other accessible locations without additional charge.[^6][^64][^68]

The DOJ commentary also clarifies that large assembly areas must design and alter facilities to disperse wheelchair spaces and companion seats across all levels served by accessible routes, and around fields of play when seating encircles the performance area, thereby preventing segregation of wheelchair users into a single end zone or upper deck. Stadium-style movie theatres have additional requirements that wheelchair spaces and companion seats be located on risers or cross‑aisles in the stadium section to provide comparable lines of sight.[^69][^64][^68][^6]

### 5.4 Service Animal Policies

The 2010 Title II and III regulations define “service animal” narrowly as a dog (and in limited circumstances a miniature horse) that is individually trained to do work or perform tasks for a person with a disability, and provide detailed rules on service animal access in places of public accommodation. Section 36.302(c) states that public accommodations must modify policies to permit individuals with disabilities to be accompanied by their service animals in all areas where the public is normally allowed, and that staff may ask only two questions: whether the animal is required because of a disability and what work or task it has been trained to perform, without demanding documentation or inquiring into the nature of the disability.[^70][^71][^72]

DOJ commentary explains that public accommodations may not impose surcharges for admitting service animals, though they may charge for damage if the business normally charges other customers for similar damage, and that they are not required to supervise or care for service animals. Venues may exclude a service animal that is out of control and the handler does not take effective action to control it, or if the animal is not housebroken, but must still offer the individual with a disability the opportunity to obtain goods or services without the animal present.[^73][^74][^71][^72]

### 5.5 Website Accessibility and Online Ticketing

Although the ADA statute does not mention websites explicitly, DOJ guidance released in March 2022 reiterates that Title III’s general nondiscrimination and effective communication requirements apply to public-facing websites and online services of places of public accommodation, including online ticketing platforms. DOJ’s “Guidance on Web Accessibility and the ADA” states that businesses must ensure their web content is accessible to people with disabilities and identifies common barriers (such as lack of alt text, poor color contrast, lack of keyboard navigation, missing captions, and inaccessible online forms) while referencing Web Content Accessibility Guidelines (WCAG) and Section 508 standards as helpful, though not formally mandated, benchmarks.[^75][^76][^77]

Legal analyses of the DOJ guidance observe that DOJ appears to have returned to its earlier position that all customer‑facing websites, including those of web‑only businesses, are subject to Title III obligations, and that enforcement actions often incorporate WCAG 2.0 or 2.1 Level AA criteria by settlement. Venue operators therefore increasingly incorporate WCAG‑based accessibility requirements into website and ticketing vendor contracts, including alt text, captions for video content, keyboard focus order, error identification, accessible CAPTCHAs, and mechanisms for users to report accessibility issues.[^78][^76][^75]

## 6. Intellectual Property and Venue Operations

### 6.1 Public Performance Rights for Music in Venues

Under 17 U.S.C. 106, copyright owners have the exclusive right to publicly perform their works, including musical compositions, and public performance includes playing music at any place open to the public or where a substantial number of persons outside a normal family circle is gathered. Legal and industry guidance stresses that virtually any public venue that plays popular music—whether via live bands, DJs, background systems, or broadcasts—must obtain public performance licenses from performing rights organizations (PROs) such as ASCAP, BMI, and SESAC.[^79][^80][^81]

ASCAP and BMI offer blanket licenses that allow venues to publicly perform any compositions in their repertories, with license fees based on business type, venue capacity, ticket revenue, or music usage patterns. SESAC similarly provides non‑exclusive licenses granting the right to publicly perform all works in its catalog at specified premises, with license fees typically assessed on a semi-annual or annual basis and limited territorially to the United States and its territories.[^82][^83][^84][^85]

### 6.2 Need for Multiple PRO Licenses and Liability Exposure

Because ASCAP, BMI, and SESAC represent different catalogs of songwriters and publishers, licenses from one or two organizations do not authorize public performance of works represented by the others, and many venues therefore obtain licenses from all three PROs to achieve broad coverage. SESAC explains that businesses that have licenses only with ASCAP and BMI can still infringe SESAC‑represented works performed in their establishments and that the owner or operator of a business can be held liable for unlicensed public performances even when the music is provided by a band or DJ.[^80][^85][^79]

Case studies of enforcement actions show that venues that fail to obtain or maintain PRO licenses may be sued for copyright infringement and face statutory damages, even when they have previously held licenses but stopped paying fees. For stadiums and arenas, licensing responsibilities may be split contractually between the facility operator and third-party concessionaires or team organizations, but from a copyright perspective any entity that “performs” or causes the performance of music publicly can face joint and several liability unless licenses are in place.[^81][^79][^80]

### 6.3 Broadcast, Streaming, and Recording Rights

Public performance licenses obtained from PROs generally cover on‑premises live and recorded performances but do not grant synchronization rights for pairing music with video, mechanical rights for reproducing sound recordings, or rights to stream performances over the internet, which require separate licenses from music publishers, record labels, or collective licensing bodies. BMI guidance notes that venues or producers who record or stream performances may need additional licenses beyond the venue’s public performance blanket, particularly if concerts are streamed online or incorporated into audiovisual productions.[^83][^86][^81]

Television and radio broadcast of games or concerts from venues are normally subject to network or league licensing arrangements for both compositions and master recordings, which may involve the Copyright Royalty Board’s statutory licenses for certain digital audio transmissions, though these regimes primarily affect broadcasters and digital services rather than venue operators. Venue contracts with promoters and leagues often allocate responsibility for securing broadcast and recording rights, including any necessary union clearances for recorded performances by musicians and technicians.[^81]

### 6.4 Naming Rights, Trademarks, and Branding

Venue naming rights agreements are complex commercial contracts granting a sponsor the right to have a venue bear its brand in exchange for substantial consideration, often tied to multi‑year terms and performance metrics. While there is no single statute governing naming rights, trademark law under the Lanham Act requires registrants and licensees to control quality and prevent consumer confusion, and naming rights agreements typically grant the sponsor trademark license rights and control over logo usage in venue signage, tickets, and marketing materials.[^16][^81]

Trademark guidance for businesses emphasizes that venue operators should register their own venue names and logos (where not controlled by a naming rights partner) and ensure that any co‑branding with teams, leagues, or sponsors is governed by written trademark licenses specifying territory, duration, quality control standards, and enforcement mechanisms. Failure to manage trademark usage can lead to infringement claims or dilution, especially where third-party events at the venue use unapproved logos or imply endorsements not granted in contracts.[^81]

## 7. Practical Compliance Takeaways for Large Venues

### 7.1 Insurance and TRIA

Large venues should negotiate master CGL and umbrella programs with limits consistent with contractual and lender expectations (often 5 million per occurrence plus excess), ensure owners and key stakeholders are added as additional insureds by endorsement, and maintain a robust certificate tracking process for promoters, vendors, and contractors. Venues should review terrorism risk appetites and consider purchasing TRIA-backed terrorism endorsements on property and liability lines, especially for high-profile or urban venues, and document policyholder acceptances or rejections according to TRIA disclosure requirements.[^8][^9][^1][^7][^3][^17]

### 7.2 Labor, Unions, and Workers’ Compensation

Compliance programs should map all venue job roles to appropriate workers’ compensation class codes and ensure that multi-state touring employees are covered under policies that reflect extraterritorial and reciprocity rules for each performance jurisdiction. Venue operators should maintain a matrix of union jurisdictions (IATSE, UNITE HERE, Teamsters, building trades) by venue and task type, incorporate union work-rule obligations into event production planning, and coordinate with public works and procurement teams to ensure Davis–Bacon prevailing wage compliance on publicly funded construction and renovation projects.[^18][^42][^38][^23][^44][^53]

### 7.3 ADA and Accessibility

To meet ADA Title III obligations, venues should align new construction and alterations with the 2010 ADA Standards for assembly areas, including dispersion of wheelchair and companion seating, equivalent viewing angles, and accessible routes to all seating levels. Operational policies must implement DOJ ticketing and service-animal rules, ensure staff are trained on accessible ticket sales and seating relocations, and require website and online ticketing platforms to follow current DOJ web accessibility guidance and industry standards such as WCAG 2.1 Level AA.[^66][^76][^77][^64][^6]

### 7.4 Intellectual Property and Branding

Venue operators should centralize responsibility for music licensing with a designated department, maintain active blanket licenses with ASCAP, BMI, and SESAC, and ensure that promoter and tenant contracts allocate responsibility for additional music uses such as recording or streaming. Naming rights and branding strategies must be supported by trademark registrations and carefully drafted trademark licenses that control use of venue and sponsor marks on signage, tickets, merchandise, and digital platforms.[^85][^79][^81]

Overall, large-venue risk management requires integrated coordination among legal, risk, operations, HR, and marketing teams, supported by specialist brokers and outside counsel, to align insurance programs, labor practices, accessibility initiatives, and intellectual property management with the evolving regulatory environment through at least 2027.[^4][^56]
