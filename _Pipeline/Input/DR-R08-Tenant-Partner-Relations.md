# Tenant and Partner Relations in Multi-Use Venues

## Executive Summary

Tenant and partner relations represent one of the most operationally complex domains in public assembly venue management, requiring the venue operator to act simultaneously as landlord, service provider, booking coordinator, and governance agent for multiple parties with overlapping — and sometimes competing — interests. The relationship structures differ fundamentally by venue type: arenas and stadiums are anchored by professional sports franchises operating under formal license agreements; convention centres manage a fluid mix of show organizers, general service contractors (GSCs), and destination marketing organizations (DMOs); performing arts centres balance subsidized resident companies against commercial rental presenters; and fairgrounds mediate between agricultural societies, exhibition operators, and seasonal lessees. Across all types, a defining structural shift of the past decade is toward vertical integration — where operators like Live Nation, AEG, and Oak View Group simultaneously own venues, promote events, and control ticketing — creating new governance tensions between theoretical open competition and real-world booking leverage.

## Section 1: Anchor Tenant Agreement Frameworks

### License vs. Lease: The Defining Legal Distinction

The single most consequential structural choice in a sports venue anchor tenant agreement is whether the arrangement is characterized as a license or a lease. A lease conveys a possessory real property interest — the tenant has exclusive control, can exclude the landlord during the term, and the relationship is governed by landlord-tenant law including security-of-tenure protections. A license conveys only a contractual permission to use — no property interest transfers, the licensor retains operational control, and the agreement is terminated by its own terms rather than by eviction proceedings. The industry has moved decisively toward license models over the past 25 years, primarily because they preserve operator control of non-game-day scheduling, enable the venue to generate revenue from other events without tenant interference, and avoid landlord-tenant statutory protections that would complicate enforcement.

The Madison Square Garden/New York Knicks Arena License Agreement, dated April 15, 2020, is among the most detailed publicly available examples of a license-model anchor tenant agreement. The agreement explicitly states that it "shall be construed as a grant of a license by Licensor to the Knicks and the Knicks' Personnel and Guests, and shall not operate to vest in the Knicks any ownership or leasehold interest, or other real estate interest, in the Arena". The 35-year term (April 2020–June 2055) provides substantial stability while avoiding the landlord-tenant framework. The Knicks pay a fixed license fee that started at approximately $21.8M (prorated) and escalates at 3% annually — a structure that transfers the revenue risk of good seasons and poor seasons away from the base facility payment entirely onto the tenant.[^1]

### Revenue Allocation in Arena License Agreements

Revenue allocation in anchor tenant agreements follows a consistent logic: the team controls revenues that derive from team identity (tickets, team merchandise, broadcasting rights, team-specific sponsorships), while the operator controls revenues that derive from venue ownership (premium seating licenses, naming rights, F&B concessions, non-team programming). In practice, the boundary is negotiated extensively, with both parties receiving shares of "game-day shared" assets.

The MSG/Knicks agreement illustrates these splits across major revenue streams:[^1]

| Revenue Stream | Knicks (Tenant) Control | MSG Arena (Operator) Control | Shared/Split |
|---|---|---|---|
| General admission tickets | 100% — Knicks sell and retain all revenue | None | None |
| Suite licenses | Percentage share on game nights | Exclusive licensing and operations for all events | Revenue-split on mixed-event suites |
| F&B concessions | Revenue share (%) | Exclusive operations management | Yes — share % confidential |
| Team merchandise | ~majority of net revenue | In-arena sales and operations | Licensor retains commission |
| Non-team merchandise | None | 100% | None |
| Signage — courtside/team assets | 100% | None | None |
| Signage — arena game shared | Revenue share (%) | Exclusive management | Yes |
| Naming rights | Revenue share (%) | Exclusive licensing | Yes |
| Broadcasting rights | 100% | None (provides facilities only) | None |

An important practical comparator is the Oklahoma City arrangement at Paycom Center, where the city (owner), Thunder (anchor tenant), and Legends/ASM Global (venue manager) operate as a three-party structure. The Thunder retains all ticket, concessions, merchandise, and premium seating license revenue from its business at the arena, as well as building naming rights. The base management fee paid to Legends/ASM is $350,000 annually, with an 8% incentive on adjusted gross income over $11M and a compensation cap of 16% of gross adjusted income. The Thunder pays $58,000 per home game to Legends/ASM for game-day services (increasing 3% annually for the first four years).[^2]

### Capital Expenditure Responsibility

The allocation of capital expenditure is a primary source of dispute in anchor tenant agreements and typically follows three principles: (1) the venue operator bears responsibility for maintaining the facility to the agreed "Standard" (typically defined as first-class or peer-comparable); (2) alterations required solely to comply with new league rules are borne by the tenant; and (3) capital improvements that generate additional premium seating revenue are shared or separately governed. In the MSG/Knicks agreement, the licensor bears responsibility for maintaining the arena to NBA arena standards in major U.S. markets, but the Knicks must fund any capital changes required solely by new NBA rule enactments after the commencement date.[^1]

### Multi-Tenant Venue Priority and Scheduling

Multi-tenant venues hosting both NBA and NHL franchises face the most intensive scheduling governance challenges, as both seasons run from October through June with significant overlap. NBA league rules give NBA teams contractual priority in shared arenas, a provision embedded in arena license agreements with both tenants. The NBA and NHL work intensively together to coordinate scheduling, particularly for cities with both franchises sharing a facility. Physical infrastructure solutions have emerged to reduce operational friction: the Utah Delta Center is undergoing a major renovation to install a convertible lower bowl system that can shift the seating by nearly 12 feet, allowing the venue to seamlessly transition between basketball and hockey configurations. The Washington Wizards/Capitals' Capital One Arena features what is described as the largest game-day locker room compound in the NBA in an arena where the practice facility is not attached — three times the size of previous accommodations — demonstrating how multi-sport venue operators invest in back-of-house infrastructure to satisfy both anchor tenants.[^3][^4][^5][^6]

## Section 2: Promoter and Touring Production Relationships

### Core Deal Structures

The relationship between venue operators and concert promoters is fundamentally a risk allocation conversation. Three primary deal structures define the market:[^7][^8]

**Flat Rental (Hard Ticket):** The venue charges the promoter a fixed rental fee regardless of attendance or revenue performance. The venue retains concessions, parking, and service charge revenues. The promoter's only revenue source is ticket sales, meaning the promoter absorbs 100% of the show risk. This is the standard model at independent and third-party venues — the venue generates predictable income while the promoter bets on their ability to fill seats.

**Co-Promotion:** Multiple parties divide costs, responsibilities, and profits. A local promoter might handle venue logistics and community marketing while a national promoter provides financial resources and artist relationships. Profit splits are negotiated based on investment level and responsibilities — one partner might handle venue costs and production while another manages marketing and ticketing. This model has enabled shows at venues and budgets that would be impossible for single promoters.[^7]

**Four-Wall Deal:** The venue "rents all four walls" to the promoter, including equipment, staff, and infrastructure. The promoter effectively operates the building for the event, bearing all costs and retaining most revenues. This gives maximum flexibility to touring productions but requires sophisticated promoters capable of full venue operations.

Live Nation, as the world's largest promoter, operates across all three models depending on whether it is using its own venues or third-party facilities. At its owned venues (377 globally as of 2024), Live Nation primarily operates in a co-promotion/internal model, generating revenue from concessions, parking, premium seating, rental income, and ticket service charges.[^8]

### The Advance and Settlement Process

Concert financial management follows a standard workflow of two key financial checkpoints:[^9][^10]

**The Advance:** Before the show, the promoter provides the artist management with a "preliminary settlement" estimating ticket revenue, deposits received, and anticipated expenses. This sets expectations and is typically provided 24–72 hours before showtime.

**The Settlement:** Post-show, the promoter's finance team reconciles actual ticket revenue against all expenses with the artist's tour manager and issues payment per the signed deal. Components include: artist fee (guaranteed amount), venue rental, production costs, marketing expenses, staffing, and any promoter profit or loss. When numbers don't align with expectations, relationships strain — meaning accurate documentation of every revenue and cost category during the event is operationally critical.[^11][^9]

For venue operators, the settlement process requires real-time visibility into ticketing data, concessions revenue attribution, and any ancillary charges. Technology platforms that unify ticketing, POS, and operations data have become central to dispute-free settlement.[^12]

### Vertical Integration: Live Nation and AEG

The vertical integration of venue ownership, concert promotion, and ticketing has fundamentally altered the power dynamics in the promoter/venue relationship. Live Nation operates a closed ecosystem in which an artist represented by Live Nation is promoted by Live Nation, performs at a Live Nation venue, and sells tickets through Ticketmaster — with every revenue layer captured within the same corporate entity. With contracts spanning over 400 artists, ownership of 377 venues, and 620 million+ tickets annually, Live Nation's competitive position makes disintermediation commercially difficult for independent venues.[^13]

The U.S. Department of Justice reached a tentative settlement with Live Nation in early 2026 requiring the company to divest 13 exclusive booking agreements at amphitheaters, open those venues to all promoters, cap service fees at 15%, allow venues to choose non-exclusive ticketing arrangements, and pay up to $280M to states. The settlement extends Live Nation's DOJ consent decree by eight years and, if finalized, represents the most significant structural intervention in the live entertainment market in the company's history.[^14][^15]

AEG operates a parallel model — AEG Facilities manages venues, AEG Presents promotes concerts — and following the Legends/ASM Global acquisition, AEG structured a deal to provide concerts and other content at ASM Global-managed venues, creating a cross-platform booking relationship between previously competing operator networks.[^16]

The OVG/Ticketmaster kickback scandal, which emerged from the DOJ's investigation of the ASM/Legends merger, revealed that Oak View Group received undisclosed payments from Ticketmaster (reportedly approaching $20M) in exchange for directing OVG venue clients toward exclusive Ticketmaster agreements. OVG CEO Tim Leiweke was indicted on bid-rigging charges related to the Moody Center arena contract, though OVG itself resolved the matter without admission of fault. These developments underscore the systemic conflicts of interest inherent in vertically integrated venue management, particularly where the operator controls both booking and ticketing access.[^17][^18]

## Section 3: Convention Centre Partner Ecosystem

### The Show Organizer Relationship

The convention centre's primary client is the show organizer — an association, trade organization, government body, or commercial event company that contracts space, services, and logistics for a defined event. The show organizer is simultaneously a client (paying rent, F&B minimums, and service fees) and a partner (driving economic impact that justifies the convention centre's public subsidy). This dual nature creates a structural tension, particularly at publicly owned facilities, between pricing events for profitability and pricing to win economically impactful business.

Ontario's Auditor General examined this tension directly in a December 2023 value-for-money audit of the Metropolitan Toronto Convention Centre (MTCC) and Ottawa Convention Centre (OCC), both Crown agencies of the Ontario government. The audit found that both centres were making operational decisions that favoured their own financial profitability rather than their legislative mandates to "promote and develop tourism and industry in Ontario". Since 2018, the centres had collectively lost 97 events because their bid prices were set too high, missing an estimated $52 million in potential economic impact for the province. The OCC outsources most F&B services to Aramark, which employs 230 staff including chefs and service staff, while the OCC's own core team numbers only about 18 full-time staff.[^19]

The lead times in convention booking are operationally unusual: large international conventions are booked three to five years in advance, and some of the largest events are committed eight or more years out. This creates a multi-year pipeline that functions as committed revenue and demands governance structures with long planning horizons. A negative experience at one event can eliminate a rotating convention's return for a decade or more.[^19]

### General Service Contractors and Official Contractor Models

The GSC ecosystem sits between show organizers and exhibitors, providing the physical installation and logistics services that make trade shows operable. Major GSCs — Freeman, GES (now operating within merged Informa/Emerald structures), and Shepard — trace their origins to the early 20th century and provide: material handling (drayage), booth installation and teardown, electrical services, AV, furniture rental, and exhibitor display services.[^20]

Two contractor models exist at convention centres:

**Exclusive/Official Contractor:** The show organizer (or venue) designates a single GSC as the exclusive provider for specific services — most commonly material handling. Shepard, for example, is contracted as the exclusive provider for material handling at some shows. This simplifies logistics, creates accountability, and provides the venue and organizer with predictable pricing and labor coordination. The tradeoff is that exhibitors pay whatever the exclusive contractor charges and have no competitive alternative.[^21]

**Preferred/Approved Contractor:** The show organizer establishes a list of approved vendors, designating one as "official" while permitting Exhibitor Appointed Contractors (EACs) — contractors hired directly by exhibitors — to operate under specified conditions. EACs must typically register with the show organizer, provide current insurance certificates, and comply with all venue and show rules. This model increases exhibitor flexibility and drives pricing competition among contractors but multiplies the number of vendors the show manager must coordinate on the show floor.[^22]

### CVB and Destination Marketing Organization Partnership

Convention and visitors bureaus (CVBs), also called Destination Marketing Organizations (DMOs), are the critical interface between venues and the national/international meeting market. At MTCC, Destination Toronto managed the bidding process for certain international events on behalf of the convention centre, acting as a co-selling partner rather than just a referring body. The relationship is symbiotic: the convention centre needs bookings to fulfill its mandate; the CVB needs the centre to be competitive in order to attract the delegates, hotel nights, and restaurant spending that justify tourism investment.[^19]

Destination Canada's International Convention Attraction Fund, launched in March 2024 as a three-year, $170M+ program, formalizes the public subsidy infrastructure that supports competitive bidding for large international conventions — providing bid support subsidies that Canadian centres can deploy when competing against US and European venues that operate with similar incentives. For venue operators working with CVB partners, the practical implication is that multi-year booking commitments made with CVB bid support may come with performance obligations or reporting requirements tied to the subsidy.[^23]

## Section 4: Third-Party Service Provider Management

### F&B Concessionaire Contract Structures

Food and beverage concessions are typically the largest outsourced service contract at any public assembly venue, and the structure of these agreements determines both operating economics and guest experience quality. The primary concessionaires operating in North American sports and entertainment venues — Levy, Aramark, Delaware North, and Sodexo Live (formerly Centerplate) — compete for long-duration anchor contracts that give them operational stability in exchange for upfront capital investment in equipment and branded infrastructure.

Delaware North generates approximately 40-45% of its total revenue from concessions and premium hospitality, with per-capita spending rising mid-to-high single digits as mobile/cashless payment adoption improves throughput and average ticket. Aramark signed a 20-year, $175M agreement with the MLB Athletics for F&B at their planned Las Vegas stadium, illustrating the length and capital commitment expected in new-build concessionaire partnerships.[^24][^25]

The OKC Paycom Center new arena deal provides detailed public insight into modern concessionaire contract structures. The arrangement required the concessionaire to: (1) pay Legends/ASM Global (venue operator) an annual payment of 1% of gross sales from the prior operating year into a capital investment fund for periodic repair and replacement of F&B equipment; and (2) be selected jointly by the city, Thunder, and Legends/ASM rather than by any one party alone. Levy had handled premium F&B at the arena since 2009 and took over full arena concessions in 2023. This joint selection model — municipality, sports franchise, and venue manager all participating — reflects how multi-party governance complicates what might otherwise be a straightforward operator decision.[^2]

Performance guarantees in F&B contracts typically address: minimum per-capita spending targets, food quality and temperature standards, staffing ratios, wait-time benchmarks, sanitation compliance scores, and capital investment commitments. Penalties for sustained underperformance may include revenue share reductions, cure periods, and ultimately early termination rights.

### Security, Cleaning, Parking, and Technology Provider Management

Security contractor management requires integration of the contractor's staff into the venue's incident command and emergency response framework — the contractor's supervisors must operate within the venue's security hierarchy, not parallel to it. Following the 2017 Manchester Arena bombing, security budgets for major touring events increased by as much as 25%, a cost that promoters now integrate into their financial models. For venue operators, this means security SLAs must account for scaled staffing during high-risk events and clear escalation protocols when contractor personnel encounter conditions outside their baseline deployment plan.[^9]

The IAVM Venue Executive Competency Standards identify managing service provider contracts (Domain 6.F) as a "High Risk/Very Important" competency requiring 3–4 years of proficiency. Core responsibilities include: evaluating service providers against RFP benchmarks, monitoring performance against expenditures, implementing corrective actions when performance falls below standard, and documenting issues for potential contract amendment.[^26][^1]

For technology and connectivity providers, particularly Distributed Antenna System (DAS) operators providing in-venue cellular coverage, the neutral host model has become standard: a third-party DAS integrator or the building owner operates the shared infrastructure, and individual carriers (AT&T, T-Mobile, Verizon) lease access to reach their subscribers. This shifts ownership of the system from any individual carrier to an independent host, aligning incentives for multi-carrier coverage while distributing infrastructure costs. Venue operators must negotiate carrier lease agreements, maintain minimum signal performance commitments, and coordinate upgrades when new spectrum bands require hardware changes — a responsibility that falls squarely in the shared infrastructure governance domain.[^27][^28]

### Service Provider Transition Management

Changing a major service provider — particularly an F&B concessionaire after a multi-year agreement — is one of the operationally highest-risk transitions in venue management. Key provisions that should be embedded in any outgoing contract include:[^29]

- **Termination transition period:** The outgoing provider continues operations at agreed rates and service levels during a specified handover period
- **Asset return:** Clear delineation of what equipment belongs to the venue versus the provider, with bill of sale or transfer procedures for provider-owned assets the venue wishes to purchase
- **Knowledge transfer:** The provider must transfer operational documentation, vendor relationships, inventory management records, and software access
- **IP licensing:** Critical software or proprietary processes developed during the contract may need to be licensed to ensure operational continuity
- **Personnel:** Whether the new provider has the right to recruit key personnel from the departing provider must be explicitly addressed — employee contracts may limit this
- **KPI continuity:** Performance standards must be maintained throughout the transition period, not suspended because of the changeover

Executing a three-way governance agreement between the venue, outgoing provider, and incoming provider during the transition period is a recognized best practice — it defines the roles and responsibilities of all parties during the most fragile operational window.[^30]

## Section 5: Shared Infrastructure and Cost Allocation

### Common Area Maintenance (CAM) Principles

Common area maintenance charges are the mechanism by which shared facility costs are allocated among multiple tenants. In arena and convention centre contexts, CAM typically covers: utilities for common areas, security for shared spaces, routine cleaning and maintenance of lobbies and corridors, insurance on shared infrastructure, and grounds maintenance. The standard calculation allocates costs proportionally to each tenant's share of gross leasable area:[^31]

\[\text{Tenant CAM} = \frac{\text{Tenant RSF}}{\text{Gross Leasable Area}} \times \text{Annual CAM Charge}\]

Capital expenditures are typically excluded from CAM calculations; they are handled through separate capital reserve funds or direct cost-sharing agreements. This distinction matters operationally because capital improvement decisions — a new scoreboard, HVAC upgrades, locker room renovations — require different governance processes than routine maintenance expenses. In multi-tenant venues, major capital improvements that benefit multiple tenants may require approval from all benefiting parties before proceeding, creating potential coordination challenges when tenant priorities diverge.[^31]

The MSG/Knicks agreement addresses capital allocation through explicit provisions: the licensor bears responsibility for maintaining the arena to the NBA standard at its cost, but the Knicks must fund any alterations required solely by new NBA rule enactments after the agreement's commencement. Alterations intended to generate additional premium seating revenues for both parties are governed by a separate joint revenue-sharing process.[^1]

### Technology Infrastructure Ownership and Governance

In modern multi-use venues, technology infrastructure — scoreboards, ribbon boards, Wi-Fi, POS networks, DAS cellular systems, broadcast infrastructure — represents both a significant capital investment and a source of governance complexity when multiple tenants expect priority access or control.

The practical norm is that the venue operator owns the core technology infrastructure (scoreboards, ribbon boards, building Wi-Fi, POS backbone) while tenants have defined usage rights during their occupancy windows. The OKC new arena model — where the concessionaire contributes 1% of annual gross sales to a capital fund for F&B equipment replacement — shows how the cost of technology maintenance can be shared through a tiered contribution model rather than a single owner-pays-all approach.[^2]

Data ownership has become a significant tension point in technology infrastructure governance, particularly for ticketing systems. In 2026, venues are increasingly demanding full customer data from ticketing platforms by default, rather than receiving limited aggregate reports. The OVG/Ticketmaster kickback case demonstrated how undisclosed financial relationships between venue operators and technology vendors can distort what should be arm's-length procurement decisions. Best practice now requires explicit contractual language establishing that customer purchase data belongs to the venue, not the ticketing platform.[^18][^32]

### Loading Dock and Staging Area Coordination

Back-of-house space allocation in multi-sport and multi-use venues requires a dedicated scheduling governance layer. Loading dock access, staging areas, locker rooms, and production spaces must be sequenced around multiple tenants' event calendars — compounded by the fact that touring productions, sports teams, corporate events, and cleaning/changeover crews may all require overlapping access windows. The IAVM notes that venues should work with tenants to establish which plans supersede others during shared operational periods — for example, when a PPE protocol or operational requirement from one tenant conflicts with another's procedures.[^33]

## Section 6: Partnership Governance Frameworks

### Governance Structures by Ownership Model

The fundamental governance dynamic in any venue is determined first by who owns the building and who is accountable for operations. Two primary models operate in North American venues:

**Publicly-Owned with Private Manager (most common for arenas and convention centres):** The city, county, authority, or Crown agency owns the asset; a private venue management company operates it under a management agreement. The Overland Park Convention Center model illustrates the standard structure: the city engages the operator as "an independent contractor to perform... management services," acting on the city's behalf and as agent for the city in operational matters, subject to the city's retained authority over significant decisions. The operator manages day-to-day operations, enters contracts in the city's name, and maintains all licenses and permits in the city's name. Upon termination, the city or its successor manager assumes responsibility for all existing bookings and contracts.[^34]

The Ontario Crown agency model (MTCC/OCC) extends this further: a legislated mandate defines the operational purpose; a board of directors (with provincial and municipal government appointees) provides governance oversight; the Ministry of Tourism, Culture and Sport reviews and approves annual business plans, issues mandate letters, and tables annual reports in the legislature. The auditor's critique — that the Ministry failed to ensure the centres operated in alignment with their tourism mandate rather than optimizing for their own financial profitability — illustrates the accountability gap that emerges when public oversight is insufficiently engaged with operational decisions.[^19]

**Privately-Owned by Operator or Owner-Operator:** AEG-owned venues (Staples Center predecessor, Kia Forum), Oak View Group's developing portfolio, and Live Nation's 377-venue network operate with the simplest governance structure: the owner makes operational decisions as owner, subject to tenant agreements but not to a public board. Accountability flows to investors rather than elected officials. The Saskatoon arena P3 negotiation provided a detailed public record of how a private management model is structured: OVG360's proposed 30-year contract would have seen total revenues of $411M, with OVG360 receiving $176M in management fees and the city retaining $235M, plus a tiered profit-sharing structure above $2M and $4M thresholds. City council ultimately rejected the private partnership in March 2026, preferring to retain direct public control.[^35]

### Joint Operating Committees

Multi-tenant venues and public authority/private operator venues typically establish Joint Operating Committees (JOCs) or equivalent governance bodies to manage shared operational decisions. In sports venues with multiple anchor tenants, a JOC commonly involves representatives from each franchise and the venue operator meeting regularly to coordinate scheduling, capital improvements, and service standards. Decision-making authority in JOCs typically follows a tiered structure: routine operational matters delegated to management level; capital expenditures above a threshold requiring committee approval; fundamental changes to the agreement requiring consent of all parties.

The IAVM CVE Competency Standards identify stakeholder engagement and management (Domain 11) as a core executive function, with specific competency 11.A — "Develop Tenant Management Operating Principles and Policies" — establishing the expectation that venue executives proactively set the framework for tenant relationships rather than responding ad hoc. Domain 11.C further requires executives to identify, cultivate, and manage all internal and external stakeholders — recognizing that the stakeholder map extends beyond tenants to include service providers, government bodies, and community partners.[^1]

### Business Reviews, KPIs, and SLA Compliance

Service provider relationships are governed through a performance monitoring cycle that combines formal SLAs (Service Level Agreements) — contractual minimum standards — with KPIs (Key Performance Indicators) used internally to track performance trends beyond the minimum threshold. SLAs define what the venue can enforce contractually (with penalties, credits, or cure periods); KPIs diagnose why performance is succeeding or failing.[^36]

For F&B concessionaires, common SLA metrics include: per-capita spending minimums, wait-time maximums (90 seconds is a common industry benchmark), food quality compliance scores, sanitation audit pass rates, and staff-to-patron ratios during peak event periods. For security contractors: response time standards, incident reporting timelines, staffing ratio compliance, and emergency protocol adherence. For facility maintenance: HVAC uptime percentages, restroom cleanliness scores (often verified by third-party inspection), and work order response/resolution times.

The IAVM PAC Standard Performance Reporting handbook provides a detailed framework of KPIs for performing arts centres, including event-level performance measures, organizational productivity metrics (Revenue per Patron, Revenue per Event, Occupancy Cost per Square Foot), and institutional financial health ratios. These tools allow PAC executives to benchmark performance against peer institutions and demonstrate public value to boards and funding bodies.[^37]

### Dispute Resolution Mechanisms

Anchor tenant license agreements typically specify a tiered dispute resolution process: operational escalation (management to management), executive escalation (executive director/CEO level), followed by formal mediation, and ultimately binding arbitration. The MSG/Knicks agreement specifies governing law and dispute resolution in Section 20.06, with the league's oversight rights acknowledged (NBA has rights to notice of and opportunity to cure Knicks defaults per Section 17.04) — an unusual third-party governance layer where the professional sports league serves as a co-enforcer of the facility agreement.[^1]

For public-sector venues with private managers, dispute escalation ultimately involves the public authority board or municipal council as the final decision-making body. This creates a longer decision timeline than private owner disputes but adds democratic accountability — and a political dimension — to operational disagreements that purely commercial parties would resolve faster.

### Brand Standards and Multi-Party Operations

When multiple parties operate within a single venue's branded identity — the venue brand, the anchor tenant team brand, service provider sub-brands, naming rights sponsor brand — brand standards governance becomes an explicit operational function. Performing arts centres, convention centres, and multi-use arenas all maintain brand standards documents that define signage specifications, communication protocols, and visual identity requirements for all parties operating in the space. These standards must be embedded in all tenant and service provider agreements, not just enforced through after-the-fact complaints.

## Section 7: Performing Arts Centres and Fairgrounds — Venue-Specific Dynamics

### Performing Arts Centre Resident Company Relationships

The performing arts centre resident company relationship is structurally distinct from commercial tenant models. The IAVM defines a resident company as "most often an arts organization whose rent is either fully or partially subsidized by the PAC," which may also benefit from PAC marketing and PR activities on its behalf. Resident company agreements typically include priority access to the PAC calendar — enabling the company to schedule its season before the calendar opens to commercial rental presenters. Ticketing arrangements and revenue splits vary significantly by institution.[^37]

In practice, resident company agreements function as hybrid arrangements between tenant and program partner. The K-W Symphony's arrangement at a mid-sized Canadian centre illustrates this: the Symphony commits to 50 annual use-days (rehearsals and performances) and receives special rental rates in exchange for that volume commitment. Commercial renters at the same venue pay 11% of gross ticket sales (net of HST) plus a capital reserve fund charge of $2.25 per ticket, or whichever is greater — a pricing model that cross-subsidizes resident companies and community programming.[^38]

From a scheduling and operations standpoint, PACs must balance resident companies (who expect priority and subsidized rates) against commercial rental presenters (who pay market rates and may generate more revenue per event) and their own centre-presented programming (which the PAC controls and profits from directly). The IAVM PAC handbook classifies events by presenter type for performance reporting purposes, distinguishing between center presentations, resident companies, non-profit rentals, and commercial rentals — each with different financial characteristics and governance relationships.[^37]

### Fairgrounds and Agricultural Society Operations

Fairgrounds present a particularly complex multi-party environment, hosting agricultural societies as anchor operators, seasonal exhibition tenants, commercial event promoters, and temporary ground-lease users for the same physical asset across a compressed seasonal calendar. The Alberta agricultural society model — documented in the Russell Agricultural Society's Equipment and Facilities Rental Agreement — demonstrates how fairground operators distinguish between organizational categories: commercial renters pay full rates, while not-for-profit organizations and board members may qualify for fee waivers. Customers at fairgrounds typically bear responsibility for all permits and licenses associated with their use.[^39]

Alberta's regional agricultural societies face increasing viability pressures, with rodeo and summer fair operators in mid-sized centres evaluating their long-term financial sustainability as costs rise and government funding remains flat. This creates a governance challenge for fairground operators who must balance their core agricultural society mission — often legislated — against the commercial event income required to fund facility maintenance and capital reserves.[^40]

## Section 8: Emerging Partnership Models

### Vertical Integration and Its Structural Consequences

The consolidation of the venue management, event promotion, and ticketing sectors into a small number of integrated operators represents the most significant structural shift in the industry over the past 15 years. The 2024 acquisition of ASM Global by Legends, valued at more than $2.4 billion, created a combined entity managing approximately 350 arenas, stadiums, and convention centres globally. AEG subsequently structured a deal to provide concerts and content at ASM Global-managed venues. Live Nation operates 377 owned venues globally with exclusive or preferred ticketing arrangements driving cross-platform revenue.[^41][^13][^16]

The practical consequence for publicly owned venues is that the selection of a private management company now carries significant booking policy implications — which promoters get access, which ticketing platform is used, and what terms artists and agents can negotiate for venue access. The OVG kickback case made explicit what has been an implicit concern: management companies may have undisclosed financial incentives to direct client venues toward specific technology vendors, booking relationships, or promoters that benefit the management company's broader business interests rather than the public venue's.[^18]

### Public-Private Partnerships (P3 Models) for New Venues

New arena and entertainment district development increasingly employs formal P3 structures in which the public sector provides land, public financing, and long-term operating subsidy while a private operator provides management expertise, development capital, and booking relationships. The Saskatoon arena district negotiation — where OVG360 proposed a 30-year management contract in exchange for $15M in development funding and a fee structure capturing up to 15% of profits above $4M annually — illustrates both the appeal and the governance complexity of P3 arrangements. City council's March 2026 rejection of that proposal reflects continued public sector ambivalence about ceding operational control to private operators, even when projected revenues are favorable.[^35]

Mobile, Alabama's parallel partnership — contracting OVG and Live Nation to build a new civic arena and operate the Arthur Outlaw Convention Center and Saenger Theater simultaneously — demonstrates how full-service operators can bundle venue development, management, and programming relationships into a single municipal partnership. This bundling creates operational efficiencies but concentrates the municipality's dependency on a single operator relationship.[^42]

### Community Benefit Agreements

Community benefit agreements (CBAs) have become standard features of new public arena development since the 2010s, embedding obligations on venue operators and anchor tenants related to local hiring, living wage standards, small business procurement preferences, and community programming commitments. These agreements are typically negotiated between the municipality (representing community interests), the venue developer/operator, and anchor tenants as a condition of public subsidy. While the Calgary Stampede's specific CBA structure is noted as an exemplar, the general CBA framework requires venue operators to track, report on, and be accountable for commitments that extend beyond the operating metrics typically captured in management agreements.

### ESG and Sustainability Commitments as Partnership Infrastructure

The Climate Pledge Arena in Seattle — operated by OVG, anchored by the Seattle Kraken, and named through Amazon's naming rights deal — became the world's first net-zero carbon-certified arena in 2023. The arena's sustainability commitments span across all operational partners: OVG as operator manages the building's all-electric infrastructure and waste diversion; the Kraken as anchor tenant commits to team operational sustainability; and Amazon's naming rights agreement is explicitly linked to a corporate commitment to net-zero carbon by 2040. This represents a new model in which sustainability commitments are embedded as binding partnership terms rather than voluntary aspirational statements.[^43][^44]

Oak View Group subsequently developed the GOAL (Green Operations and Advanced Leadership) sustainability platform, drawing from the Climate Pledge Arena experience, to provide a replicable framework for other arenas and stadiums. For venue operators and their anchor tenants, the practical implication is that ESG commitments are increasingly moving from branding exercises into operational governance — with specific measurable targets for energy consumption, waste diversion, single-use plastics, and carbon emissions embedded in management agreements, naming rights deals, and anchor tenant licenses.[^45]

***

## Operational Implications: Key Principles Across Venue Types

The relationship/contract/governance layer between venue operators, tenants, promoters, and service partners distills into several cross-cutting operational principles:

1. **Legal structure determines operational control.** The license vs. lease distinction is not semantic — it determines who has authority over scheduling, alterations, access, and termination. Venue operators seeking to maximize programming flexibility should default to license models for all anchor tenant arrangements.

2. **Revenue splits must be defined for every monetizable asset.** Vague language about "shared" revenues becomes a source of dispute. Every category — signage, digital boards, suites, F&B, parking, naming rights, data — requires a specific allocation rule, and "game-day" vs. "non-game-day" distinctions should be explicit throughout.

3. **Public ownership creates accountability structures that private ownership does not.** Publicly-owned venues with private managers operate under a tripartite governance structure — public authority, private manager, and anchor tenants — that requires explicit documentation of each party's authority limits and escalation paths. The failure of Ministry oversight of MTCC and OCC demonstrates the risk when public principals disengage from operational accountability.[^19]

4. **Technology vendor relationships carry undisclosed conflict-of-interest risk.** The OVG/Ticketmaster kickback case established that management companies may have financial incentives to direct clients toward specific platforms. Venues should require management companies to disclose all third-party financial relationships and should independently evaluate technology procurement decisions rather than delegating them entirely to the management company.[^18]

5. **Transition management for service providers requires dedicated planning.** Changing a major concessionaire, security provider, or cleaning contractor mid-contract or at renewal is operationally equivalent to a partial venue reopening. Best practice requires contractual transition provisions, three-way governance agreements during handover, and explicit IP and personnel transfer provisions.[^30][^29]

6. **Vertical integration in promotion/management is reshaping competitive access.** The Live Nation/Legends/OVG consolidation means that the choice of venue management company now implies booking preferences, ticketing platforms, and hospitality vendor relationships. Public-sector owners must evaluate management company selection with full awareness of the downstream booking implications and should include open-booking provisions and ticketing platform flexibility in management agreements.[^13][^14]
