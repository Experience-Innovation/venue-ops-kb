## 1. Mass Gathering Medical Staffing Evidence
### 1.1 The "Saunders et al." Citation
The "Saunders et al." staffing framework in the KB maps to two separate authoritative sources that have been conflated. The foundational reference is:

**Sanders AB, Criss E, Steckl P, Meislin HW, Raife J, Allen D. "An analysis of medical care at mass gatherings." *Annals of Emergency Medicine*. 1986 May;15(5):515–9. PubMed ID: 3963529.**[^1]

This 1986 paper surveyed 15 Arizona facilities providing public events and is the origin of the response-time staffing standard that is widely attributed to "Sanders/Saunders": Basic Life Support (BLS) first aid responder within **4 minutes**, Advanced Life Support (ALS) within **8 minutes**, and transport to a medical facility within **30 minutes** of a patient becoming ill. These intervals are cited in the NIH StatPearls chapter on EMS mass gatherings as the foundational benchmarks.[^2][^3]

The physician-to-attendee ratio of approximately "12 physicians per 50,000 attendees" appears in EMRA's 2015 review attributing it to "Sanders, et al." and the broader NAEMSP Resource Document. The most comprehensive current statement of staffing ratios comes from:[^4]

**Schwartz B, Nafziger S, Milsten A, Luk J, Yancey A. "Mass Gathering Medical Care: Resource Document for the National Association of EMS Physicians Position Statement." *Prehospital Emergency Care*. 2015;19(4):559–68. PubMed ID: 26270473.**[^5][^2]

| Staff Category | Recommended Ratio | Source |
|---|---|---|
| Physician | 1 per 5,000–50,000 attendees | StatPearls/NAEMSP [^2] |
| Physician (alternate) | 1–2 per 50,000 attendees | PMC review [^6] |
| Nurse | 1 per 2,600–15,000 attendees | StatPearls/NAEMSP [^2] |
| EMT | 1 per 2,600–65,000 attendees | StatPearls/NAEMSP [^2] |
| Paramedic | 2 (or 1 paramedic + 1 EMT) per 10,000 | PMC review [^6] |
| Basic First Aid | 1 per 1,000 attendees | PMC review [^6] |
| BLS response time target | ≤4 minutes | Sanders 1986 / NAEMSP [^4][^2] |
| ALS response time target | ≤8 minutes | Sanders 1986 / NAEMSP [^4][^2] |
| Transport-to-hospital target | ≤30 minutes | Sanders 1986 / NAEMSP [^2] |

**Note on "Saunders" vs. "Sanders":** The KB entry references "Saunders et al." — this is almost certainly a spelling variation of **Sanders AB et al. (1986)**, the Ann Emerg Med paper. No independently authored "Saunders" mass gathering framework could be verified. The EMRA review article and the StatPearls chapter both use "Sanders" (one 'd').
### 1.2 The NIH StatPearls Chapter
**Wolin J, Friedman M. "EMS Mass Gatherings." In: *StatPearls* [Internet]. Treasure Island, FL: StatPearls Publishing; 2023 Jan–. Updated: October 26, 2023. NCBI Bookshelf ID: NBK597369. Available at: https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/books/NBK597369/**[^2][^7]

This is the current NIH-hosted synthesis chapter. It cites the Sanders 1986 paper for the 0.5–2 per 1,000 patient presentation rate benchmark (reference  in the chapter's reference list) and the Locoh-Donou 2013 paper for the 95.97% mild presentations statistic (reference ).[^8][^9][^2]
### 1.3 The 95.97% Mild Presentations Statistic
This specific figure originates from a single, identifiable study:

**Locoh-Donou S, Yan G, Welcher M, Berry T, O'Connor RE, Brady WJ. "Mass-gathering medicine: a descriptive analysis of a range of mass-gathering event types." *American Journal of Emergency Medicine*. 2013 May;31(5):843–6. DOI: 10.1016/j.ajem.2013.01.016. PubMed ID: 23453125.**[^10][^11][^12]

The study analyzed EMS records from October 24, 2009 to August 27, 2011 at a southeastern US university, covering 79 mass gathering events with 670 patient presentations and cumulative attendance of 917,307 persons. Severity categories were: mild (non-life threat, no transport), moderate (transport required), and severe (life-threatening with transport). The finding: **95.97% of cases were mild**; 27 cases required hospital transport; 3 were life-threatening.[^13][^10]
### 1.4 Patient Presentation Rate Benchmark (0.5–2 per 1,000)
The 0.5–2 per 1,000 spectators benchmark is supported by three independent studies:

| Author | Year | Journal | Finding | Citation |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Arbon P, Bridgewater FH, Smith C | 2001 | *Prehosp Disaster Med* 16(3):150–8 | PPR = 0.992/1,000 across 201 Australian mass gatherings (12M+ total attendees) | [^14] |
| Milsten AM, Maguire BJ, Bissell RA, Seaman KG | 2002 | *Prehosp Disaster Med* 17(3):151–62 | Literature review; confirmed 0.5–2 per 1,000 as the observed range across diverse events | [^15][^16] |
| Sanders AB et al. | 1986 | *Ann Emerg Med* 15(5):515–9 | Foundational US survey; established baseline reference range | [^1][^2] |

## 2. START/SALT Triage Comparison
### 2.1 START Triage — Origin and Citation
| Field | Data |
|---|---|
| Full name | Simple Triage and Rapid Treatment |
| Developed | 1983 |
| Origin organizations | Newport Beach Fire and Marine Department and Hoag Hospital, Newport Beach, California |
| Key co-developer | Victoria Cleary, EMS Manager, Newport Beach Fire Department |
| Primary classification source | CHEMM (Chemical Hazards Emergency Medical Management), U.S. DHHS, at chemm.hhs.gov/startadult.htm |
| 1996 modification | Benson et al. substituted radial pulse for capillary refill, improving accuracy in cold temperatures |

**Primary source citation:** CHEMM (U.S. HHS/ASPR). "START Adult Triage Algorithm." Available: https://chemm.hhs.gov/startadult.htm. References: (1) Benson M et al. modification, 1996; (2) Newport Beach/Hoag 1983 origin.[^17]

Additional confirmations: Wikipedia (PMID-linked article); CEHSA EHS document; EMS1 review article. START is described as the most widely used mass casualty triage system in the United States.[^18][^19][^20]

**Four categories:** Deceased/Expectant (Black), Immediate (Red), Delayed (Yellow), Walking Wounded/Minor (Green). Assessment criteria: Respiration → Perfusion → Mental Status.
### 2.2 SALT Triage — Origin and Citation
| Field | Data |
|---|---|
| Full name | Sort, Assess, Lifesaving Interventions, Treatment/Transport |
| Developed | 2008 |
| Origin | CDC-sponsored expert working group |
| Endorsing organizations | ACEP, ACS Committee on Trauma, American Trauma Society, NAEMSP, National Disaster Life Support Education Consortium, STIPDA |

**Primary source citation:** "SALT mass casualty triage: concept endorsed by the American College of Emergency Physicians [et al.]" *Disaster Medicine and Public Health Preparedness*. 2008 Dec;2(4):245–6. DOI: 10.1097/DMP.0b013e31818d191e. PubMed ID: 19050431.[^21][^22]

**Published (full protocol document):** Available at University of Maryland Emergency Medicine (em.umaryland.edu/files/uploads/ems/salt_2008.pdf).[^23]

SALT is compliant with the Model Uniform Core Criteria (MUCC) for mass casualty triage, which is the proposed US national standard.[^24][^25][^26]
### 2.3 START vs. SALT Comparison
| Dimension | START | SALT |
|---|---|---|
| Origin year | 1983 | 2008 |
| Origin body | Newport Beach Fire Dept./Hoag Hospital | CDC-sponsored working group |
| Categories | 4 (Deceased, Immediate, Delayed, Minor) | 5 (Dead, Immediate, Delayed, Minimal, Expectant) |
| Lifesaving interventions before tagging? | No | Yes (hemorrhage control, airway, chest decompression) |
| Population scope | Originally adults | All ages, all hazards |
| MUCC compliance | No | Yes |
| Primary adoption | Widely used in US EMS systems | Increasingly mandated as national standard |
| Assessment sequence | RPM (Respirations, Perfusion, Mental status) | Global sort → individual assess → LSI → assign category |

**Where SALT is mandated:** SALT is endorsed by all major US national EMS organizations and is the recommended standard under the MUCC framework. Individual state and local EMS systems vary; SALT replaces START in jurisdictions that have adopted MUCC.[^24][^25][^26]

## 3. FEMA P-361 / ICC 500 Requirements for Assembly Occupancies
### 3.1 FEMA P-361 — Safe Rooms for Tornadoes and Hurricanes
**Publication:** FEMA P-361, *Safe Rooms for Tornadoes and Hurricanes: Guidance for Community and Residential Safe Rooms*, 3rd Edition, 2015. Published by Federal Emergency Management Agency.[^27][^28][^29]

**Key characteristics:**
- Guideline document, not an enforceable standard; uses "should/recommended" language[^27]
- **Becomes mandatory** only when FEMA grant funds are used to construct the safe room[^30][^27]
- Not referenced by the International Building Code (IBC) or International Residential Code (IRC)[^30]

**Design criteria:**
- Design wind speed: **250 mph** (3-second gust at 33 feet above ground in open terrain) for all geographic areas[^29][^31]
- Missile impact resistance: 15-pound 2×4 wood stud at **100 mph** horizontal (vertical surfaces) and **67 mph** (horizontal surfaces)[^31][^29]
- FEMA P-361 requires full compliance with ICC 500 plus additional siting and fire-resistance requirements[^30]
- Safe rooms provide "near-absolute" life-safety protection[^27]
### 3.2 ICC 500 — Standard for the Design and Construction of Storm Shelters
**Publication:** *ICC 500-2020: ICC/NSSA Standard for the Design and Construction of Storm Shelters*. International Code Council. ANSI-approved. Referenced in the 2021 IBC and 2021 IEBC.[^32][^8]

**Key characteristics:**
- Enforceable consensus standard; uses "shall/required" language[^27][^32]
- Wind speed requirements range from **130 mph to 250 mph** depending on the shelter's location within the tornado wind design zone[^27]
- Storm shelters provide "life safety" protection (vs. FEMA P-361's "near-absolute" protection)[^27]

**When ICC 500-compliant shelters are required (IBC Section 423):**
Storm shelters must meet ICC 500 when new structures are built inside the 250-mph tornado design wind speed zone for the following occupancy types:[^33][^32]

| Occupancy Type | Threshold | IBC Basis |
|---|---|---|
| Group E (K–12 schools) | Occupant load ≥50 | IBC Section 423 |
| 911 call stations | All | IBC Section 423 |
| Emergency operation centers | All | IBC Section 423 |
| Fire, rescue, ambulance, police stations | All | IBC Section 423 |

**Critical note for assembly venues:** ICC 500 and IBC Section 423 do **not** currently mandate storm shelters specifically for Group A assembly occupancies (arenas, stadiums, concert venues). The obligation applies primarily to Group E (schools), critical emergency facilities, and government emergency operations structures. Public assembly venue owners who voluntarily construct storm shelters must meet ICC 500-2020 standards if they seek FEMA grant funding.[^32][^33]

**ICC 500-2020 notable provisions:**
- **Section 108**: Owner must submit written statement of responsibility and emergency preparedness plan to the Authority Having Jurisdiction (AHJ) during the permit process[^32]
- **Section 113**: Annual evaluations of the storm shelter envelope and impact-protective systems[^32]
- **Section 305.3**: Expanded criteria for laydown, falling, and collapse hazards[^32]
- Roof live loads: 100 psf (tornado shelters); 50 psf (hurricane shelters)[^32]

## 4. Fire Watch Regulatory Standards — Code Section Reference
### 4.1 NFPA 101 Fire Watch Triggers
| Trigger Condition | Threshold | Code Section | Action Required |
|---|---|---|---|
| Fire alarm system out of service | >4 cumulative hours in 24-hr period | NFPA 101 Section **9.6.1.6** | Notify AHJ; evacuate or provide approved fire watch |
| Automatic sprinkler system out of service | >4 cumulative hours in 24-hr period | NFPA 101 Section **9.7.6.1** | Notify AHJ; evacuate or provide approved fire watch |
| Water-based fire protection system out of service | >10 hours in 24-hr period | NFPA 25 Section **15.5.2** | Evacuate, fire watch, temporary water supply, or approved ignition-source program |

**Source:** NFPA 101 Life Safety Code (2012 and 2018 editions), Sections 9.6.1.6 and 9.7.6.1. NFPA 25 (2017 edition), Section 15.5.2.[^34][^35][^36][^37][^38]

**Important clarification:** The 4-hour threshold is **cumulative**, not consecutive — the fire watch obligation is triggered when the sum of all outages within a 24-hour period exceeds 4 hours. Many AHJs independently apply stricter standards requiring a fire watch or standby firefighters if systems are down for even shorter periods.[^37][^39][^38]

**Definition of fire watch (NFPA 101 Section 3.3.108 / 3.3.91):** "The assignment of a person or persons to an area for the express purpose of notifying the fire department, the building occupants, or both of an emergency; preventing a fire from occurring; extinguishing small fires; or protecting the public from fire or life safety dangers."[^35][^38]
### 4.2 IFC Fire Watch Provisions
**International Fire Code (IFC) Section 901.7** — "Systems Out-of-Service" — is the primary IFC provision for fire protection system impairments. This section requires notification of the fire code official and mandates evacuation or fire watch when required fire protection systems are out of service. The IFC provision is jurisdiction-specific and is adopted by reference in state and local fire codes (e.g., New York State Fire Code Section 901.7, California Fire Code Section 901.7, New Jersey per N.J.A.C. 5:70-3).[^40][^41][^42][^43]

**IFC Section 3504.2** — Fire watch during hot work activities: fire watch must continue for **not less than 30 minutes** after the conclusion of hot work.[^38]

**Key IFC difference from NFPA:** NFPA 101 and NFPA 25 specify time-based thresholds (4 hours, 10 hours); IFC Section 901.7 in most editions requires fire watch or evacuation **immediately** when required systems are out of service, with the code official empowered to waive the requirement where there is no substantial life-safety risk.[^41]
### 4.3 NFPA 1126 Pyrotechnic Setback Distances
**Publication:** NFPA 1126, *Standard for the Use of Pyrotechnics Before a Proximate Audience*, 2025 Edition.

| Requirement | Distance / Standard | Code Section (2025 Ed.) |
|---|---|---|
| Minimum audience separation | 15 feet **or** 2× fallout radius (whichever is greater) | Section **8.4.1** |
| Concussion mortar setback (from all persons) | Minimum **25 feet** in secured area | Section **8.4.2** |
| No smoking zone | Within 25 feet | Section **8.5.5.1** |
| Trajectory of comets/mines | Not over audience | Section **8.2.10** |
| Airbursts over audience | Minimum height = 3× diameter of effect; no sparks within 15 ft of floor | Sections **8.2.13(1)** and **(2)** |

[^44][^45]

**Note on edition numbering:** The 2020 (older) edition used different numbering — audience separation at Section 6.4.1; concussion mortars at Section 6.4.2. Some jurisdictions still reference the prior edition. Confirm the adopted edition with the local AHJ.[^46]
### 4.4 Canada — Fireworks Operator Certificate (FOC)
**Governing legislation:** *Explosives Act*, R.S.C., 1985, c. E-17; *Explosives Regulations, 2013*, SOR/2013-211, Sections 387 and 423.[^47][^48]

**Issuing authority:** Natural Resources Canada, Explosives Regulatory Division (ERD). Canada (outside Québec): 580 Booth Street, Ottawa, ON K1A 0E4; ERDmms@nrcan.gc.ca.[^49]

**FOC classification levels and requirements:**

| FOC Level | Eligibility Requirements |
|---|---|
| Display Assistant | Complete ERD Display Fireworks Course (or certified equivalent); age 18+ |
| Pyrotechnician | Complete ERD Special Effects Pyrotechnics Safety and Legal Awareness Course (or certified equivalent) |
| Senior Pyrotechnician | 1 year as certified Pyrotechnician + work journal + supervisor recommendation letter |
| Special Effects Pyrotechnician | 1 year as Senior Pyrotechnician + work journal + supervisor recommendation letter |
| Special Effects Pyrotechnician – Detonating Cord | Hold Special Effects Pyrotechnician certificate + demonstrated ability with initiation systems |
| Visitor Pyrotechnician | Reside outside Canada; demonstrate equivalent experience for Pyrotechnician-level activities |

[^48][^50][^51][^47]

**Application fee:** CAD $150.00, payable to the Receiver General for Canada. Renewal: ERD sends reminder at least two months before certificate expiry.[^50][^49]

## 5. Provincial and State OHS Code Comparison — Venue Emergency Planning
### 5.1 Alberta OHS Code — Part 7: Emergency Preparedness and Response
Alberta's Occupational Health and Safety Code, Part 7, is the specific provincial regulation governing emergency preparedness at Alberta workplaces, including assembly venue operations. Source: search-ohs-laws.alberta.ca[^52]

| Section | Title | Key Requirements |
|---|---|---|
| **115(1)** | Emergency response plan | Employer must establish an ERP for any emergency requiring rescue or evacuation |
| **115(2)** | Worker involvement | Workers must be involved in establishing the ERP |
| **115(3)** | Currency | ERP must be kept current |
| **116** | Contents of plan | (a) identification of potential emergencies; (b) procedures for identified emergencies; (c) location/operation of emergency equipment and PPE; (d) training requirements; (e) location/use of emergency facilities; (f) fire protection requirements; (g) alarm and emergency communication requirements; (h) first aid services required; (i) procedures for rescue and evacuation; (j) designated rescue and evacuation workers |
| **117(1–3)** | Rescue and evacuation workers | Designate workers for rescue/evacuation; training must include exercises simulating potential emergencies in the ERP |
| **118(1)** | Equipment | Provide appropriate equipment and PPE to designated workers |

**Important:** Alberta OHS Code Part 7 does not specify a minimum threshold (number of occupants or venue type) for mandatory ERP development. The trigger is whether an emergency "requiring rescue or evacuation" may arise — which applies to any public assembly occupancy.[^52]

| Jurisdiction | Governing Regulation | Key ERP Requirements | Assembly-Specific Provisions |
|---|---|---|---|

| **British Columbia** | WorkSafeBC OHS Regulation, Part 4, ss. 4.13–4.14 | Risk assessment required; written rescue/evacuation procedures for identified risks; annual drills mandatory | Written procedures required where persons need physical assistance to be moved (s. 4.13(3)(f)); relevant to large venues |
| **Federal (Canada Labour Code workplaces)** | Canada OHS Regulations SOR/86-304, ss. 17.4–17.10 | Emergency evacuation plan if >50 employees in building; annual drills; 24-hr advance notice to fire department | >50-employee threshold for written plan; worker consultation required |
| **Ontario** | OHSA; Ontario Regulation 851 (Industrial Establishments) | General duty under OHSA; OHS reg 851 applies to industrial, not specifically assembly occupancies | No single assembly-venue OHS regulation; general duty clause applies |

[^52][^53][^54][^55][^56][^57][^58][^59]

**Ontario gap note:** Ontario does not have an assembly-venue-specific OHS regulation comparable to Alberta's OHS Code Part 7. Ontario venues are subject to the general duty clause of the OHSA, the Fire Protection and Prevention Act, and the Ontario Building Code, but not a codified venue-emergency-planning section comparable to Alberta's 10-element requirement under Section 116.
### 5.3 US State Enhanced Requirements
**States with codified mass gathering medical requirements:** New York, Pennsylvania, Maryland, Georgia, Iowa, Oregon, and Washington D.C. have enacted legislation stipulating minimum onsite medical resources at mass gathering events. Nevada does not appear in this list.[^2]

**California:** Cal Fire Code, Chapter 9, Section 901.7 contains the California impairment program requirements, consistent with IFC but with California-specific additions.[^40]

**New York:** FDNY Fire Code Chapter 9, Section FC 901.7, with detailed fire guard provisions specific to standpipe, sprinkler, and fire alarm systems.[^43]

### 6.3 OSHA Proposed Heat Safety Rule — Regulatory Status
**Rule citation:** 29 CFR §1910.148, "Heat Injury and Illness Prevention in Outdoor and Indoor Work Settings."[^69]

**Federal Register:** Notice of Proposed Rulemaking (NPRM) published **August 30, 2024** (89 FR [2024-14824]).[^70][^71]

**Heat trigger thresholds (as proposed):**

| Trigger Level | Heat Index Threshold | WBGT Equivalent |
|---|---|---|
| Initial heat trigger | ≥80°F | NIOSH Recommended Alert Limit (RAL) |
| High heat trigger | ≥90°F | NIOSH Recommended Exposure Limit (REL) |

[^72][^71][^73][^69]

**Regulatory status as of April 2026:** The rule is **currently suspended under a regulatory freeze**. On January 20, 2025, President Trump issued a Presidential Memorandum titled "Regulatory Freeze Pending Review," which paused all pending OSHA rulemaking including this heat standard. The rule was not formally withdrawn but has not been finalized. Public comment closed January 14, 2025; informal public hearings concluded July 2, 2025; post-hearing comment deadline was October 30, 2025. The Trump administration has not withdrawn the rule but is expected to revise or significantly scale it back.[^74][^75][^76][^77][^78][^72]

**Current enforcement basis:** Without a finalized standard, OSHA enforces heat safety through the **General Duty Clause** (Section 5(a)(1) of the OSH Act), which requires employers to provide a workplace free from recognized hazards.[^71][^76]
### 6.4 NOAA/NWS Heat Alert Thresholds
As of March 4, 2025, the NWS renamed "Excessive Heat Warning" to **"Extreme Heat Warning."** Threshold criteria were not changed.[^79]

| Alert Level | General Threshold | Venue Relevance |
|---|---|---|
| Heat Advisory | Heat index ≥100–105°F for ≥2 hours (varies regionally; some areas as low as 95°F) | Trigger for enhanced hydration stations, rest break requirements, medical standby upgrades |
| Extreme Heat Warning (formerly Excessive Heat Warning) | Heat index ≥105°F for >3 hrs per day / ≥115°F for any period (regional variation: some areas 110°F) | Consider event cancellation or postponement; mandatory cooling and medical resource escalation |

[^80][^81][^82][^83][^79]

Regional variation is significant: Florida triggers excessive heat warnings at 110–113°F, while Maine triggers at 105°F. Venue operators should consult their local NWS Weather Forecast Office (WFO) for applicable thresholds.[^83]

## 7. Lightning Detection and Venue Protocols
### 7.1 NWS Lightning Safety Standards for Outdoor Venues
**Primary NWS sources:**
- NWS Lightning Safety Toolkit for Outdoor Venues (PDF): weather.gov/media/safety/lightning/Lightning_Safety_Toolkit_Outdoor_Venues.pdf[^84]
- NWS Lightning Safety and Outdoor Sports Activities: weather.gov/safety/lightning-sports[^85]
- Environment and Climate Change Canada: canada.ca/en/environment-climate-change/services/lightning/safety/large-outdoor-venues.html[^86]

**NWS Three-Radius Monitoring Guidance (from the Toolkit):**

The NWS Toolkit does not formally enumerate a "15/12/8-mile" three-phase system by those exact names. The actual NWS language establishes two operationally distinct resumption thresholds:[^84]

| Scenario | Resumption Criterion |
|---|---|
| Venue consulting a **professional meteorologist with real-time lightning data** | Lightning has moved beyond **8 miles** from venue; storm motion taking lightning away from venue; threat of new lightning within 8 miles has ended |
| Venue **without** professional meteorologist / real-time data | Wait **30 minutes** after last observed thunder or lightning (day) or thunder (night) before resuming |

[^84]

The "30-30 Rule" widely cited in safety guidance means: (1) seek shelter when lightning flash-to-thunder count is ≤30 seconds (lightning within ~6 miles); (2) wait **30 minutes** after the last sound of thunder before returning to outdoor activities.[^87][^88][^89]

NWS separately notes that "a significant lightning threat extends outward from the base of a thunderstorm cloud about 6 to 10 miles," and that organized events should account for time to evacuate all patrons to safe shelter before beginning evacuation procedures.[^85]

Canada's guidance is consistent: "Activities do not resume until at least 30 minutes after the last rumble of thunder."[^86]
### 7.2 NWS StormReady Recognition Program for Venues
**Program type:** Voluntary NWS recognition program for large outdoor venues.[^84][^90]

**Eligible venue types:** Outdoor sporting arenas, concert venues, water parks, raceways, and amusement parks.[^84]

**Requirements for recognition:**

| Category | Requirement |
|---|---|
| **Information Reception** | Install locally-run lightning detection system with display unit on site OR subscribe to a commercial notification system; have NOAA Weather Radio on location with continuous access to NWS warnings |
| **Decision Support** | Written lightning safety plan; instructions for contacting local emergency management and a weather service provider |
| **Public Notification** | Multiple patron notification methods: outdoor warning sirens, PA system, internal TV/radio, text/email alerts, social media, staff announcements |
| **Protection Program** | Means to shelter patrons; written emergency operations safety plan to evacuate; shelter location signage; investigate lightning protection equipment |

[^90][^91][^84]

**Certification process:** (1) Notify local NWS office; (2) Complete application; (3) Site visit by local StormReady Advisory Board; (4) Notification of acceptance; (5) Recognition ceremony; (6) Recertification every **3 years**.[^92]
### 7.3 Commercial Lightning Detection Providers
| Provider | Network | Key Specifications |
|---|---|---|
| **Vaisala / NLDN** | National Lightning Detection Network (NLDN) | 100+ ground-based sensors across continental US; detects cloud-to-ground lightning; 40+ years of continuous operation; used by NASA, US Coast Guard; location accuracy within ~84 meters |
| **Perry Weather** | Uses NLDN data | Real-time alerts in <20 seconds; customizable distance alerts (any radius); on-site weather stations with WBGT monitoring |

### 7.4 NFPA 780 — Lightning Protection Systems for Venue Structures
**Publication:** NFPA 780, *Standard for the Installation of Lightning Protection Systems*, **2023 Edition**.[^93][^94]

**Scope (Section 1.1.1):** Covers traditional lightning protection system installation for "ordinary structures" and "miscellaneous structures and special occupancies" — categories that encompass assembly venue structures (arenas, amphitheaters, grandstands).[^95]

**Key 2023 edition updates:** Added requirements for lightning protection for additions to a protected structure; revised definitions for surge protective device and related equipment.[^93]

**Application:** NFPA 780 provides requirements for air terminals (lightning rods), down conductors, grounding electrodes, bonding, and surge protection devices. It uses the Rolling Sphere Method and Protection Angle Method to determine coverage zones. Integration with NFPA 70 (National Electrical Code) grounding requirements is required.[^96][^97][^95]

**NFPA 780 is not mandatory** unless adopted by the local AHJ; it does not require lightning protection systems to be installed (that decision is risk-based). However, where a system is installed on a structure, NFPA 780 governs the design and installation.[^94][^95]

## 8. NFPA 1026 — Incident Commander Competency Requirements
### 8.1 Publication Details
**NFPA 1026, *Standard for Incident Management Personnel Professional Qualifications*. 2024 Edition.** Published by the National Fire Protection Association.[^98][^99][^100]

NFPA 1026 establishes minimum Job Performance Requirements (JPRs) for personnel performing roles within an all-hazards incident management system.[^99]

**Key 2024 edition additions:**
- New requirement for **continuing education and professional development** for all incident management positions, including Incident Commanders[^101]
- Expanded scope of Incident Commander coverage in Chapter 4[^101]
### 8.2 Chapter 4 — Incident Commander JPRs
| Section | Topic | Key Requirement |
|---|---|---|
| **4.1** | General | IC is the individual responsible for all incident activities, strategies/tactics, and ordering/releasing resources; overall authority and responsibility at the incident site |
| **4.2** | Assume, Transfer, and Terminate Command | IC must be able to assume command of an established incident scene given an initial action plan, transition documents, incident documentation, and available resources |
| **4.2.1(B) Skills** | Requisite skills for command assumption | Analyzing incident conditions and developing plan of action; transmitting accurate size-up report; completing incident worksheets; selecting/setting up command post; delegating responsibilities; coordinating with other disciplines; operating incident communications |
### 8.3 Competencies Required for Venue Incident Commanders
NFPA 1026 Chapter 4 competencies as applicable to assembly occupancy planned events:

- **Incident size-up and command establishment:** Ability to arrive at a planned event or incident and establish, communicate, and transfer command[^98]
- **Action plan development:** Develop and communicate an Incident Action Plan (IAP) based on event conditions[^98]
- **Resource management:** Accurately estimate, order, and release resources; coordinate multi-agency response[^98]
- **Communication operations:** Operate incident communications systems; maintain contact with field personnel and external agencies[^98]
- **ICS integration:** Function within the NIMS Incident Command System framework; interface with Planning, Operations, Logistics, and Finance/Administration sections[^102][^103]
- **Demobilization:** Create and implement an incident demobilization plan[^104]
- **Continuing education:** 2024 edition requires ongoing professional development for certification maintenance[^101]
### 8.4 Relationship to FEMA ICS Training
NFPA 1026 aligns with the NIMS Incident Command System; NFPA 1026-compliant training enhances and extends the foundation established by FEMA ICS courses. The FEMA ICS sequence relevant to venue Incident Commanders:[^102]

| FEMA Course | Scope | Relationship to NFPA 1026 |
|---|---|---|
| IS-100 | ICS fundamentals, terminology | Foundation level; prerequisite knowledge |
| IS-200 | ICS for single resources and initial action incidents | Basic IC functions |
| IS-700 | NIMS overview | Framework alignment |
| IS-300/400 | Intermediate and advanced ICS | Closer to NFPA 1026 IC JPR level |

NFPA 1026 certification (via Pro Board or IFSAC-recognized testing organizations) provides the formal professional qualification layer above FEMA online courses.[^104][^105]
### 8.5 Adoption Status
**Texas:** Texas Commission on Fire Protection (TCFP) certifies Incident Commanders per NFPA 1026 Chapter 4, 2024 Edition, as part of state fire certification requirements.[^103]

**TEEX (Texas A&M Engineering Extension Service):** Offers NFPA 1026-compliant Incident Commander course open to emergency management professionals.[^102]

**Pro Board:** Certifies against NFPA 1026 through member agencies; widely used for portable credentialing.[^104][^105]

**Venue-operator mandates:** No jurisdiction has been identified that **mandates** NFPA 1026 compliance specifically for private venue operators (as opposed to fire service personnel responding to events). Venue operators typically face ICS/NIMS requirements through permit conditions, special event licensing, or contractual obligations with jurisdictional fire authorities, rather than a direct NFPA 1026 mandate.
