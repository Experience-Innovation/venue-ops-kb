# Gender-Inclusive Facility Design Codes for Large Public Assembly Venues
### North American Building Codes, Human Rights Legislation, and Design Standards — April 2026

***

## Executive Summary

The knowledge-base concept *Inclusivity-And-Accessibility-Gender-Inclusive-Facilities* cites only the 2010 ADA Standards for Accessible Design — a document focused entirely on disability access that contains no provisions governing gender-inclusive facilities. This report closes that structural gap (DELTA-007) with authoritative reference on the two primary US model plumbing codes, the Canadian federal and provincial human rights frameworks, US state and municipal legal provisions, design standards for all-gender multi-stall facilities in assembly occupancies, and documented venue implementation examples as of April 2026.

The regulatory landscape is bifurcated and evolving rapidly. In Canada, gender identity is a protected ground at the federal level and in all provinces, creating clear duty-to-accommodate obligations for public venue operators. In the United States, protection depends almost entirely on state and local law; a Trump administration executive order (January 2025) has reversed federal-level protections, while active litigation challenges these actions. Both model plumbing codes — the IPC and UPC — now contain explicit all-gender facility provisions, with the 2021 IPC and 2024 UPC being the key editions.

***

## 1. International Plumbing Code (IPC) — ICC Provisions

The International Plumbing Code, published by the International Code Council (ICC), is adopted in 37 US states and serves approximately 76% of the US population. Canada does not use the IPC directly; the National Building Code of Canada (NBC) is the analogous instrument. IPC all-gender provisions developed incrementally across multiple code cycles.[^1]

### 1.1 Historical Development

**2000 IPC (baseline):** Required a separate compartment with walls, partitions, and a door for each water closet in public restrooms, establishing the privacy foundation on which later all-gender provisions build.[^2]

**2006 IPC:** Extended privacy requirements to urinals, requiring a separate area with partitions and defining partition dimensions.[^2]

**2012 IPC:** Prohibited locking multi-occupant restroom egress doors from inside to deter inappropriate activity.[^2]

**2018 IPC:**
- **§403.1.2:** Single-user toilet and bathing rooms count toward total required fixture quantities; must be identified as available to all persons regardless of sex. This was the first provision explicitly enabling all-gender single-occupancy facilities to contribute to code-required fixture counts.[^3][^2]
- **§403.2 (new exception):** Separate facilities not required where rooms have water closets for both sexes with privacy in accordance with §405.3.4, and urinals are in stalls or a separate area.[^3]

**2021 IPC (major revision):**
- **§403.1.1 Exception 2:** Multiple-user all-gender facilities are now explicitly addressed. Where a facility is designed for all genders, the minimum fixture count is calculated at **100% of the total occupant load** — not the default 50/50 male/female split. This is the critical assembly-occupancy provision.[^4][^2]
- **§403.2 Exception 5:** Single-user toilet rooms need not be designated by sex.[^5]
- **§403.2 Exception 6:** Introduces the explicit concept of multiple-user toilet facilities designed to serve both sexes; any urinals must be in privacy stalls.[^5]
- **§403.1.2 (revised):** Strengthened language requiring single-user facilities to be identified for use by all persons, regardless of sex.[^2]
- Privacy note: Beyond urinal stall requirements, the 2021 IPC does not mandate full-height partitions for water closets in all-gender multi-user facilities beyond what is already required for same-sex facilities — this differs materially from the UPC.[^4]

**2024 IPC:**
- **§403.4 (IBC §2902.4):** Signage for toilet facilities must now indicate whether the facility is designated for males, females, or **all persons regardless of sex**, bringing signage requirements into alignment with the 2021 fixture changes.[^6][^7]
- **Lockable egress door exception:** Multiple-user toilet facilities may have the egress door lockable from the inside if three criteria are met, addressing a security concern raised for all-gender facilities.[^7]
- All accessible fixtures in all-gender facilities must comply with **ICC A117.1** (Accessible and Usable Buildings and Facilities).[^4]

### 1.2 Assembly Occupancy — Fixture Count Application

For a large assembly venue under the IPC, the fixture count calculation for an all-gender multi-user facility proceeds as follows:

For an occupant load of 2,000 in an assembly occupancy:
- **Separate-sex approach (default §403.1.1):** Divide load in half (1,000 male / 1,000 female), then apply Table 403.1 ratios for each sex separately.
- **All-gender approach (Exception 2):** Apply Table 403.1 ratios at 100% of occupant load (2,000), using whichever ratio (male or female) applies to each fixture type. Where male and female ratios differ (e.g., female water closet ratios are typically higher), the all-gender approach produces a larger total fixture count to ensure adequacy for any demographic mix.[^4][^2]

The intent is that all-gender facilities must not result in a reduced total fixture count compared to the sum of separate-sex facilities they replace.[^4]

## 2. Uniform Plumbing Code (UPC) — IAPMO Provisions

The Uniform Plumbing Code, published by the International Association of Plumbing and Mechanical Officials (IAPMO), is adopted in approximately 12–13 states, concentrated in the western United States (California, Washington, Oregon, Hawaii, Alaska, Idaho, Montana, South Dakota, New Mexico, Minnesota, Missouri, Iowa) and in certain local jurisdictions in other states.[^8][^9]

### 2.1 Pre-2024 UPC

Prior to the 2024 edition, the UPC contained no explicit all-gender multi-user facility provisions. IAPMO's 1999 Toilet Room Design Manual for All-Gender Facilities provided guidance but was not code text. The 2021 UPC lacked the provisions later introduced in 2024.[^10][^11]

### 2.2 2024 UPC (Published April 2023)

The 2024 UPC introduced the first explicit all-gender facility provisions in the UPC code body:[^12][^13]

**§422.1.1 (Fixture Calculations):** Added language: *"For toilet facilities designed for use by all genders, the minimum number of fixtures shall be the aggregate calculated at 50 percent female and 50 percent male in accordance with Table 422.1."* Where all-gender fixtures are provided in addition to separate facilities, those fixtures are included in the total fixture count for the occupancy.[^14]

**§422.2 (Separate Facilities):** Exception (4) revised to allow omission of separate sex facilities where water closets are in privacy compartments and urinals are in privacy compartments or a separate private area.[^14]

**§422.6 (Water Closet Compartment):** For water closets in all-gender toilet facilities, partitions must comply with **Type A Security Requirements of IAPMO/ANSI/CAN Z124.10**. Type A is a higher standard than Type B (which applies to gendered facilities). This is a key differentiator from the IPC.[^15]

**§422.7 (Urinal Partitions):** Urinals in all-gender toilet rooms must be in privacy compartments complying with Type A security requirements of Z124.10, or located in a separate private area visually separated from the remainder of the room.[^15]

**IAPMO/ANSI/CAN Z124.10-2022** (Water Closets and Urinal Partitions): Published as both an American National Standard and a Canadian National Standard, this standard covers materials, construction, performance testing, and markings for partition systems. Type A is the most secure classification. The Standards Council of Canada formally recognized Z124.10 as a National Standard of Canada.[^16][^17]

### 2.3 IAPMO Manual 1 (2023 Revision)

The *Toilet Room Design Manual: All Gender, Health, Safety, Privacy and Security* (IAPMO Manual 1, 2023 edition) provides design and approval guidance for all-gender toilet facilities not specifically covered in other parts of plumbing or building codes. It addresses assisted-use and all-gender design in a single reference document and is available from ASSE International.[^18][^10]

## 3. IPC vs. UPC All-Gender Provisions — Comparison Matrix

| Dimension | IPC (2021/2024) | UPC (2024) |
|---|---|---|
| **Publisher** | ICC (International Code Council) | IAPMO |
| **Geographic reach** | 37 US states; ~76% US population[^1] | ~12–13 states; western US concentration[^9] |
| **First all-gender multi-user provisions** | 2021 edition (§403.1.1 Ex. 2; §403.2 Ex. 6)[^2] | 2024 edition (§422.1.1; §422.2 Ex. 4)[^14][^12] |
| **Fixture count method — all-gender multi-user** | 100% of total occupant load (§403.1.1 Ex. 2)[^4] | Aggregate of 50% female + 50% male (§422.1.1)[^14] |
| **Net fixture count vs. separate-sex** | Must equal or exceed equivalent separate-sex total[^4] | Must equal aggregate of equivalent separate-sex total[^14] |
| **Privacy partition standard — WC** | ICC A117.1 for accessible; §405.3.4 for general privacy (no Z124.10 reference)[^11][^4] | IAPMO/ANSI/CAN Z124.10 **Type A** security requirements (§422.6)[^15] |
| **Urinal privacy requirement** | Must be in stall per §405.3.5 (urinal stall dimensions defined)[^2] | Privacy compartment (Type A per Z124.10) or separate private area (§422.7)[^15] |
| **Single-user all-gender labeling** | Required — "available for use by all persons regardless of sex" (§403.1.2)[^2] | Permitted; not expressly required in same terms |
| **Signage requirement** | §403.4 (2024): Must state male, female, or all persons regardless of sex[^7] | Not explicitly addressed in same section |
| **Egress door locking** | 2024: Permitted from inside if 3 criteria met[^7] | Not addressed separately |
| **Privacy partition standard** | Not cross-referenced to Z124.10 (IPC limitation noted by IAPMO)[^11] | Z124.10 Type A — more specific and stringent[^15] |
| **Assembly occupancy treatment** | Same §403.1.1 Exception 2 applies; 100% load calculation[^2] | Same §422.1.1 aggregate approach applies |
| **Code design manual** | ICC Building Safety Journal commentary[^2] | IAPMO Manual 1 (2023)[^10] |

**Key practical difference:** The UPC, through its mandatory reference to IAPMO/ANSI/CAN Z124.10 Type A for all-gender water closet and urinal compartments, imposes a more prescriptive and standardized partition specification than the IPC, which relies on general privacy language and ICC A117.1 for accessibility. Designers working in UPC jurisdictions (especially California) have clearer material and performance requirements for all-gender stall enclosures.

## 4. National Building Code of Canada (NBC) — All-Gender Provisions

Canada does not adopt the IPC or UPC. The National Building Code of Canada (NBC 2020, with provincial editions) governs building design.[^19]

**NBC §3.7.2.2:** Requires water closets for each sex, calculated assuming occupant load equally divided between males and females unless actual proportion can be predicted. The wording historically could be misread as requiring gender-segregated washrooms.[^19]

**Proposed Change 1750 to NBC 2020:** This proposed amendment to the explanatory note for §3.7.2.2 clarifies definitively that the NBC does *not* require gender-segregation of washrooms and is entirely compatible with gender-neutral facilities. The amendment states that the NBC requires only a minimum number of water closets, not that washrooms be separated by sex. Full-height partitions for privacy of washroom users, and features such as enclosed stalls, are consistent with NBC compliance.[^19]

**NBC-2019 Alberta Edition (NBC(AE)):** Where a washroom facility is gender-inclusive, water closets must be in "enclosing rooms per §3.7.2.2.(17)" — each WC must have a full-height enclosure. This is the Edmonton building authority's guidance and represents the most specific Canadian provincial-code guidance on all-gender multi-stall design found in this research.[^20]

**Canada Occupational Health and Safety Regulations (COHSR) — Amendment effective December 1, 2024:** Federally regulated workplaces may now count all-gender toilet rooms toward the total number of toilets required. An all-gender toilet room containing a urinal does not qualify (urinals count only in male facilities). This removes the prior disincentive for federally regulated employers to provide all-gender facilities.[^21][^22]

## 5. Canadian Human Rights Framework

### 5.1 Federal: Canadian Human Rights Act (CHRA) — Bill C-16

Bill C-16, *An Act to amend the Canadian Human Rights Act and the Criminal Code*, received Royal Assent and came into force on **June 19, 2017**, following a Senate vote of 67–11. The amendment added **"gender identity or expression"** as a prohibited ground of discrimination under the CHRA.[^23][^24][^25]

**Scope of the CHRA:** The Act applies to the federal government itself, First Nations governments, and federally regulated employers (banks, telecommunications companies, airlines, railways, interprovincial transportation, broadcasting). Public services, most industries, and most public-facing venues are governed by **provincial** human rights legislation, not the CHRA.[^26]

**Practical effect for venues:** A federally regulated entity operating a venue (e.g., a federal Crown corporation running a conference centre) must not discriminate on the basis of gender identity or expression, including in washroom/facility access. However, most arenas, convention centres, and stadiums operated by municipalities or private corporations fall under provincial jurisdiction.

**COHSR (2024 amendment):** As noted, this federal regulation now explicitly enables all-gender toilet rooms in federally regulated workplaces to count toward required totals, eliminating the bureaucratic disincentive.[^21]

### 5.2 Ontario: Ontario Human Rights Code (OHRC Policy)

**Protected grounds:** Gender identity has been protected under Ontario's Human Rights Code (the "Code") through the ground of "sex" since at least 2000; the OHRC issued its *Policy on discrimination and harassment because of gender identity* in 2000, updated in 2014.[^27]

**Washrooms — §13.4 of OHRC Policy:**
The OHRC policy is explicit and directly applicable to public venue operators:

- *"Access to washrooms is a basic physical need at the core of human dignity for everyone."*[^27]
- Trans people have the **right to access washrooms based on their lived gender identity**, not birth-assigned sex or anatomy.
- A trans person *does not need to ask* to use the washroom — access is a right, not a privilege subject to organizational approval.
- Organizations **cannot require trans persons to use separate washrooms** because other patrons express discomfort or transphobic attitudes.[^27]
- Employers and service providers have a **duty to accommodate** trans persons' washroom needs **to the point of undue hardship**.[^27]

**Accommodation and inclusive design (§13.4.2):**
The OHRC policy identifies proactive design measures — before individual accommodation requests arise — as best practice:
- All-gender single-user or multi-stall washrooms that anyone can use
- Privacy enhancements in traditional gendered washrooms (individual shower and changing stalls with curtains or doors)
- Universal washrooms for persons with disabilities and others requiring privacy[^27]

Ontario Building Code (effective January 1, 2015): Requires at least one universal washroom in all new buildings or major renovations; permits single-user gender-neutral washrooms in lieu of separate male and female washrooms.[^27]

**Key tribunal decisions:**
- *Vanderputten v. Seydaco Packaging Corp.*, 2012 HRTO 1977: Employer prohibited trans woman from using women's change room and subjected her to harassment in men's change room — held to be discrimination on the basis of sex.[^28]
- *XY v. Ontario (Government and Consumer Services)*: HRTO held that requiring surgical alteration as a condition for document gender change perpetuated discriminatory stereotypes.[^27]

### 5.3 British Columbia: BC Human Rights Code

**Protected grounds:** British Columbia's Human Rights Code was amended by Bill 27, effective **July 28, 2016**, to add **"gender identity or expression"** as an explicit prohibited ground. Prior to this, the BC Human Rights Tribunal accepted trans discrimination claims under "sex" and "sexual orientation".[^29][^30]

**Scope:** Applies to tenancy, services customarily available to the public, and employment.[^29]

**Key tribunal precedent:**
- *Sheridan v. Sanctuary Investments Ltd. (No. 3)*, 1999 CanLII 35172 (BC HRT): A trans woman patron was denied access to the women's washroom at a nightclub; bouncer and manager enforced anatomy-based washroom policy. Tribunal held this was discriminatory and the venue had a **duty to accommodate**. Tribunal stated: *"transsexuals in transition who are living as members of the desired sex should be considered to be members of that sex for the purposes of human rights legislation."*[^28][^27]
- *Ferris v. O.T.E.U., Local 15* (1999): Workplace washroom denial caused emotional harm; trans people *"particularly vulnerable to discrimination"*[^28][^27]

BC Human Rights Commissioner provides guidance consistent with OHRC that trans individuals should be permitted to use facilities consistent with their lived gender identity without producing medical or legal documentation.[^31]

### 5.4 Alberta: Alberta Human Rights Act (AHRA)

**Protected grounds:** The AHRA explicitly lists **"gender identity"** and **"gender expression"** as protected grounds (in addition to "gender") — amendments codified as RSA 2000 c.A-25.5, with additions from 2015 c.18 s.3 and 2017 c.17 s.2. The Alberta Human Rights Commission confirms that employers, landlords, and service providers cannot discriminate based on gender, gender identity, or gender expression.[^32][^33]

**Current status (April 2026):** The Alberta Human Rights Act's protections for gender identity and expression in public services, employment, and tenancy remain **in force**. The Alberta government's controversial restrictive legislation (Bills 26, 27, and the Fairness and Safety in Sport Act) targets trans youth healthcare, school name/pronoun protocols, and sports participation — not adult public venue washroom access.[^34][^35][^36]

Bill 9 (*Protecting Alberta's Children Statutes Amendment Act*, introduced November 2025) invokes the notwithstanding clause to shield those three youth-focused laws from Charter challenge. This does not alter the AHRA's protections for gender identity and expression in adult public service contexts.[^35][^36]

**Practical implication for Alberta venue operators:** Service providers operating arenas, convention centres, and similar public venues remain subject to AHRA obligations. Denying a trans patron washroom access consistent with their lived gender identity could constitute discrimination under §4 of the AHRA (denial of goods, services, or accommodation customarily available to the public).[^32]

### 5.5 Canadian Human Rights Framework — Summary Table

| Jurisdiction | Instrument | Gender Identity Protected Since | Scope for Venue Operators | Washroom Access Guidance |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Federal (CHRA)** | Canadian Human Rights Act (Bill C-16) | June 19, 2017[^23][^24] | Federally regulated entities; narrow scope[^26] | Implicit in non-discrimination on basis of gender identity/expression |
| **Federal (workplace)** | Canada OHS Regulations | Dec 1, 2024 (all-gender toilet counting)[^21] | Federally regulated workplaces | All-gender toilets count toward required total |
| **Ontario** | Ontario Human Rights Code | Interpreted under "sex" since ~2000; explicit OHRC policy 2000/2014[^27] | All public services, facilities, tenancy | Right to access based on lived gender identity; duty to accommodate to undue hardship[^27] |

| **Alberta** | Alberta Human Rights Act | 2015 (gender expression); 2017 (gender identity reinforced)[^32] | All services, facilities, tenancy[^33] | Anti-discrimination obligations apply; no direct washroom design mandate |

## 6. US State and Municipal Provisions

### 6.1 State-Level Landscape (April 2026)

The US state landscape is sharply bifurcated. As of April 2026:[^37][^38]

**States with explicit gender identity non-discrimination protections in public accommodations (no state ban on trans bathroom use):**
California, Colorado, Connecticut, Delaware, Hawaii, Illinois, Iowa, Maine, Maryland, Massachusetts, Minnesota, Nevada, New Jersey, New Mexico, Oregon, Rhode Island, Vermont, Washington, and Washington D.C. These states have human rights laws that prohibit discrimination based on gender identity in places of public accommodation, including venues with public restrooms.[^39]

**States with bathroom restriction legislation (as of April 2026):**
- 29 states have enacted some form of restriction on trans bathroom access in K-12 schools and/or government buildings[^38][^37]
- 12 states enacted new bathroom restriction laws in 2025: Arkansas, Georgia, Idaho, Mississippi, Montana, North Dakota, South Carolina, South Dakota, Texas, Utah, West Virginia, and Wyoming[^37]
- 4 states have criminalized trans bathroom use in certain circumstances[^38]
- **Idaho (April 2, 2026):** Signed HB 752, the strictest trans bathroom ban in the nation — criminalizes trans bathroom use in any bathroom that does not align with biological sex, including in **private businesses**, not just government buildings. First offense is a misdemeanor; repeat offense is a felony[^40][^41]

**Active federal legal challenges:**
- Trump Executive Order 14168 (January 20, 2025): Directs federal agencies to define sex as a binary biological classification and designate "intimate spaces" by biological sex rather than gender identity[^42][^43]
- GSA formally rescinded the 2016 bulletin permitting trans federal employees to use preferred bathrooms (May 2025)[^42]
- ACLU and Democracy Forward filed class action on behalf of trans federal workers (May 2025), citing *Bostock v. Clayton County* (2020 SCOTUS) and Title VII[^44]
- Multiple ACLU cases challenging state bathroom bans are ongoing; as of April 2026, courts have issued injunctions in some states[^45]
- **Important:** Trump's EO and state bathroom bans primarily target government buildings and K-12 schools. Their application to **private assembly venues** is generally limited, except in Idaho (private businesses included) and Florida/Kansas/Montana/Wyoming/Ohio (some private settings covered)[^38]

### 6.2 Key Municipal Ordinances

| Municipality | Ordinance | Key Requirements |
|---|---|---|
| **Washington, D.C.** | Human Rights Act of 1977, implemented 2006[^46] | First major municipal rule; right to use bathroom consistent with gender identity; single-occupancy restrooms must use gender-neutral signage |
| **San Francisco** | SF Police Code §3305.3, Ord. 53-16, effective May 2016[^47][^48] | All single-user restrooms in any business open to public must be gender-neutral; multiple-user optional; 90-day compliance for existing businesses |
| **Seattle** | Title 14 amendment, August 2015[^46] | Right to use gender-specific bathrooms consistent with gender identity in single and multi-stall; single-occupancy restrooms in city buildings/public accommodations must be gender-neutral |
| **Philadelphia** | Code Ch. 9-600 §9-636, October 2015[^46] | All single-occupancy public business bathrooms must use gender-neutral signs; existing art exemption with clear signage |
| **New York City** | Plumbing and Building Code amendment, June 2016[^46][^49] | All single-occupancy bathrooms must be gender-neutral; since 2002, illegal to discriminate on basis of gender identity in bathrooms |
| **Denver** | Building/Fire Code update, effective May 2018[^50] | All single-stall restrooms must be labeled gender-neutral; did not impact multi-stall at that time |
| **West Hollywood** | Municipal ordinance[^51] | Requires all businesses to relabel restrooms gender-neutral |
| **Chicago** | Ordinance[^46] | Single-occupancy restrooms in city-owned buildings or public accommodations must be gender-neutral |

**Interaction with building codes:** Several municipalities (notably New York City) had to amend their plumbing codes simultaneously with human rights ordinances, because the prior plumbing code required separate-sex designation for multi-occupancy facilities. This illustrates the need for coordinated building code and human rights law review when implementing all-gender facilities.[^46]

## 7. Design Standards for All-Gender Multi-Stall Facilities in Assembly Occupancies

### 7.1 Privacy Compartments — Partition Specifications

The core design distinction between conventional and all-gender multi-stall facilities is the treatment of stall enclosures.

**Standard (gendered) restroom stalls:**
- Partitions typically extend from approximately 12–14 inches above finished floor (AFF) to 58–70 inches AFF
- Floor gaps of 12–14 inches (305–356 mm) are historical norms[^52]
- Partition tops typically at 70 inches (1,778 mm) AFF minimum[^52]

**All-gender facility stall specifications:**

*Full-height (floor-to-ceiling) enclosures — preferred standard:*
- Floor gap: Practical minimum approximately **1 inch (25 mm)**; uneven floors and drains set actual lower limit[^52]
- Note: ADA/accessible stalls without dimensions ≥66"×66" require minimum **9-inch (229 mm)** floor gap; if stall is ≥66"×66", gap can be as small as 1 inch[^52]
- Ceiling gap: As small as 1 inch (25 mm) feasible; level ceiling and installation clearances are limiting factors[^52]
- **IAPMO/ANSI/CAN Z124.10 Type A:** Required under 2024 UPC for all-gender water closet and urinal compartments (§422.6, §422.7); provides materials, construction, and performance specifications for the most secure partition classification[^15]
- University of Washington design guidelines: Full height (floor to ceiling) hard walls or restroom partitions to ensure visual AND acoustic privacy; "zero sightline" construction using manufacturers such as Ironwood MFG[^53]
- NBC-2019 Alberta Edition: Where gender-inclusive, WCs must be in enclosing rooms per §3.7.2.2.(17)[^20]

*California amendments to 2024 UPC:*
The California Building Standards Commission (BSC) has amended UPC §422.6 to allow "privacy compartments" (not necessarily full-height/Type A) where permitted by the enforcing agency, and to require each compartment door to have an inside lock distinguishable as locked from outside. This creates a California-specific variation.[^15]

**Stall depth considerations:** Panels for stalls deeper than 60 inches (1,524 mm) typically require costly splices or additional pilasters, because panel materials rarely come in sheets wider than 60 inches.[^52]

**Drainage:** Full-height partition systems with minimal floor gaps may require one drain per stall.[^52]

### 7.2 Urinal Privacy Requirements

All applicable codes (IPC §405.3.5, UPC §422.7) require urinals in all-gender facilities to be in privacy compartments or in a separate private area visually separated from the rest of the facility. Under the UPC, urinal compartment partitions must comply with Z124.10 Type A security requirements in all-gender settings.[^54][^14][^2][^15]

University of Washington guidelines specify urinals in all-gender settings must be enclosed within a stall partition to accommodate a variety of users.[^53]

### 7.3 Layout and Circulation

Best-practice design guidance for assembly-scale all-gender multi-stall facilities:[^55][^56]

- **Looping circulation:** At least two points of ingress and egress; avoid dead ends
- **Doorless or grill-entry approach:** Eliminating entry doors reduces queuing barriers; open transoms or screen walls maintain privacy from corridor[^57]
- **Three-zone model (Canadian Architect, 2022):** Private stalls → communal handwashing/grooming stations → lounge/circulation zone[^55]
- **Shared open sink areas:** Handwashing in a shared common area is standard in all-gender multi-stall design; the privacy emphasis is on the toilet compartment, not the sink
- **Occupancy indicators:** Visible occupied/vacant signals on each stall door; door closers hold vacant stalls open at approximately 10 degrees[^56]
- **Visual privacy from exterior:** Eliminate sightlines from corridor into toilet compartment area; angled entries or sight screens[^56]
- **Acoustic privacy:** Higher STC-rated partitions and ceiling assemblies where full-height walls not used[^56]
- **Lighting:** Bright, open common areas in gender-inclusive washrooms improve perception of safety[^58]

### 7.4 Fixture Count Examples — Assembly Occupancy

For a 5,000-person assembly occupancy applying IPC 2021 Table 403.1:

*Separate-sex approach (default):*
- Male (2,500 persons): apply male ratios → X water closets + Y urinals + Z lavatories
- Female (2,500 persons): apply female ratios (typically more water closets) → A water closets + B lavatories
- Total: sum of male and female fixtures

*All-gender approach (IPC §403.1.1 Ex. 2):*
- Apply Table 403.1 at 100% of occupant load (5,000), using the higher of male/female ratio for each fixture type
- Result: Equal or greater total fixtures, no reduction permitted[^4]

For a UPC jurisdiction, §422.1.1 produces the same numerical result via the aggregate method (50% female + 50% male = 100%), but applies Z124.10 Type A to all stall enclosures.[^14]

**IBC 2021 §1109.2.1 note:** In assembly and mercantile occupancies, an accessible family or assisted-use toilet room is required where six or more male and female water closets are required in aggregate. This requirement coexists with all-gender multi-stall provisions and should be included in facility planning.[^59]

### 7.5 Signage Design Approaches

The shift in signage philosophy is from identity-based to function-based communication:

**Traditional approach:** Gender-based stick figures (human silhouette; human-with-dress silhouette) signaling *who* can use the room.

**All-gender / function-based approach:** Pictograms depicting *what is in the room* (toilet symbol, sink symbol, urinal symbol) without reference to gender identity. Removes the initial barrier of hostility — any restroom with a toilet is the "right" restroom.[^60]

**Code-required signage (IPC 2024 §403.4; IBC §2902.4):** Signage must indicate whether the facility is for males, females, or all persons regardless of sex. "All persons regardless of sex" aligns with the function-based approach.[^7]

**Recommended signage elements for assembly venues:**
- Remove gendered stick figure pictograms from all-gender facility signage
- Use toilet, sink, and (where applicable) urinal icons to indicate fixture content
- Include "All Gender / Tous les genres" text (bilingual for Canadian venues) or equivalent
- Maintain high contrast, tactile (raised) text and Braille per accessibility code
- IPC 2024 and San Francisco ordinance both require signage to comply with ADA (US) or Title 24 / accessibility code standards[^47][^7]
- Direction signs at main building entrance indicate locations of all-gender washrooms (per Boulder, CO requirements)[^61]

Queen's University's 2023 conversion of approximately 250 single-user washrooms used "symbol-based signage that focuses on the usage of the space rather than the identity of the user — the universal standard for inclusive design".[^62]

The Canadian Museum for Human Rights (Winnipeg, 2022) replaced male/female icons with signage indicating the *amenities available* in each space, allowing patrons to choose based on their needs rather than gender compliance.[^63]

## 8. Venue Implementation Case Studies

### 8.1 Scotia Place, Calgary, Alberta — New Construction (Opens Mid-2027)

Scotia Place is the most detailed and directly relevant Canadian example:[^64][^65][^66][^67]

- **Scale:** 18,400-seat multi-purpose arena; home of the Calgary Flames (NHL)
- **Washroom concept:** Three washroom types: men's, women's, and **"flex washrooms"** — 13 flex washroom pods with 85 stalls total
- **Flex design:** 20% of total washrooms can be reconfigured by event type. For NHL games (≈70% male audience), flex washrooms designated for men; for concerts with predominantly female audiences, flex washrooms converted primarily to women's stalls
- **Inclusivity:** 100% accessible washrooms; gender-neutral and flex washrooms; doorless entrances where possible; accessible and limited-mobility stalls; universal washrooms with adult change tables[^64]
- **Capacity vs. code:** 20% more bathrooms than Rogers Place (Edmonton); **60% more than building code requires** for an arena of its size[^67]
- **Approach:** The flex concept is operationally gender-neutral — the same physical space serves any gender demographic based on event demand, with stall configurations adjusted (all stalls vs. stalls + urinals) as needed[^65][^66][^68]
- Began construction mid-2024; opens midway through 2027[^65]

### 8.2 Lansdowne 2.0 Event Centre, Ottawa, Ontario — New Construction (Phase 1 est. July 2028)

- **Scale:** 6,600-seat event centre (hockey); 7,000 for concerts; part of $419M redevelopment[^69][^70]
- **Washroom provisions:** Two (2) multi-user All Access (gender neutral) washrooms in Event Centre and North Side Stands; multiple Accessible Family washrooms; multiple Universal Washrooms with height-adjustable adult change tables[^69]
- **Design priority:** Gender-inclusive facilities listed as a core design priority alongside full accessibility[^71][^72]
- **Women's sports accommodation:** Purpose-built athlete facilities for Ottawa Charge (PWHL) with customized dressing rooms and training spaces[^72][^71]
- **Standards:** Designed to meet/exceed AODA standards and Ontario Building Code[^73]
- Phase 1 construction commenced November/December 2025[^70][^74]

### 8.3 Canadian Museum for Human Rights, Winnipeg — Retrofit (2022)

- **Scope:** Converted all single-occupancy and shared multi-stall washrooms to gender-inclusive spaces[^63]
- **Signage:** Removed male/female icons; replaced with function-based signage showing amenities available in each space[^63]
- **Approach:** Converted *all* washrooms rather than a select few — specifically to avoid "othering" trans patrons by directing them to a different floor or separate facility[^63]
- **Features:** Accessible toilets with built-in lift and transfer system for adults; adult-size and baby change tables; free menstrual products in all washrooms[^63]
- **Cost note:** Primarily a signage and policy change for existing single-user facilities; multi-stall conversions required partition modifications

### 8.4 UEFA Euro 2024, Germany — Stadium Network (Summer 2024)

- **Scale:** All 10 host stadiums across Germany[^75][^76]
- **First:** First major international football tournament to offer gender-neutral toilets at all venues — described as part of UEFA's ESG strategy[^77][^75]
- **Features:** Gender-neutral toilet facilities inside stadiums; gender-neutral body search lanes at all stadium entrances; security conducted by mixed male/female staff[^76][^75]
- **Design context:** Retrofit of existing German stadium facilities; temporary signage changes combined with designated gender-neutral zones[^76]

### 8.5 Colorado Convention Center, Denver — Convention Centre (Ongoing)

- Has mapped and designated all-gender restroom locations, including in the upper E-lobby accessible via concourse[^78]
- Consistent with Denver's 2018 building code ordinance requiring gender-neutral signage on single-stall restrooms[^50]

### 8.6 Evergreen Brick Works, Toronto — Event Venue (Retrofit)

- All-gender washrooms with individual fully-enclosed stalls; shared handwashing common area[^79]
- Visible occupied/vacant indicators on each stall door — eliminates queuing and door-knocking[^79]
- No queue observations reported; attributed to individual-stall access model[^79]

### 8.7 Taylor Swift Eras Tour — Temporary Conversions (2023–2024)

- Multiple stadiums temporarily converted existing men's toilet areas to gender-neutral facilities for Eras Tour events[^80]
- Operational solution: Signage change + stall conversion (removing urinals or covering with temporary partitions) for specific events
- Demonstrates the operationally flexible model that Scotia Place has now embedded as a permanent design feature

### 8.8 WSL Stadium Design Framework (England, 2025)

England's Women's Super League published the first comprehensive stadium design framework for female players and fans (2025):[^81]
- Recommends shifting restroom provision from standard 80/20 male-female ratio to **45/45/10** (with 10% designated gender-neutral)[^81]
- Eliminates urinals in player changing areas in favour of private stalls[^81]
- Adds breastfeeding facilities, menstrual supplies in changing areas[^81]
- Applicable to both new construction and retrofit contexts

## 9. Retrofit Approaches vs. New Construction

| Dimension | New Construction | Retrofit |
|---|---|---|
| **Partition standard** | Full-height enclosures designed from the ground up; Z124.10 Type A compliance straightforward | Raising existing partition heights; structural/drainage challenges; potential floor levelling required[^52] |
| **Drainage** | Drain-per-stall designed in from the start | Retrofitting drains under raised/lowered partitions may be costly[^52] |
| **Fixture count compliance** | IPC/UPC all-gender provisions fully applicable at design stage | Existing buildings may use IPC §403.1.2 or NBC §3.7.2.2 to allow combination of single-user and multi-stall; "grandfathering" provisions may apply |
| **Signage** | Custom signage specified and integrated | Primary retrofit mechanism for single-user washrooms (lowest cost); Queen's University, CMHR examples[^63][^62] |
| **Cost** | Marginal incremental cost when designed from outset | Partition replacement for full-height enclosures; plumbing for additional drains; possible structural work |
| **Operational flexibility** | Flex washroom model (Scotia Place) enables demand-responsive gender allocation without physical rebuild[^65] | Temporary signage changes (Eras Tour model) lowest-cost conversion for events |
| **Human rights obligation** | Code compliance + human rights code alignment from opening | Duty to accommodate may require upgrades to existing facilities even short of full code compliance[^27] |

## 10. Active Legal Challenges and Jurisdictional Risks (April 2026)

### United States
- **Trump EO 14168 (Jan 20, 2025):** Directs binary sex definition in all federal agency activities; requires sex-based designation of "intimate spaces" in federal facilities and federally funded entities. Multiple ACLU lawsuits challenging this order remain pending. Bostock v. Clayton County (2020 SCOTUS) — holding that Title VII prohibits sex discrimination against trans workers — is a primary legal counterargument.[^43][^44][^45]
- **Title IX investigations:** US Dept. of Education OCR launched directed investigation into Denver Public Schools (Jan 2025) for converting a girls' bathroom to all-gender without maintaining comparable girls' facilities, potentially violating Title IX. This case — while school-based — establishes OCR's position that all-gender conversions must maintain comparable sex-specific options.[^82][^83][^84]
- **Idaho HB 752 (signed April 1, 2026):** Criminalizes trans bathroom use in public and private facilities — the strictest such law in the nation, with felony penalties for repeat violations. Legal challenges expected.[^41][^40]
- **State bathroom bans in 25 states** covering trans youth in schools/government buildings; 4 states with criminal penalties. Venue operators in these states must monitor ongoing litigation and avoid inadvertent compliance conflicts.[^37][^38]

### Canada
- **Alberta notwithstanding clause (Bill 9, November 2025):** Shields three anti-trans youth laws from Charter challenge. Does not affect AHRA protections for adult public services. Legal challenges to the underlying healthcare and education laws continue.[^36][^35]
- **Canadian charter protections:** Trans inclusion in gendered spaces is "not a federal imposition — it is the product of long-standing provincial law". Canadian courts have upheld careful balancing of equality rights; blanket exclusion of trans people from gendered spaces is not supported by Canadian jurisprudence.[^31]

## 11. Design Standards Checklist — All-Gender Multi-Stall Facilities in Assembly Occupancies

The following checklist synthesises the building code, human rights, and design guidance applicable to large public assembly venues (arenas, convention centres, stadiums, PACs) as of April 2026.

### Fixture Count & Code Compliance
- [ ] Determine applicable plumbing code (IPC or UPC jurisdiction; or NBC province)
- [ ] IPC jurisdictions: Calculate fixture count for all-gender facility at **100% of occupant load** (§403.1.1 Ex. 2, 2021 IPC)[^4]
- [ ] UPC jurisdictions: Calculate fixture count as aggregate of 50% male + 50% female (§422.1.1, 2024 UPC)[^14]
- [ ] NBC (Canada) jurisdictions: Consult Proposed Change 1750 interpretation; apply NBC §3.7.2.2 with all-gender enclosing rooms per provincial guidance[^20][^19]
- [ ] Confirm total fixture count equals or exceeds equivalent separate-sex calculation
- [ ] Verify IBC 2021 §1109.2.1 requirement for accessible family/assisted-use toilet room where ≥6 WCs aggregate[^59]

### Stall Enclosures & Privacy
- [ ] UPC jurisdictions: Water closet compartments must comply with **IAPMO/ANSI/CAN Z124.10 Type A** security requirements (§422.6)[^15]
- [ ] IPC jurisdictions: Comply with §405.3.4 privacy requirements; consider Z124.10 Type A as best practice even if not strictly required
- [ ] NBC (Canada, Alberta): WCs in enclosing rooms with full-height door lockable from inside (§3.7.2.2(17))[^20]
- [ ] Floor gap: target ≤1 inch (25 mm) for non-accessible stalls; ADA/CSA accessible stalls ≥9 inches unless ≥66"×66" footprint[^52]
- [ ] Ceiling gap: target ≤1 inch (25 mm); verify ceiling level and installation clearances[^52]
- [ ] Door hardware: Inside lock distinguishable as locked from exterior; occupancy indicator (vacant/occupied)[^56][^15]
- [ ] Drainage: One floor drain per stall where floor-level gaps minimised[^52]

### Urinals
- [ ] All urinals in all-gender facilities must be in enclosed privacy compartments or separate private area (IPC §405.3.5; UPC §422.7)[^14][^2]
- [ ] UPC: Urinal compartments comply with Z124.10 Type A[^15]
- [ ] Consider substituting water closets for urinals in all-gender settings (California permits this)[^15]

### Layout & Circulation
- [ ] Minimum two points of ingress/egress with looping circulation[^56]
- [ ] Consider doorless/grill entry to eliminate queuing barriers while maintaining visual privacy from corridor[^57][^56]
- [ ] Shared open handwashing/grooming area outside stall zone (common sink area)[^55]
- [ ] Bright, open common area lighting for safety perception[^58]
- [ ] Acoustic partition specifications (STC-rated assemblies) where full-height hard walls not used[^56]
- [ ] Eliminate sightlines from exterior corridor into stall area[^56]

### Accessible Design Integration
- [ ] All-gender facilities must also comply with ADA (US) / CSA B651 (Canada) accessibility requirements
- [ ] At minimum one accessible all-gender stall per facility
- [ ] If all-gender is provided as the primary restroom type, all fixtures per ICC A117.1 (IPC) or CSA B651 (NBC)[^4]
- [ ] Adult change table in at least one all-gender or universal washroom (per emerging best practice in Canadian venues)[^64][^69]

### Signage
- [ ] IPC 2024 §403.4: Signage must state male, female, or **"all persons regardless of sex"**[^7]
- [ ] Replace gendered stick-figure pictograms with function-based icons (toilet, sink, urinal)[^62][^60]
- [ ] Include bilingual (English/French) text for Canadian venues
- [ ] Tactile (raised) text and Braille per ADA/CSA accessibility requirements
- [ ] Signage at main building entrance indicating all-gender washroom locations[^61]
- [ ] San Francisco/California: Signage must comply with California Title 24 or ADA accessibility standards[^47]

### Human Rights Compliance (Canadian Venues)
- [ ] Confirm applicable provincial human rights code (Ontario OHRC, BC HRC, AHRA, etc.)
- [ ] Review OHRC Policy §13.4 / equivalent provincial guidance on washroom access based on lived gender identity[^27]
- [ ] Ensure washroom policy does not require trans patrons to use separate facilities or produce documentation
- [ ] Train venue staff on duty to accommodate trans and gender non-conforming patrons
- [ ] Post washroom policy at venue entry or on venue website

## 12. Knowledge Gaps and Limitations

The following areas could not be fully verified from available sources and should be supplemented with primary code documents for specific projects:
