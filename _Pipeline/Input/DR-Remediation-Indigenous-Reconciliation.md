# Indigenous Reconciliation and Cultural Programming in Canadian Public Assembly Venues

**Reference Document — DR PROMPT R-05 | April 2026**
**Resolves DELTA-008 (HIGH): Primary TRC source citation required**

***

## Executive Summary

This document provides an authoritative reference on Indigenous reconciliation practices and cultural programming in Canadian public assembly venues, grounded in primary source citation of the Truth and Reconciliation Commission of Canada's 94 Calls to Action (released June 2, 2015). It replaces the secondary TicketFairy citation identified in DELTA-008 with direct citation to the NCTR-hosted primary document and Government of Canada implementation tracking resources.

Ten years after their release, the TRC Calls to Action remain substantially incomplete: the Assembly of First Nations reported in September 2025 that no new Calls to Action were completed in the 2024–2025 year, with approximately 13–15 completed in total. The government of Canada's own assessment — that 85% are "completed or well underway" — is disputed by Indigenous organizations. This contested implementation landscape is the essential backdrop for understanding venue operators' obligations and opportunities.[^1][^2][^3]

At the ten-year mark of the TRC Calls to Action's release, there is significant divergence in how completion is counted:

| Tracking Organization | Completed CTAs | Status as of |
|----------------------|---------------|--------------|
| Assembly of First Nations | ~13 | September 2025[^2] |

The Yellowhead Institute — which produced five years of independent annual accountability reports — ceased publishing in 2024, stating "there are limits to how many times you can write a report about how Canada, once again, has failed to make any meaningful progress". The AFN's September 2025 report card found **no new Calls to Action completed in the 2024–2025 year**.[^2][^19]

Of the venue-relevant CTAs, the clearly completed items are: CTA 13 (language rights acknowledgement), CTA 14 (Indigenous Languages Act), CTA 15 (Languages Commissioner), CTA 80 (National Day for Truth and Reconciliation), and CTA 83 (Canada Council for the Arts reconciliation funding). CTAs 87–91 (Sports and Reconciliation) remain in progress, with CTA 91 now seeing its most significant real-world implementation through the FIFA 2026 Vancouver agreement.

## Part 2: Territorial Land Acknowledgment Best Practices for Venues

### 2.1 Origins and Significance

Land acknowledgments have been used traditionally by Indigenous peoples since time immemorial as cultural and political tools used by sovereign nations. Following the TRC's Final Report and 94 Calls to Action, land acknowledgments in non-Indigenous spaces delivered by non-Indigenous peoples became important gestures of reconciliation, acknowledging that Indigenous sovereignty has existed in Canada since time immemorial. They are not mandated by a specific TRC Call to Action but are widely understood as responsive to the spirit of CTAs 43–45 (reconciliation relationships), CTA 92 (corporate sector reconciliation), and the general reconciliation framework.[^20]

### 2.2 What Distinguishes Meaningful from Performative Acknowledgments

The central distinction — widely documented across Indigenous and professional organizations — is whether the acknowledgment is **accompanied by concrete action** or is merely recited words:

- A meaningful land acknowledgment "includes calls to action that seek to motivate their audiences," encouraging donation to Indigenous organizations, supporting Indigenous-owned businesses, or furthering education[^20]
- A performative acknowledgment functions as "an aggressor merely acknowledging aggression" without the presence of Indigenous people and knowledge, or any action that follows[^21]
- Territory acknowledgments "must go beyond words to include concrete commitments to reconciliation, such as supporting Indigenous-led initiatives" and language revitalization programs[^22]
- WordCamp Canada (2024) noted: "If a Land Acknowledgement becomes tokenistic, routine or just performative it has no meaning at all"[^23]

Key principles from published guidance by Indigenous and professional organizations:

**What makes an acknowledgment meaningful:**
- **Specificity**: Name the exact Nation(s) whose territory you occupy — not generic "Indigenous peoples"[^24][^20]
- **Active voice and present tense**: Indigenous peoples are active community members, not historical figures[^20]
- **Commitment language**: State specific actions your organization will take toward reconciliation[^22][^23]
- **Research and consultation**: Developed in genuine partnership with the relevant Nation(s), not drafted unilaterally[^25][^26]
- **Leadership delivery**: The highest-ranking non-Indigenous person of the organization should deliver the acknowledgment to signal institutional commitment[^20]
- **Elder or Knowledge Holder presence**: Where possible, invite an Elder from the traditional territory to open the gathering, following their lead on timing and form[^25]
- **Indigenous language elements**: Incorporate Nation-specific language forms where appropriate and invited[^22]

**What makes an acknowledgment performative:**
- Boilerplate language copy-pasted from another institution's statement without local research[^27]
- No follow-up action or organizational commitment to reconciliation[^23]
- Delivered perfunctorily at the top of every event without context or explanation[^23]
- Generic references to "the land" without naming specific Nations or acknowledging treaty status[^24]

### 2.3 Territorial Identification Resources

Published by Indigenous or Indigenous-led organizations:

- **Native Land Digital** (native-land.ca): Interactive map identifying Indigenous territories, languages, and treaties by location; updated as a living document[^28][^24]
- **Whose Land** (whoseland.ca): Interactive mapping tool specifically designed for Canadian land acknowledgment research, identifying territories, treaties, and Nations[^29]
- **CAUT Guide to Acknowledging First Peoples and Traditional Territory**: Province-by-province templates developed in consultation with Indigenous university councils or advisory groups, explicitly noting multiple or contested acknowledgments[^30][^27]
- **RAIC Land Acknowledgement Resource Guide** (Royal Architectural Institute of Canada, updated December 2025): Guidance for the built environment sector[^24]

### 2.4 Venue-Specific Acknowledgment Development Process

The following process synthesizes guidance from the Law Society of Alberta (June 2025), First Nations Health Authority (BC), BC College of Nurses and Midwives (BCCNM), and Evergreen Civic-Indigenous Engagement Protocols:[^31][^32][^33][^20]

**Phase 1 — Research (4–8 weeks minimum)**
1. Identify which Nation(s) hold traditional territory at the venue's location using Native Land Digital or Whose Land
2. Research treaty status (treaty land, unceded, shared/overlapping territories) — note that treaty and unceded territories have different implications
3. Identify the Nation's current governance body (Band Council, Tribal Council, or equivalent)
4. Review whether the Nation has published preferred acknowledgment language on their website

**Phase 2 — Consultation (timeline varies; allow 3–6 months)**
1. Contact the Nation's governance office; explain your organization's purpose, scope of events, and reason for seeking guidance
2. Offer to compensate the Nation for consultation time; do not ask the community to do the work for free
3. Ask if the Nation has a preferred statement for third-party use or prefers to develop language collaboratively
4. Determine whether the Nation wishes to deliver acknowledgments at major events themselves (preferred practice) or if they permit non-Indigenous delivery
5. Ask about Indigenous language pronunciation for Nation names and territory terms — obtain audio guides where available

**Phase 3 — Integration**
- **Event openings**: Acknowledgment delivered at the start of all public events by the most senior present representative of the venue operator
- **Permanent signage**: Display at main entrance points and on the venue website; the First Nations Health Authority notes acknowledgments can take the form of "policy, mission statements, posters and artworks, and myriad other creative ways"[^31]
- **Printed/digital event materials**: Include territorial acknowledgment in programs, event apps, and pre-show slides
- **Multi-day events**: A single acknowledgment on Day 1 is generally sufficient for continuous gatherings of the same group[^25]
- **Virtual events**: The host acknowledges the territory they themselves are on; acknowledge that attendees may be on different territories[^27]

**Phase 4 — Review and Relationship Maintenance**
- Revisit language with the Nation annually or when significant changes occur
- Document the consultation process and relationship as part of venue reconciliation records
- Build toward longer-term partnership commitments (programming, procurement, employment)

## Part 3: Indigenous Artist Programming in Canadian Venues

### 3.1 The Sector Context

Indigenous art contributed over $1 billion to the Canadian economy in 2018, with Indigenous artists directly earning $486 million net of expenses and supporting approximately 14,620 full-time-equivalent positions. This economic reality frames Indigenous artist engagement not as a cost or accommodation but as a significant cultural-economic sector with established professional norms.[^42]

TRC Call to Action 83 directly addresses arts programming: the Canada Council for the Arts is called to establish as a funding priority a strategy for Indigenous and non-Indigenous artists to undertake collaborative reconciliation-oriented projects. This Call is completed, meaning Canada Council funding for reconciliation-oriented arts commissioning is available.[^43][^11][^4]

### 3.2 Models of Integration

**Dedicated Department / Permanent Programming Stream**

The National Arts Centre (Ottawa) represents the most advanced model of Indigenous arts integration in a major Canadian venue. Key milestones:
- October 2015: Initial talks on Indigenous Theatre department between NAC leadership and Indigenous performers/community leaders nationwide[^44]
- March 2016: Formal announcement of new Department of Indigenous Theatre as part of NAC's 2015–2020 strategic plan — described as "the most significant thing we've done since 1969"[^45]
- June 2017: Kevin Loring (Nlaka'pamux, from Lytton, BC) appointed first artistic director of NAC Indigenous Theatre[^44]
- June 2018: $1M+ raised for the department through a tribute dinner[^44]
- Fall 2019: First full Indigenous Theatre season launched[^44]
- 2024–25 season: Programming across nations including *Homelands* (multimedia women's connection to land), *Dancers of Damelahamid* (Northwest Coast culture), *Bear Grease Christmas Special*, *Qaumma* (Inuit women and cultural protection), *The Secret to Good Tea* (residential school legacy/reconciliation), and *Marguerite: le feu* (French-language, fight for justice)[^46]
- Ongoing: The #ReconcileThis Indigenous Voices Online project[^47]
- Budget: Comparable to NAC's English Theatre (~$4M) and French Theatre (~$2.2M) departments[^45]

**Annual Themed Event / Celebration Day**

The Vancouver Whitecaps' annual *Indigenous Peoples Celebration* at BC Place (3rd annual in 2023) exemplifies the themed event model:
- 75+ Indigenous youth from across the region participating in a pre-match pitch jamboree[^48]
- Indigenous vendor marketplace and performers at Terry Fox Plaza from 4 hours before kickoff[^48]
- Indigenous artists' work available for purchase[^48]
- Nations (Katzie First Nation and others) as active participants, not merely symbolic[^48]

**September 30 / National Day for Truth and Reconciliation Programming**

Given CTA 80's completion (federal statutory holiday), September 30 is now an anchoring date in all venue programming calendars. Programming models include:
- CFL Week 17: League-wide orange and white pre-game jerseys; Indigenous-designed logos with proceeds to Indigenous organizations; anthems and starting lineups announced in Indigenous languages; hosting residential school Survivors and families; performances by Indigenous musicians, throat singers, dancers[^49]
- CFL explicitly links these initiatives to TRC CTAs 83, 87, 88, and 92[^49]
- Winnipeg Jets at Canada Life Centre: Annual Winnipeg Aboriginal Sport Achievement Centre (WASAC) night (8th annual in January 2026)[^50][^51]
- Vancouver Canucks: First Nations Chiefs deliver live land acknowledgments on-ice; Indigenous artists design "Every Child Matters" t-shirts with proceeds to Orange Shirt Society[^52]
- RBC Convention Centre Winnipeg: Hosted the 5th Annual Orange Shirt Day Healing Walk and Pow Wow (September 30, 2025)[^53]

**Public Art Commissioning**

The emerging precedent for new venue construction is to commission Indigenous public art as permanent venue infrastructure:
- **Scotia Place Calgary** (under construction): 4 outdoor public art commissions; 2 specifically dedicated to Indigenous cultural connections:[^54][^55]
  - *"Home Fire"* ($1.5M CAD budget): Symbolizes the home fire as gathering/story-exchange place; live flame; open call for mid-career/established Indigenous artists from Treaty Seven or Historic Northwest Métis Homeland
  - *"East Façade"* ($150K): Integrated panels on the east side facing the Elbow River and Elbow River Camp; open call for emerging Indigenous artists

Artist fees follow **CARFAC (Canadian Artists' Representation / Le Front des artistes canadiens) guidelines** for commissioning new works.[^55]

### 3.3 Revenue and Compensation Models

Best practices for fair compensation of Indigenous cultural practitioners at venues:

**Performing Arts**
- Apply standard CAEA (Canadian Actors' Equity Association) or equivalent union rates for Indigenous performers in professional productions
- For NAC Indigenous Theatre model: department budget parity with other theatre departments signals institutional commitment
- Canada Council for the Arts reconciliation funding (CTA 83, completed) is available for co-productions involving Indigenous artists[^11]

**Cultural Knowledge Holders and Elders**
Per BCCNM Guidelines for Working with Indigenous Elders and Knowledge Keepers and Carleton University Elder Guidelines:[^32][^56]
- Honoraria should be confirmed before any engagement and provided **on the day of the event**; Elders should never have to ask
- Honoraria is not payment for service but "a culturally appropriate exchange of things of value"[^32]
- Cover all associated costs: travel, transportation, parking, meals, accommodation
- If an Elder brings a Helper, the Helper also receives honorarium and/or gifts
- Minimum 4 weeks advance notice for confirming Indigenous Elders, Knowledge Keepers, and guest speakers[^57]
- Respect cultural protocols which may include tobacco, gifts, or other offerings — ask the community what is appropriate[^57]

**Visual Artists / Public Art**
- CARFAC fee schedule for commissioned works
- Percentage of gross merchandise revenues for reproduced designs (e.g., team apparel featuring Indigenous design elements)
- Proceeds from reconciliation merchandise (jerseys, t-shirts) should go to designated Indigenous organizations as agreed with the artist/designer[^52]

## Part 4: Community Consultation Process

### 4.1 The Distinction Between Meaningful and Tokenistic Consultation

Meaningful consultation — as defined in Indigenous law, Canadian common law, and UNDRIP — is substantively different from box-ticking engagement:

| Meaningful Consultation | Tokenistic Inclusion |
|------------------------|---------------------|
| Nation-to-Nation relationship as the foundation | Treating Nation as a stakeholder among many |
| Initiated early, before key decisions are made | Seeking approval after decisions are effectively finalized |
| Nation has genuine capacity to influence outcomes | Outcome predetermined; "consultation" is documentation only |
| Venue compensates community for consultation time | Community expected to advise for free |
| Long-term relationship, not project-specific | Engagement ends after the event or opening |
| Nation's own protocols govern the process | Venue's administrative timelines govern the process |
| Free, Prior, and Informed Consent (FPIC) framework | Notification and comment period |

Sources: RCAANC consultation protocols; CAID Meaningful Consultation framework; CRTC Indigenous Co-development Wise Practices (2024).[^58][^59][^60]

### 4.2 Identifying Appropriate Nations and Governing Bodies

A venue's territory may encompass traditional lands of multiple Nations, overlapping or shared territories, and/or Treaty lands with specific rights-holders. The identification process:

1. **Use territorial mapping tools**: Native Land Digital and Whose Land identify Nations, languages, and treaties by precise location[^28][^29]
2. **Distinguish Treaty vs. unceded territory**: Treaty land has specific rights-holders under the treaty; unceded land means no treaty was signed and original sovereignty was never extinguished
3. **Identify current governance structure**: First Nation Band Councils (federal Indian Act governance), Tribal Councils (representing multiple First Nations), Métis Nation governing bodies (e.g., Métis Nation of Ontario, Métis Nation of Alberta), or Inuit organizations depending on territory
4. **Where territories overlap**: Engage all Nations whose territories overlap the venue location; do not select one and ignore others
5. **Consult legal counsel** on duty to consult obligations where applicable (government-owned venues have stronger obligations than privately operated ones)[^61]

### 4.3 Formal Partnership Agreements

Beyond initial consultation, venue operators should pursue **formal partnership agreements** that:
- Define ongoing rights and responsibilities between the venue and the Nation(s)
- Establish regular relationship touchpoints (not just event-specific contact)
- Include economic participation provisions (procurement, employment, revenue-sharing)
- Address cultural programming rights (which cultural elements may be displayed/performed and under what conditions)
- Reference the UNDRIP framework (TRC CTA 92)[^4]

The FIFA World Cup 2026 MOU between BC, City of Vancouver, and Musqueam, Squamish, Tsleil-Waututh Nations (signed June 27, 2025) is a benchmark example: it commits all parties to collaborate on planning, staging, and hosting of matches, ensuring Nations' interests are reflected throughout, and approaches to shared opportunities are co-developed. The Nations received $18M from the FIFA hosting budget.[^62][^63][^14]

### 4.4 Protocol for Events and Ceremonies

Indigenous cultural protocols for events should be governed by the Nation involved, not by the venue's standard event template. Key considerations per Evergreen's Civic-Indigenous Engagement Guide:[^33]

- **Ceremony**: Recognize Indigenous ancestry of place/land as a significant and sacred element, not a formality
- **Land acknowledgment**: Delivered either before or after the opening ceremony, following the Elder or Knowledge Holder's lead on timing[^25]
- **Openings and closings**: Smudging, prayer, drumming, song — these are ceremonial protocols with specific participation norms that should be explained to non-Indigenous audiences without trivializing
- **Photography/recording**: Some ceremonial activities are not to be recorded; confirm with knowledge holders before any documentation
- **Seating and spatial arrangements**: Some ceremonies have specific spatial requirements; build flexibility into venue floor plans
- **Food and refreshments**: Culturally appropriate offerings may differ from standard venue catering; confirm requirements in advance

### 4.5 Community Consultation Process Checklist

**Pre-Consultation**
- [ ] Identify the Nation(s) on whose territory the venue sits using Native Land Digital / Whose Land
- [ ] Determine treaty or unceded status; identify current governance body
- [ ] Assign a dedicated staff contact for the relationship (not a rotating position)
- [ ] Allocate budget for consultation honoraria, travel, and community capacity support
- [ ] Review the Nation's own website for published guidance, preferred acknowledgments, or contact protocols

**Initiation**
- [ ] Contact the Nation's governance office (phone call or formal letter preferred over email for initial contact)
- [ ] Explain the venue, the events hosted, and the purpose of seeking a relationship
- [ ] Ask how the Nation prefers to engage (community meeting, designated liaison, working group)
- [ ] Offer to visit the Nation's community rather than requiring them to come to the venue
- [ ] Do NOT present pre-drafted acknowledgment language or programming plans at the first meeting

**Ongoing Engagement**
- [ ] Establish regular touchpoints (quarterly minimum) outside of specific event requests
- [ ] Invite Nation representatives to venue tours and programming briefings
- [ ] Identify Indigenous community members who may be interested in employment, vendor opportunities, or programming roles
- [ ] Provide meaningful timeline for input; do not request urgent turnarounds
- [ ] Compensate all consultation time, including preparation and follow-up

**Cultural Events and Programming**
- [ ] Confirm Indigenous language forms for venue/territory names 4+ weeks ahead of event
- [ ] Book Elders and Knowledge Keepers minimum 4 weeks in advance[^57]
- [ ] Provide full agenda and role description in advance; confirm how the Elder/Knowledge Keeper wishes to be addressed
- [ ] Confirm honoraria amount and payment method in advance; pay on the day of the event[^32]
- [ ] Cover all travel, accommodation, and meals
- [ ] Confirm photography/recording permissions for ceremonial elements
- [ ] Ensure venue is physically accessible; brief staff on cultural protocols

**Post-Event**
- [ ] Send formal thank you to Nation representatives and Elders/Knowledge Keepers within 5 business days
- [ ] Provide event report or recording to Nation (if permitted)
- [ ] Document consultation activities for venue reconciliation records
- [ ] Review what worked and what could improve in the next engagement

## Part 5: Venue Case Study Catalog

### 5.1 Rogers Centre Ottawa (formerly Shaw Centre) — Ottawa, Ontario

**Territory**: Unceded Algonquin Anishinaabeg Territory[^36]

The venue, formerly known as the Shaw Centre, was renamed Rogers Centre Ottawa in 2024. As a convention centre on unceded Algonquin territory, it has become a significant venue for Indigenous event programming.

**Key Initiatives**:
- **2024 International Indigenous Tourism Conference (IITC)**: Hosted February 26–28, 2024, by the Indigenous Tourism Association of Canada (ITAC) — the world's largest Indigenous tourism conference, drawing 1,000+ delegates from over 10 countries[^64][^37][^65]
- Theme: "Where Nations Meet," a direct reference to Ottawa's three rivers and the diversity of Indigenous nations gathering[^64]
- Format included a Grand Entry ceremony, keynotes, plenary conversations, marketplace, and awards gala — bringing Indigenous ceremony into the convention centre context[^66]
- Shaw Centre also sponsored the Indigenous Experience at Mādahòkì Farm (an Indigenous cultural destination near Ottawa)[^67]
- All events at Rogers Centre Ottawa include territorial acknowledgment on the unceded Algonquin Anishinaabeg Territory[^36]

**Assessment**: The venue demonstrates strong capacity as a convener of Indigenous-led events; less documented evidence of venue-initiated Indigenous programming or a formal partnership agreement with Algonquin Nations.

### 5.2 Vancouver Convention Centre / BC Place (PavCo) — Vancouver, BC

**Territory**: Ancestral, unceded territories of xʷməθkʷəy̓əm (Musqueam), Sḵwx̱wú7mesh (Squamish), and Sel̓íl̓witulh (Tsleil-Waututh) Nations[^68]

Note on BMO disambiguation: **BC Place** is the stadium venue operated by PavCo in Vancouver; it is home to the BC Lions (CFL) and Vancouver Whitecaps FC (MLS). "**BMO Field**" is in Toronto (home of Toronto FC). There is no "BMO Stadium" in Vancouver. The prompt's reference to "BMO Stadium (Vancouver)" appears to conflate BMO Field (Toronto) with BC Place (Vancouver). Vancouver Whitecaps FC and BC Lions both play at BC Place.

**Key Initiatives**:
- **PavCo Accessibility Plan 2023–2026**: Formally acknowledges both the VCC and BC Place as being on the unceded territories of the three Nations[^68]
- **Vancouver Whitecaps Indigenous Peoples Celebration** (annual, BC Place): 3rd annual in June 2023 featured 75+ Indigenous youth pitch jamboree, Indigenous vendor marketplace from 4 hours pre-kickoff, artists' work for sale, Katzie First Nation involvement[^48]
- **FIFA World Cup 2026 Partnership** (BC Place, June–July 2026): Historic MOU signed June 27, 2025 between Musqueam, Squamish, Tsleil-Waututh Nations, Province of BC, and City of Vancouver; Nations are recognized as **co-hosts**; $18M allocated to the three Nations from FIFA hosting budget for infrastructure, recreational programs, and community welfare; directly implements TRC CTA 91 (Indigenous territorial protocols at international sporting events)[^63][^14][^62]
- The Nations' interests are to be reflected "throughout" all planning and staging[^14]

**Assessment**: BC Place represents an emerging benchmark for CTA 91 implementation through the FIFA co-hosting model. The formal Nation co-host framework, with substantial financial recognition, goes significantly beyond acknowledgment toward genuine partnership.

### 5.3 Canada Life Centre — Winnipeg, Manitoba

**Territory**: Treaty One Territory (Anishinaabe, Cree, Oji-Cree, Dakota, and Dene peoples, and the Métis Nation)[^69]

**Key Initiatives**:
- **Winnipeg Jets / Winnipeg Aboriginal Sport Achievement Centre (WASAC) Night**: Annual event held at Canada Life Centre; 8th annual edition held in January 2026[^51][^50]
- The event celebrates Indigenous culture and supports the WASAC organization, which provides sports programming to Indigenous youth in Winnipeg
- Jets commemorate National Day for Truth and Reconciliation (September 30) with reconciliation programming[^50]
- City of Winnipeg's Journey of Reconciliation: As of December 2018, 8,000+ employees completed mandatory Indigenous awareness training; the city's Indigenous Youth Strategy supports 300+ Indigenous youth annually in employment, training, and recreation[^69]

**Assessment**: The WASAC night represents a multi-year, relationship-based initiative (8+ years) rather than a one-off commemoration — an important model of sustained engagement. Documentation of formal venue-Nation partnership agreements is limited in publicly available sources.

### 5.4 Scotiabank Arena — Toronto, Ontario

**Territory**: Lands of the Huron-Wendat, Seneca, and Mississaugas of the Credit River; home to many Indigenous people from across Turtle Island[^70]

Note: "Scotiabank Arena" refers to the naming rights sponsorship (Scotiabank). The venue itself is operated by Maple Leaf Sports & Entertainment (MLSE). Indigenous initiatives documented here reflect Scotiabank's corporate reconciliation commitments as naming sponsor; MLSE-specific programming is covered separately in sports league initiatives.

**Key Initiatives**:
- **Scotiabank Truth & Reconciliation Action Plan** (released October 15, 2024): 37 specific commitments across 6 pillars — Leadership & Governance, Education, Employment, Community, Client & Third Party, and Environment & Social Impact[^15][^16]
- Developed through 18 months of consultation with Indigenous rightsholders inside and outside the organization, plus focus groups with employees nationwide[^15]
- Co-developed with Indigenous employees and external Indigenous rightsholders[^15]
- Bank holds **Partnership Accreditation in Indigenous Relations (PAIR) certification** from the Canadian Council for Indigenous Business[^16]
- Scotiabank's Orange Shirt Day programming since 2021, aligned with National Day for Truth and Reconciliation[^71]

**Assessment**: As a naming sponsor, Scotiabank's 37-commitment Action Plan is a documented corporate reconciliation framework. The PAIR certification provides third-party verification. The venue operator (MLSE) also runs annual NDTR programming — documented through CFL and NHL league-wide initiatives for Toronto-based games.

### 5.5 National Arts Centre — Ottawa, Ontario

**Territory**: Unceded Algonquin Anishinaabeg Territory[^40]

**Key Initiatives**:
- **Indigenous Theatre Department** (launched 2016, first season 2019): The most comprehensive Indigenous arts integration in a major Canadian performing arts venue[^72][^45][^44]
- Department budget comparable to English Theatre (~$4M) and French Theatre (~$2.2M) — signals institutional equality, not add-on status[^45]
- Artistic Director Kevin Loring (Nlaka'pamux, hired June 2017) has full creative authority; programming decisions are Indigenous-led[^44]
- Department mission: increase representation of Indigenous peoples through theatre; provide space and resources for Indigenous actors and playwrights to thrive[^44]
- **2024–25 Season** includes works spanning First Nations, Inuit, and Métis storytelling forms across multiple genres (dance, theatre, multimedia, French-language):[^46]
  - *Homelands* — women's connection to land and waterways (choreographed multimedia)
  - *Dancers of Damelahamid* — Northwest Coast culture and storytelling (with NAC Dance)
  - *Bear Grease Christmas Special* — crowd-favourite holiday programming
  - *Qaumma* — Inuit women protecting culture and language
  - *The Secret to Good Tea* — post-residential school resilience and reconciliation (described as "hilarious" and emotionally layered)[^46]
  - *Marguerite: le feu* — French-language work on justice and memory
- #ReconcileThis: Indigenous Voices Online project, running continuously since 2021[^47]

**Assessment**: The NAC Indigenous Theatre represents the most thoroughly documented and institutionally integrated Indigenous programming model in Canadian performing arts venues. Its budget parity, Indigenous leadership, year-round season structure, and explicit reconciliation mandate make it the reference standard for performing arts centres.

## Part 6: Indigenous Languages and Multilingual Services

### 6.1 TRC Framework

CTAs 13–15 (all completed) established the federal obligation to acknowledge Indigenous language rights, enact legislation, and appoint a Commissioner. The Indigenous Languages Act (2019) governs the framework, with $1.1B in federal investment 2019–2029. CTA 16 (post-secondary language programs) remains in progress. The Act's Section 10.1 provides that federal institutions "may, in accordance with the regulations, provide access to services in an Indigenous language, if the" capacity and demand exists.[^6][^79]

### 6.2 Venue Language Implementation Examples

The direct application of Indigenous language integration to venue operations remains at an emerging stage in Canada, with stronger examples in adjacent sectors:

| Sector | Initiative | Relevance to Venues |
|--------|-----------|---------------------|
| Campus wayfinding | UBC Musqueam bilingual street signs: 9 sign types at 54 campus locations in Musqueam (hən̓q̓əmin̓əm̓) alongside English[^80] | Model for venue entrance, lobby, and wayfinding signage |
| National trail signage | Trans Canada Trail: updated wayfinding signage in Indigenous languages in Nova Scotia, Ontario; Manitoba and Nunavut upcoming[^81] | Model for outdoor venue signage |
| Municipal planning | Yellowknife Wayfinding Strategy: recommends bilingual welcome signs at airport and key locations in Indigenous languages[^82] | Municipal precedent for venue signage standards |
| Sports events | CFL NDTR Week 17: starting lineups announced in Indigenous languages; anthem performed by Indigenous singers[^49] | Event-specific language integration model |
| Sports events | Vancouver Canucks NDTR 2025: live land acknowledgment delivered by First Nations Chiefs (in their own languages and forms)[^52] | Ceremonial language model for sports venue events |
| Convention | International Indigenous Tourism Conference (Rogers Centre Ottawa 2024): Grand Entry ceremony conducted per Indigenous protocols including language[^66] | Convention/conference model |

### 6.3 Practical Implementation Guidance for Venues

- **Signage**: Consult with the Nation to determine whether and how they wish their language represented on venue signage; Nations may have orthography preferences and may wish to control use of their language forms
- **PA/audio**: Pre-event announcements can include Nation-specific welcome greetings in Indigenous language with English/French; obtain audio pronunciation guides from Nation language keepers
- **Event programs**: Include Nation name in correct spelling/orthography; include pronunciation guide for audience members
- **Staff training**: Where Indigenous language use is introduced, train relevant staff on correct pronunciation and cultural context — avoid superficial transliteration that distorts language
- **Collaboration over extraction**: Indigenous languages are not content resources to be "used" by venues — they are living systems governed by the Nations that speak them. Language integration should always be co-developed with Nation language keepers, never unilaterally applied

## Part 7: Sports and Reconciliation Implementation Tracker

### 7.1 CTA 87 Operational Context

CTA 87 calls for **public education** on "the national story of Aboriginal athletes in history" — a substantive educational obligation, not merely ceremonial recognition. The Manitoba Aboriginal Sports and Recreation Council (MASRC) contextualizes CTAs 87–91 as a call to:[^83][^4]
- Follow Indigenous sport news and events weekly
- Complete the Aboriginal Coaching Module
- Learn and share information about Indigenous athletes, coaches, officials, and sport developers
- Support Indigenous athletes for awards and nominations[^84]

### 7.2 League and Team-Level Initiatives

| League / Team | Initiative | TRC CTA Alignment |
|---------------|-----------|-------------------|
| **CFL** (league-wide) | Week 17 (Sept. 30 games): Orange and white pre-game jerseys for all dressed players; jersey proceeds to Indigenous organizations; Indigenous-designed logos; anthems and lineups in Indigenous languages; hosting of Survivors and families; performances by Indigenous musicians, throat singers, dancers[^49] | CTAs 83, 87, 88, 92[^49] |
| **Vancouver Canucks (NHL)** | Annual NDTR preseason games: First Nations Chiefs deliver live on-ice land acknowledgments; Indigenous artists design "Every Child Matters" t-shirt; proceeds to Orange Shirt Society[^52][^85] | CTAs 80, 87, 91 |
| **Winnipeg Jets (NHL)** | Annual WASAC Night at Canada Life Centre (8th annual, Jan 2026): celebration of Indigenous culture through hockey[^50][^51] | CTAs 87, 88, 90 |
| **Vancouver Whitecaps FC (MLS)** | Annual Indigenous Peoples Celebration at BC Place: youth jamboree, vendor marketplace, Nations as active participants[^48] | CTAs 87, 91 |
| **Orange Jersey Project** | 75,000+ orange jerseys delivered to youth hockey teams across Canada; conversations about truth and reconciliation in locker rooms[^86] | CTA 87, 88 |
| **BC Place / FIFA World Cup 2026** | Historic MOU with Musqueam, Squamish, Tsleil-Waututh as co-hosts; $18M to Nations for infrastructure and legacy programs[^63][^14] | CTA 91 |
| **Southern Chiefs' Organization** | 5th Annual Orange Shirt Day Healing Walk and Pow Wow at RBC Convention Centre, Winnipeg, September 30, 2025[^53] | CTA 80 |

### 7.3 Venue Operator vs. Team-Driven Initiatives

An important operational distinction for venue operators:

**Team-driven initiatives** (most common): Sports franchises (Canucks, Jets, Whitecaps, CFL clubs) design and execute Indigenous reconciliation programming as their own. The venue operator's role is to facilitate — ensuring production capacity, PA access, floor/surface accommodations for ceremony, staffing cultural awareness.

**Venue-operator-driven initiatives** (emerging): The venue operator independently commissions, programs, or permanently installs Indigenous content, independent of tenant teams. Scotia Place Calgary's public art commissions are a leading example of venue-operator-driven Indigenous integration. The Vancouver Convention Centre's territorial acknowledgment embedded in all event documentation is another.

**Hybrid/collaborative model** (best practice): Venue operator and tenants co-develop a reconciliation framework that ensures Indigenous programming occurs regardless of which tenant event is active — including non-sporting events (conferences, trade shows, concerts) that may not otherwise have reconciliation programming mandates.
