### A-4: Safety-And-Risk-Lightning-Severe-Weather.md
**Domain:** safety-and-risk
**Enrichment type:** Substantial -- corrects "15/12/8-mile" claim, adds NWS toolkit specifics, NFPA 780, StormReady program

**Claims to supplement:**
1. NWS Toolkit establishes two operationally distinct resumption criteria -- NOT the "15/12/8-mile" three-phase system. With professional meteorologist + real-time lightning data: resume when lightning has moved beyond 8 miles and threat of new lightning within 8 miles has ended. Without professional meteorologist: wait 30 minutes after last observed thunder or lightning.
2. The "15/12/8-mile" three-radius system used in some commercial/sports protocols does not appear verbatim in official NWS publications. Likely derives from commercial provider documentation (Perry Weather, Earth Networks) or sports governing body protocols layered on NWS base guidance.
3. The "30-30 Rule": seek shelter when flash-to-thunder count <=30 seconds (lightning within ~6 miles); wait 30 minutes after last thunder before returning outdoors.
4. NWS notes "significant lightning threat extends outward from the base of a thunderstorm cloud about 6 to 10 miles."
5. NWS StormReady Recognition Program for outdoor venues: voluntary program covering outdoor sporting arenas, concert venues, water parks, raceways, amusement parks. Requirements across 4 categories (Information Reception, Decision Support, Public Notification, Protection Program). Recertification every 3 years.
6. NFPA 780 (2023 Edition) provides lightning protection system installation requirements for "ordinary structures" and "miscellaneous structures and special occupancies" including assembly venue structures. Not mandatory unless adopted by local AHJ. Uses Rolling Sphere Method and Protection Angle Method. Integrates with NFPA 70 (NEC) grounding requirements.
7. Canada guidance (Environment and Climate Change Canada) consistent with NWS: activities do not resume until at least 30 minutes after last rumble of thunder.
8. Commercial lightning detection providers: Vaisala/NLDN (100+ ground-based sensors, ~84m accuracy, 40+ years continuous operation); Perry Weather (NLDN data, <20 second alerts, WBGT monitoring); Earth Networks Total Lightning Network (~100m accuracy, in-cloud + cloud-to-ground detection); DTN (broader meteorological intelligence services).

### A-5: Safety-And-Risk-Mass-Casualty-Incident-Planning.md
**Domain:** safety-and-risk
**Enrichment type:** Substantial -- START/SALT triage origins with primary citations, NFPA 1026 IC competencies

**Claims to supplement:**
1. START (Simple Triage and Rapid Treatment) developed 1983 by Newport Beach Fire and Marine Department and Hoag Hospital, California. Key co-developer: Victoria Cleary, EMS Manager. 4 categories: Deceased/Expectant (Black), Immediate (Red), Delayed (Yellow), Walking Wounded/Minor (Green). Assessment sequence: Respiration, Perfusion, Mental Status. 1996 modification by Benson et al. substituted radial pulse for capillary refill. Most widely used MCI triage system in the US.
2. SALT (Sort, Assess, Lifesaving Interventions, Treatment/Transport) developed 2008 by CDC-sponsored expert working group. 5 categories (adds Expectant separate from Dead, and Minimal separate from Delayed). Key differentiator: allows lifesaving interventions (hemorrhage control, airway, chest decompression) before tagging. Covers all ages, all hazards. MUCC compliant. Endorsed by ACEP, ACS Committee on Trauma, American Trauma Society, NAEMSP, NDLSEC, STIPDA.
3. SALT is compliant with Model Uniform Core Criteria (MUCC) -- the proposed US national standard for mass casualty triage. START is not MUCC compliant.
4. NFPA 1026 (2024 Edition) Chapter 4 establishes IC Job Performance Requirements including: incident size-up and command establishment, IAP development, resource management, communication operations, ICS integration, demobilization, and (new in 2024) continuing education/professional development.
5. NFPA 1026 aligns with NIMS ICS; FEMA IS-100/200/700 provide foundation; IS-300/400 approach NFPA 1026 IC JPR level. Pro Board or IFSAC certification provides formal professional qualification layer.
6. No jurisdiction identified that mandates NFPA 1026 specifically for private venue operators (vs. fire service personnel). Venue operators typically face ICS/NIMS requirements through permit conditions, special event licensing, or contractual obligations.
7. Texas Commission on Fire Protection certifies ICs per NFPA 1026 Chapter 4 (2024 Edition). TEEX offers NFPA 1026-compliant IC course.

### A-6: Safety-And-Risk-Medical-Staffing-Models.md
**Domain:** safety-and-risk
**Enrichment type:** Critical -- resolves "Saunders vs. Sanders" attribution error, provides complete staffing ratio table with primary sources, validates 95.97% statistic

**Claims to supplement:**
1. CORRECTION: "Saunders et al." in KB is almost certainly a misspelling of Sanders AB et al. (1986). No independently authored "Saunders" mass gathering framework could be verified. Correct citation: Sanders AB, Criss E, Steckl P, Meislin HW, Raife J, Allen D. "An analysis of medical care at mass gatherings." Ann Emerg Med. 1986 May;15(5):515-9. PMID 3963529.
2. Sanders 1986 established the foundational BLS/ALS response-time standard: BLS first aid <=4 minutes, ALS <=8 minutes, transport to medical facility <=30 minutes.
3. The comprehensive current staffing ratio framework comes from Schwartz B, Nafziger S, Milsten A, Luk J, Yancey A. "Mass Gathering Medical Care: Resource Document for the NAEMSP Position Statement." Prehospital Emergency Care. 2015;19(4):559-68. PMID 26270473.
4. Complete staffing ratio table: Physician 1:5,000-50,000; Nurse 1:2,600-15,000; EMT 1:2,600-65,000; Paramedic 2 (or 1 paramedic + 1 EMT) per 10,000; Basic First Aid 1:1,000.
5. The "12 physicians per 50,000 attendees" figure appears in EMRA's 2015 review attributing it to "Sanders, et al." and the broader NAEMSP Resource Document.
6. The 95.97% mild presentations statistic originates from Locoh-Donou S et al. "Mass-gathering medicine: a descriptive analysis..." Am J Emerg Med. 2013 May;31(5):843-6. PMID 23453125. Study: 79 events, 670 patient presentations, 917,307 cumulative attendance, October 2009-August 2011 at a southeastern US university. 95.97% mild; 27 cases required transport; 3 life-threatening.
7. The 0.5-2 per 1,000 patient presentation rate (PPR) benchmark is supported by three independent studies: Arbon et al. 2001 (PPR=0.992/1,000 across 201 Australian events, 12M+ attendees), Milsten et al. 2002 (literature review confirming 0.5-2 range), Sanders 1986 (foundational US survey).
8. NIH StatPearls synthesis chapter (Wolin J, Friedman M, "EMS Mass Gatherings," updated October 26, 2023, NCBI Bookshelf ID NBK597369) consolidates these benchmarks.
