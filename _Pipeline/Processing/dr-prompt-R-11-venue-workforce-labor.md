DR PROMPT R-11 — Venue Workforce Management and Labor Compliance

CONTEXT: The KB has 7 people-and-culture concepts but 2 are accepted HIGH delta items. Prevailing-Wage depends on a single Davis-Bacon source for 7+ statutory claims across federal, state, and provincial jurisdictions — a disproportionate traceability risk for a compliance-critical topic. Frontline-Engagement-AI-Adoption has an effective single source from one Qualtrics survey, leaving technology adoption claims under-corroborated. DEI Training Programs references specific providers (SHRM, diversity consultancies) without corresponding source notes. The domain is at minimum viable floor (7 concepts) but carries more unresolved audit findings than any other domain at this size.

RESEARCH OBJECTIVE: Produce comprehensive operational reference on venue workforce management — prevailing wage and labor compliance across US federal, state, and Canadian provincial jurisdictions; frontline technology adoption in venue operations; union and non-union staffing models; and DEI program frameworks specific to venue and events industry operations as of April 2026.

PRIMARY RESEARCH QUESTIONS:

1. PREVAILING WAGE AND LABOR COMPLIANCE: What are the prevailing wage requirements affecting public assembly venue construction and operations? Federal framework: Davis-Bacon Act, Contract Work Hours and Safety Standards Act (CWHSSA), McNamara-O'Hara Service Contract Act — applicability thresholds, covered vs. non-covered workers, enforcement mechanisms. State-level prevailing wage laws — which states have their own statutes, how do they interact with federal requirements, what are the key differences in coverage and rates? Canadian provincial equivalents — fair wage policies in Ontario, Alberta, British Columbia, Quebec. Compliance infrastructure: certified payroll requirements, apprenticeship ratios, fringe benefit calculations. Common violations and enforcement actions in venue construction and renovation projects. How do prevailing wage requirements interact with public authority venue governance structures?

2. FRONTLINE WORKER TECHNOLOGY ADOPTION: What is the current state of technology adoption among frontline venue workers? Workforce management platforms used in venue operations (UKG/Kronos, ADP, When I Work, HotSchedules, Deputy). Mobile communication tools for deskless workers (Beekeeper, Yoobic, Staffbase, Microsoft Viva). Digital training and onboarding platforms. Resistance patterns and adoption barriers specific to seasonal, part-time, and event-based workforces. Qualtrics and equivalent employee experience measurement platforms — what do they measure, how do venues use the data? Case studies from venues that have implemented frontline technology at scale. ROI metrics for workforce technology investment in venue settings.

3. UNION AND NON-UNION STAFFING MODELS IN VENUES: How do union and non-union staffing models operate in public assembly venues? IATSE (International Alliance of Theatrical Stage Employees) jurisdiction and contracts in arenas, convention centres, and performing arts centres. UNITE HERE representation in venue F&B and hospitality operations. Building trades unions in venue construction and maintenance. Collective bargaining structures — single-employer vs. multi-employer agreements. Right-to-work state implications for venue operations. Grievance procedures and arbitration in venue settings. How do hybrid staffing models work when some departments are unionized and others are not?

4. DEI TRAINING FRAMEWORKS FOR VENUE OPERATIONS: What DEI training frameworks and certification programs are applicable to venue operations? SHRM (Society for Human Resource Management) DEI competencies and certification paths. EEOC guidance on diversity training — legal boundaries and best practices. IAVM diversity and inclusion initiatives and publications. Industry-specific DEI challenges — seasonal workforce diversity, accessibility hiring, multilingual operations. Unconscious bias training program structures and evidence of effectiveness. Supplier diversity programs applicable to venue procurement. How are venues measuring DEI outcomes beyond training completion metrics?

5. SEASONAL AND EVENT-BASED WORKFORCE MANAGEMENT: How do venues manage the unique challenges of seasonal and event-based staffing? Rapid scaling models — going from 200 to 2,000+ staff for major events. Onboarding efficiency for short-tenure workers (event-by-event, seasonal contracts). Regulatory compliance for temporary and part-time workers (ACA thresholds, benefits eligibility, overtime rules). Return-rate optimization — strategies venues use to retain seasonal workers year over year. Scheduling algorithms and demand forecasting for event-driven staffing. Gig economy and on-demand staffing platforms entering the venue sector.

6. VOLUNTEER COORDINATION IN LARGE VENUES: What are the operational frameworks for managing volunteer workforces at scale? Volunteer management systems and technology platforms. Legal distinction between volunteers and employees — FLSA implications, insurance coverage, liability exposure. Training requirements and credential verification for volunteers. Volunteer recognition and retention programs. Fairground and festival volunteer models vs. performing arts volunteer (usher/docent) models. Integration of volunteer and paid staff operations during events. Canadian volunteer standards and provincial regulatory differences.

7. LABOR MARKET TRENDS FOR PUBLIC ASSEMBLY VENUES: What labor market dynamics are specific to the public assembly venue sector? BLS data on employment in arts, entertainment, and recreation (NAICS 71). Post-pandemic labor market shifts — wage compression, schedule flexibility demands, remote/hybrid expectations for venue administrative staff. Credential and certification pathways — CVP (Certified Venues Professional), CMP, CSEP. Career pathway models from entry-level to senior management in venue operations. Compensation benchmarking data for venue-specific roles. Immigration and work authorization considerations for international touring events.

SOURCE QUALITY:
- PRIORITY 1: US Department of Labor Wage and Hour Division (WHD) — Davis-Bacon guidance, Service Contract Act, FLSA interpretive bulletins; OSHA workforce safety standards; Canadian provincial labour ministry publications (Ontario, Alberta, BC)
- PRIORITY 2: SHRM — workforce management research, DEI competency models, compensation surveys; IAVM workforce-related publications and professional development materials; BLS Occupational Employment and Wage Statistics
- PRIORITY 3: Workforce management technology vendors with published case studies (UKG, ADP, Qualtrics) — factual operational data only, not marketing claims; IATSE and UNITE HERE public contract summaries and jurisdictional descriptions
- PRIORITY 4: Trade press — Venues Now, Pollstar (staffing/labor coverage), HR publications (HR Dive, SHRM Magazine), event industry trade associations (MPI, PCMA) workforce publications
- AVOID: Diversity training vendor marketing without outcome data. Blog posts or opinion pieces on DEI without cited frameworks. Staffing agency promotional content. Social media commentary on venue labor disputes.

OUTPUT STRUCTURE:
- Prevailing wage compliance matrix (federal, state, provincial — applicability, thresholds, enforcement)
- Frontline technology adoption landscape (platforms, adoption metrics, ROI frameworks)
- Union/non-union staffing model comparison (by venue type, department, jurisdiction)
- DEI training framework reference (SHRM/EEOC/IAVM guidance, measurement, legal boundaries)
- Seasonal workforce management guide (scaling models, compliance, retention strategies)
- Volunteer coordination operational framework (legal, technology, integration with paid staff)
- Venue labor market analysis (BLS data, credential pathways, compensation benchmarks)
