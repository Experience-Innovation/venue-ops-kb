DR PROMPT R-23 — Emerging and Specialty Venue Types

CONTEXT: The venue-ops-kb's 26-domain taxonomy maps operational functions (event operations, facilities, safety, F&B, etc.) that apply across venue types, and the venue_type cross-cutting dimension includes 11 categories (convention-centre, arena, stadium, performing-arts-centre, integrated-resort, theme-park, major-attraction, amphitheatre, fairground-exhibition, university-venue, all). However, the concept notes themselves are implicitly written from the operational perspective of traditional venue formats — conventional arenas, standard convention centres, traditional performing arts centres. Zero concepts address the operational realities of next-generation venue formats that are actively reshaping the industry: immersive LED environments (MSG Sphere), dedicated eSports arenas, experiential entertainment venues (Meow Wolf, teamLab), heritage/historic venue operations under preservation constraints, temporary/pop-up event infrastructure, or convertible/retractable venues. These formats create operational requirements that don't map cleanly to traditional venue management frameworks — a venue where the building IS the content (Sphere), a venue that operates continuously rather than event-by-event (Meow Wolf), a venue that exists for 72 hours then disappears (festival infrastructure), or a venue where the physical structure transforms between configurations (retractable roof, convertible floor). The av-and-production domain (13 concepts) covers production infrastructure for traditional venues. The event-operations domain (12 concepts) covers traditional event execution. The facilities-and-building-systems domain (15 concepts) covers building operations for conventional structures. None address what changes when the venue format itself is non-traditional.

RESEARCH OBJECTIVE: Comprehensive research on emerging and specialty venue types — MSG Sphere and immersive LED environments, eSports arenas, experiential entertainment venues, heritage/historic venues, temporary/pop-up event infrastructure, dome/retractable roof operations, and how these formats create new operational requirements not covered by traditional venue management frameworks as of April 2026.

PRIMARY RESEARCH QUESTIONS:

1. MSG SPHERE OPERATIONAL MODEL: How does MSG Sphere operate, and what does it reveal about immersive venue management?
   - Technical specifications and operational reality — 16K×16K interior LED display (580,000 sq ft of programmable surface), 167,000 permanently installed speakers with beamforming audio, haptic seating system (10,000 seats), Infrasound sub-bass system, exterior Exosphere LED (580,000 sq ft)
   - Content pipeline and production workflow — how is content created for a 16K×16K resolution spherical display? Content production tools, rendering requirements, content library management, live event content integration, turnaround time between shows
   - Operational staffing model — how staffing differs from a traditional 18,500-seat arena, content engineering roles, immersive technology maintenance staff, enhanced AV crew requirements
   - Cost structure — capital cost ($2.3B reported), operating cost implications, per-show content production costs, energy consumption for full LED operation, maintenance lifecycle costs for LED panels and speaker arrays
   - Audience capacity and throughput — seating configurations, standing configurations, show scheduling cadence, turnover logistics between shows
   - Where traditional venue management applies and where it breaks — which operational domains (F&B, security, ticketing, crowd management) work the same as a conventional arena and which require fundamentally different approaches?

2. ESPORTS ARENAS AND COMPETITION VENUES: What are the operational requirements of dedicated eSports facilities?
   - Dedicated eSports facilities — Esports Stadium Arlington (original and current status), Riot Games Arena (Los Angeles), Blizzard Arena, Fusion Arena (Philadelphia, planned/current status), purpose-built vs. adapted venue differences
   - Broadcast infrastructure requirements — production control rooms, observer stations, camera positions, streaming infrastructure, internet bandwidth requirements (dedicated fiber), latency management, how eSports broadcast differs from traditional sports broadcast
   - Player and team services — team rooms/practice areas, player warm-up facilities, anti-cheat isolation requirements, player hospitality, coach/analyst viewing positions
   - Audience demographics and experience design — younger audience profile (18-34 median), in-venue viewing experience (large screens, personal monitors, AR overlays), how physical venue experience competes with streaming (why come in person?)
   - Hybrid physical/streaming audience management — in-venue capacity (typically 2K-10K) vs. streaming audience (potentially millions), integrated production for both audiences, interaction between venue audience and stream audience (crowd reactions, participation mechanics)
   - Revenue model differences — how eSports venue revenue differs from traditional sports venues (lower ticket prices, different F&B patterns, merchandise, sponsorship/naming rights from endemic gaming brands)

3. EXPERIENTIAL ENTERTAINMENT VENUES: How do continuous-operation experiential venues differ from event-based venues?
   - Meow Wolf model — permanent immersive art installations (Santa Fe, Las Vegas, Denver, Houston), continuous daily operation (not event-scheduled), throughput management (timed entry, capacity caps), how operational rhythms differ from event-based venues
   - teamLab model — immersive digital art installations (Borderless, Planets), technology maintenance for always-on interactive exhibits, visitor flow through unstructured spaces, accessibility in immersive environments
   - Illuminarium model — immersive projection experiences, show scheduling in continuous-operation format, content rotation and lifecycle, multi-location operation
   - Operational distinctions — continuous operation vs. event-based scheduling, throughput management vs. crowd management, exhibit/installation maintenance vs. event changeover, visitor experience design vs. event production
   - Different F&B and retail models — themed retail/merchandise as core revenue (not ancillary), F&B as experience element (not concession), gift shop as exit pathway design, per-cap revenue expectations
   - Staffing model — host/guide roles instead of usher/security, technology maintenance as primary operations function, content/creative roles embedded in operations team

4. HERITAGE AND HISTORIC VENUES: What operational constraints and requirements apply to historic venue management?
   - Secretary of the Interior's Standards for the Treatment of Historic Properties — the 4 treatment approaches (preservation, rehabilitation, restoration, reconstruction), how each applies to venue operations, what work requires review/approval
   - ADA compliance in historic structures — "reasonable modification" standard vs. "readily achievable barrier removal," when ADA requirements can be modified for historic preservation, case law and advisory opinions, practical compromise approaches (alternative accessible routes, program relocation)
   - Preservation constraints on technology integration — running cables in historic structures, mounting displays/speakers without damaging historic fabric, wireless alternatives, temporary vs. permanent technology installations, historic preservation review processes for technology upgrades
   - Adaptive reuse as venue type — industrial buildings, churches, rail stations, and other historic structures converted to event venues, unique operational challenges (non-standard floor plans, limited load-bearing capacity, historic HVAC limitations), building code compliance for change of occupancy
   - National Register and local landmark designation — operational implications of listing, review requirements for exterior and interior modifications, tax incentives (Federal Historic Tax Credit, state credits), how designation affects insurance and liability
   - Canadian heritage venue framework — Parks Canada Standards and Guidelines for the Conservation of Historic Places, Heritage Conservation Districts, provincial heritage legislation differences

5. TEMPORARY AND POP-UP VENUES: What operational frameworks govern temporary event infrastructure?
   - Festival infrastructure design — site planning for temporary venues, infrastructure layering (base, services, structures, production), build schedule management (typical 5-14 day build for major festival), logistics sequencing
   - NFPA 102 for temporary structures — specific provisions for tents, membrane structures, grandstands, aisle widths, exit requirements, flame resistance standards (NFPA 701 test method), anchoring requirements
   - Temporary occupancy permits — application processes, inspection requirements, jurisdictional variation, when temporary vs. permanent occupancy standards apply, time limits on temporary permits
   - Rapid build/strike logistics — labor planning for construction and teardown, heavy equipment coordination, weather contingency planning, site restoration requirements, environmental protection during temporary use
   - Generator power and temporary utilities — electrical distribution planning for temporary venues, generator sizing and redundancy, temporary water/sewer services, communications infrastructure (temporary cell towers, Wi-Fi), fire suppression for temporary structures
   - Environmental and site impact — ground protection systems, noise management for temporary outdoor venues, waste management planning (Leave No Trace vs. traditional waste hauling), stormwater management during temporary use, site restoration standards

6. DOME AND RETRACTABLE ROOF OPERATIONS: What operational complexity do convertible venues create?
   - Retractable roof operational complexity — Rogers Centre (Toronto), Mercedes-Benz Stadium (Atlanta), SoFi Stadium (Inglewood), Allegiant Stadium (Las Vegas), Globe Life Field (Arlington) — roof mechanisms, operation time, maintenance requirements, operational lifecycle
   - Roof operation protocols — decision-making authority and criteria (weather thresholds, event type, broadcast requirements), notification timelines (teams, broadcasters, ticket holders), mid-event operation policies, safety protocols during roof movement
   - Weather decision-making frameworks — temperature, wind, precipitation, and air quality thresholds for roof operation, forecast lead time requirements, split-decision scenarios (open for pre-game, close for game), authority chain for weather calls
   - Maintenance of movable structures — track/rail systems, motor systems, membrane/panel maintenance, inspection schedules, lifecycle replacement costs, how weather exposure accelerates wear on movable components
   - HVAC implications of convertible venues — air conditioning with open roof (feasibility, energy cost), ventilation mode switching, condensation management during roof transitions, comfort standards for hybrid indoor/outdoor operation
   - Insurance and liability for movable structures — additional coverage requirements, inspection certification requirements, liability for roof operation failures, wind damage provisions

7. HYBRID AND MULTI-FORMAT VENUES: How do venues designed for maximum flexibility actually operate?
   - Rapid format conversion venues — design principles for venues serving 3+ event formats (concert, sports, convention, banquet, ceremony), conversion timelines by format change type, labor requirements, equipment storage
   - Flat floor to seated conversion — retractable seating systems (Hussey Seating, Irwin Seating), telescopic bleacher operations, portable seating logistics, floor protection during conversion, changeover staffing models
   - Indoor-outdoor hybrid venues — venues with operable walls, retractable glass, courtyard integration, weather management for partially exposed spaces, building code implications of convertible indoor/outdoor classification
   - The operational playbook for maximum-flexibility venues — how multi-format venues document conversion procedures, who owns the changeover process, quality control for different configurations, rehearsal/walkthrough requirements for non-standard setups
   - Economic model for utilization — how format flexibility affects booking rate, utilization optimization, pricing by configuration, the operational cost of flexibility (storage, labor, wear on convertible systems) vs. additional revenue from broader event type portfolio

KNOWN CONTEXT: The KB has av-and-production (13 concepts) covering production infrastructure for traditional venues — rigging, lighting, audio, broadcast, stage management. event-operations (12 concepts) covers traditional event execution — event-day operations, changeover, advance planning, settlement. facilities-and-building-systems (15 concepts) covers building operations for conventional structures — HVAC, electrical, plumbing, BMS, maintenance. guest-experience covers traditional guest service delivery — wayfinding, accessibility, service design. This prompt targets operational requirements that are UNIQUE to emerging venue types — not general venue management that happens to occur in a Sphere. The test for inclusion is: does this operational requirement exist BECAUSE of the venue type, or would it exist in any venue? If the latter, it belongs in existing domain coverage, not here. R-20 (Innovation and Future of Venue Experience) covers immersive technology and fan engagement as strategic/experiential concepts across all venue types; this prompt covers the operational realities of managing specific non-traditional venue formats.
